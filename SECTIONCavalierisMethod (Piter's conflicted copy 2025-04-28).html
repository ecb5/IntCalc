    Section 4.1 Historical Precursors to Definite Integration

    Newton’s Second Law: \(F=ma = m\dfdx{v}{t}\)
    Possibly as a result of the rediscovery of many classical Greek works after the fall of Constantinople
     1 
    https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantinople
    in 1453, mathematicians of the Italian Renaissance
     2 
    https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_Renaissance
    began investigating the use of what they called “indivisibles” as a tool for mathematical investigations. Today the best known such mathematician was, of course, Galileo
     3 
    https://mathshistory.st-andrews.ac.uk/Galileo
    whom we have seen before.
    But the investigations of two others will be of interest to us. Both Bonaventura Cavalieri, and Evangelista Torricelli were deeply influenced by the work and ideas of Galileo.
    Torricelli lived with and assisted Galileo during the final months of Galileo’s life. Cavalieri only met Galileo once but they exchanged a long correspondence over the years. We will look closely at Cavalieri’s ideas here, and return to Torricelli’s in section [provisional cross-reference: Torricelli].

    Historical Context.

    The concept of an indivisible was vehemently opposed by the Catholic Church on religious grounds that are quite fascinating. Unfortunately, since our topic is mathematics, not religion discussing them here would take us too far afield.
    They are discussed in detail in the book, Infinitesimal, by Amir Alexander, (2014).

    Subsection 4.1.1 Bonaventura Cavalieri and Indivisibles

    Bonaventura Cavalieri
     5 
    https://mathshistory.st-andrews.ac.uk/Biographies/Cavalieri/
    was a Jesuat
     6 
    https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesuati
    monk who studied mathematics at the University of Pisa under the tutelage of Benedetto Antonio Castelli
     7 
    https://mathshistory.st-andrews.ac.uk/Biographies/Castelli/
    . It was Castelli who introduced Cavalieri to Galileo’s methods. Cavalieri’s ideas were deep, fundamental, and direct precursors to the notion of a differential so it is worthwhile to learn a bit about them before we go on.
    In simple terms Cavalieri said that we can compute the area of a planar region by summing up “all of the lines” that make up the region. Similarly, we can compute the volume of a solid by summing up “all of the planes” that make it up. An example will be helpful.

    Example 4.1.1.2. Cavalieri’s Principle.

    Cavalieri began with the simple observation, seen in Figure 4.1.1.3, that since a rectangle is composed of lines, the area of the rectangle is equal to the sum of “all the lines” that make it up.
    He was careful not to say that the area of the rectangle is equal to the sum of the areas of the lines. He knew, as we do, that this would be problematic since line segments do not have an area, and this would have led him into logical contradictions. Instead, like Newton and Leibniz later, he simply acknowledged the contradictions and then ignored them, proceeding as if line segments do have area.
    If we accept this premise then clearly any other shape we can create using the same lines will have the same area. For example the sketch below shows the parallelogram constructed from the same lines that make up the rectangle in Figure 4.1.1.3. Clearly both have the same area.
    Nor is it necessary for the shape to be “regular” in any sense. The following shapes will also have the same area as long as “all the lines” are the same.
    The same idea can be used to compare volumes. For example the image below shows two pictures of the same deck of cards. Obviously the volumes of the stacks are the same since they are made of the same of cards.
    Generalizing the ideas in Example 4.1.1.2 slightly gives us Cavalieri’s Principle.

    Principle 4.1.1.5. Cavalieri’s Principle.

    If the cross–sectional areas of the corresponding slices of two solid figures are all in the same proportion, say \(P\text{,}\) then the proportion of the volumes of the two figures will also be \(P\) provided both figures have the same height.
    For the deck of cards shown above the proportion, \(P\text{,}\) is equal to one. If the cards had been cut in half before making the second stack then \(P=1/2\) and the second stack would have half the volume of the first.
    Cavalieri would eventually publish his results in a book titled Geometria Indivisibilibus
     8 
    https://old.maa.org/press/periodicals/convergence/mathematical-treasures-cavalieris-geometry-of-indivisibles
    . Of this Howard Eves
     9 
    In Great Moments in Mathematics Before 1650
    said,
    Cavalieri’s treatise on the method of indivisibles is voluble and not clearly written, and it is not easy to learn from it precisely what Cavalieri meant by an ‘indivisible’.
    It is likely that this is because Cavalieri was himself unsure what an “indivisible” should be. Nevertheless the fundamental notion of an indivisible was picked up by Leibniz who transformed it into his Calculus Differentialis.

    Drill 4.1.1.6.

    Cavalieri’s Principle 4.1.1.5 is normally stated in terms of volumes of solids, as we have here, but it is equally applicable to the areas of planar regions.
    State Cavalieri’s Principle for the area of planar regions.

    Example 4.1.1.7. Circles and Ellipses.

    To see how Cavalieri used his principle we will find the area enclosed by an ellipse by comparing it to the known area of a related circle.
    We begin with the graph of
    \begin{equation} \frac{x^2}{a^2}+\frac{y^2}{b^2}=1\tag{4.1} \end{equation}
    which is an ellipse. We then form the circle
    \begin{equation} x^2+y^2=a^2\tag{4.2} \end{equation}
    as shown below.
    Solving equation (4.1) for \(y\) gives
    \begin{equation*} y=\frac{b}{a} \sqrt{a^2-x^2} \text{,} \end{equation*}
    and solving equation (4.2) for \(y\) gives
    \begin{equation*} y=\sqrt{a^2-x^2} \text{.} \end{equation*}
    Thus the proportion of a typical cross–section of both figures (see the red, vertical line in the figure) is
    \begin{equation*} P= \frac{\frac{b}{a} \sqrt{a^2-x^2}} {\sqrt{a^2-x^2}}=\frac{b}{a}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Therefore by Principle 4.1.1.5 the ratio of the areas enclosed by the ellipse and the circle is also \(P\text{.}\) That is
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\text{Area of Ellipse}}{\text{Area of Circle}}=P=\frac{b}{a} \text{.} \end{equation*}
    Thus, since the area of the circle is \(\pi a^2\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \text{Area of Ellipse} = \frac{b}{a}(\text{Area of Circle}) = \frac{b}{a}(\pi a^2) =\pi ba \text{.} \end{equation*}
    Notice that when \(a=b\) the ellipse becomes a circle with radius equal to \(a\) and our area formula recovers the area of the circle.

    Subsection 4.1.2 Cavalieri, Differentials, and Definite Integration

    In view of our discussion in Section 3.1 it should be clear that the “indivisibles” Cavalieri’s is “adding up” are the same things as Leibniz’ differentials so we will adjust our point of view accordingly. In Figure 4.1.1.3 for example, instead of thinking of the “lines” as line segments with zero width we think of them as having a differential width \(\dx{x}\neq 0\text{.}\)
    In that case a rectangle consists of “all the lines” with differential area:
    \begin{equation*} (\text{length})\times\dx{x}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Using the summation notation we saw in Section 3.1 the area of a rectangle is thus given by the integral
    \begin{equation} \int(\text{length})\dx{x}\text{.}\tag{4.3} \end{equation}
    Notice that since the quantity, \((\text{length}) \times \dx{x}\) is a differential (as we observed in [cross-reference to target(s) "DIGRESSIONDifferentialNotation" missing or not unique]) formula (4.3) has exactly the same form as an integral, as defined in Section 3.1, it appears that we can compute the area of this rectangle , if only we can find a way to compute the integral \(\int \text{(length)}\dx{x}\text{.}\)

    Comment.

    Yes we know this area can be found by simpler means. We don’t care about that. We want to find a way to express this simple area using the integral notation so that we can write down expressions for more complicated areas and, hopefully, find ways to compute them. Stick with us. We’re just getting started.
    But the notation we’ve used here isn’t quite as useful, or expressive as we’d like so we will pause for a moment to refine our notation. Suppose we want to compute the area of the rectangle in the sketch below using an integral.
    In our sketch the red line represents one of the Cavalieri lines to be summed. Observe that if \(A\) represents the area of the rectangle in black then \(\dx{A}=2\dx{x}\text{.}\) That is, the quantity \(2\dx{x}\) is the differential of the area \(A\text{.}\) So to compute \(A\) we need to find a way to compute the sum
    \begin{equation} A=\int\dx{A}=\int 2\dx{x}\tag{4.4} \end{equation}
    starting at \(x=2\) and ending at \(x=6\text{.}\)
    There are two problems with the notation we used in equation (4.4).
    First Problem:
    Equation (4.4) does not reflect the fact that we begin summing our differentials at \(x=2\) and end at \(x=6\text{.}\) If we stick with this notation then another rectangle with other beginning and ending points would be written in exactly the same way. We will find it useful to reduce this ambiguity by specifying the interval \((2,6)\) within our definite integral notation.
    Second Problem:
    The notation in equation (4.4) is exactly the same notation we used in Subsection 3.1.2 to designate the most general antiderivative (multifunction).
    The purpose of notation is to clarify not to befuddle. A notation that can be read as either a number (the area of a rectangle in this case) or as an antiderivative, depending on the context of the discussion is not an ideal notation.

    Comment.

    To be sure, just as there are words in English with more than one meaning, there are also symbols in mathematics with more than one meaning. This flexibility and redundancy is what gives English its beauty and expressive power. But in mathematics it just leads to unnecessary confusion, and is best avoided if possible.
    We’d like to have a notation that reflects the fact that the antiderivative and the area computation are related (both are built from the summation of differentials), but at the same time clearly distinguishes between them. It will take a while to build up all of the necessary ideas so be patient.
    We can address the second problem by simply reserving the notation \(\int f(x)\dx{x}\) for the most general antiderivative as we originally used it. We make the following definition.

    Definition 4.1.2.1. The Most General Antiderivative.

    Given the function \(f(x)\) the formula
    \begin{equation*} \int f(x)\dx{x} \end{equation*}
    always represents the multifunction whose derivative is \(f(x)\) — the most general antiderivative.
    In particular, if \(F(x)\) is any antiderivative of \(f(x)\) Then
    \begin{equation} \int f(x)\dx{x} = F(x) +C\text{,}\tag{4.5} \end{equation}
    where, as before, \(C\) represents all possible constants.
    Definition 4.1.2.1 formalizes the work we did in Chapter 2, and establishes the notation we used there as the standard notation for an antidifferentiaton. Our next definition is new. It will establish the standard notation we will use to distinguish the antiderivative problems in Chapter 2 and the area problem in equation (4.4), as well as other kinds of problems that can be addressed using the same ideas. Since the underlying ideas are related the notations we use will also be related. You’ll need to read the notation carefully to be sure you understand which kind of problem you are looking at.
    We learned in [cross-reference to target(s) "SECTIONGalileoDrops" missing or not unique] that the velocity of an object moving in a straight line is the derivative of its position. Recasting the previous sentence in the language of integrals we it says that the position of the object is the integral of its velocity. But when we view the the problem from this direction things are complicated by the presence of the “\(+C\)” from equation (4.5).
    To understand how to deal with this difficulty suppose that we are traveling in a straight line (say, from left to right along the \(x\)—axis) with velocity \(v(t) \frac{\text{miles}}{\text{hour} }\) and we’d like to find out how far we travel between the times \(t=t_1\) and \(t=t_2\) as shown in the following sketch.
    If we suppose that our positions at \(t_1\) and \(t_2\) are \(p_1\) and \(p_2\) respectively , then the distance, \(D\text{,}\) that we travel between those times is clearly
    \begin{equation*} D=p_2-p_1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    So far, so good. Do you see how we find \(p_1\) and \(p_2\text{?}\) Recall that these are our positions, and we have know the position function. It is \(\int v(t)\dx{t}\text{,}\) an antiderivative of velocity. So to find \(p_1\) and \(p_2\) all we have to do is evaluate
    \begin{equation*} \eval{\int v(t)\dx{t}}{t}{t_1}\text {and} \eval{\int v(t)\dx{t}}{t}{t_2} \text{.} \end{equation*}
    Well, sort of.
    Remember that \(\int v(t)\dx{t}\) is a multifunction. That is, it is a lot of related functions gathered together in a single package. We need to find the one function in the package that reflects the problem at hand before we can evaluate it. So we take \(P(t)\) to be the antiderivative of \(v(t)\) with the following values: \(P(t_1)=p_1\) and \(P(t_2)=p_2 \text{.}\) Clearly then
    \begin{equation*} D=p_2-p_1=P(t_2)-P(t_1)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    There is something interesting here that may not be quite visible yet. To see it suppose that \(\hat{P}(t)\) is some other antiderivative of \(v(t)\text{.}\) From our work in Chapter 3 that \(\hat{P}(t)=P(t)+C\) where in this case \(C\) is some fixed constant. Clearly then
    \begin{align*} \hat{P}(t_2)-\hat{P}(t_1)\amp{}=\left( P(t_2)+C\right)-\left( P(t_1)+C\right)\\ \amp{} = P(t_2)-P(t_1)+\underbrace{C-C}_{=0}\\ \amp{} = p_2-p_1\\ \amp{}=D. \end{align*}
    In other words it doesn’t matter which specific antiderivative we choose from the multifunction \(\int v(t)\dx{t}\text{.}\) Because we are subtracting its value at two different values of \(t\) the constant \(C\) that is common to both evaluations will simply subtract away. If this is hard to see right now don’t worry. Our discussion has been fairly general and abstract which can often obscure the point. Once you have done Drill 4.1.2.3 below you will see that it is actually a simple idea.
    Summarizing our discussion so far we see that
    \begin{equation} D= \eval{\int v(t)\dx{t}}{t}{t_2} - \eval{\int v(t)\dx{t}}{t}{t_1}\tag{4.6} \end{equation}
    Strictly speaking equation (4.6) is a slight abuse of our notation since we really mean that we are choosing one antiderivative from the most general antiderivative and evaluating it. Because the constant will subtract out it doesn’t matter which one we choose.
    The discussion above has been quite dense. It is laden with both new ideas and new notation. Let’s use equation (4.6) to solve a very simple problem — one where we already know the answer — and verify that it all makes sense.

    Example 4.1.2.2.

    Suppose we are traveling on a straight road at \(60 \frac{miles}{hour}\text{.}\) How far do we travel between the third hour and the eighth hour?
    Of course you see that if we travel for five hours at \(60 \frac{\text{miles}}{\text{hour}}\) then we travel \(5\times 60 = 300\) miles.
    In this problem our velocity is \(v(t)=60\text{,}\) \(t_1=3\text{,}\) and \(t_2=8\text{.}\) So the distance traveled will be
    \begin{equation*} D= \eval{\int v(t)\dx{t}}{t}{5} - \eval{\int v(t)\dx{t}}{t}{3}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    One antiderivative of \(v(t)=60\) is \(P(t)=60t\text{,}\) so
    \begin{equation*} D= \eval{60t}{t}{8} - \eval{60t}{t}{3} = 60\cdot8-60\cdot3=300\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Drill 4.1.2.3.

    Find two different antiderivatives of \(v(t)=60\) in Example 4.1.2.2 and show that
    \begin{equation*} \eval{\int v(t)\dx{t}}{t}{5} - \eval{\int v(t)\dx{t}}{t}{3}=300 \end{equation*}
    using your antiderivatives.
    While expressive, the notation
    \begin{equation*} \eval{\int f(x)\dx{x}}{x}{a}-\eval{\int f(x)\dx{x}}{x}{b} \end{equation*}
    is too redundant. The only difference between the first and second term is that \(x=a\) changes to \(x=b\text{.}\) So, because mathematicians won’t write two symbols when one will do, we extend the evaluation notation introduced in [cross-reference to target(s) "EvaluationNotation" missing or not unique] as follows:
    \begin{equation*} \eval{\int f(x)\dx{x}}{x}{a}^b = \eval{\int f(x)\dx{x}}{x}{a}-\eval{\int f(x)\dx{x}}{x}{b} \text{.} \end{equation*}
    Be sure you understand this notation. There is a lot here. The formula \(\int f(x)\dx{x}\) represents the most general antiderivative of \(f(x)\text{.}\) Think of it a all of the antiderivatives gathered together in a single package. We select one of the antiderivatives for evaluation when we evaluate it at \(x=a\) and \(x=b\text{.}\) It doesn’t matter which one we select because they are all the same except for the arbitrary constant \(C\text{,}\) and it will be subtracted off when we compute the difference. This suggests the following definition.

    Definition 4.1.2.4. Definite Integral.

    Given a function \(f(x)\text{,}\) the formula
    \begin{equation} \int_{x=a}^{b} f(x)\dx{x}\tag{4.7} \end{equation}
    represents the sum of all of the differentials of the form \(f(x)\dx{x} \) beginning at \(x=a\) and ending at \(x=b\text{.}\) Such an expression is called a definite integral.
    The following theorem summarizes our discussion above. It states how a definite integral can be evaluated.

    Theorem 4.1.2.5.

    The definite integral can be evaluated by computing
    \begin{align} \int_{x=a}^{b} f(x)\dx{x}\amp{}= \eval{\int f(x)\dx{x}}{x}{a}^b\notag\\ \amp{} = \eval{\int f(x)\dx{x}}{x}{a}-\eval{\int f(x)\dx{x}}{x}{b}.\tag{4.8} \end{align}
    Often we will have a formula, say \(F(x)\text{,}\) for one of the antiderivatives of \(f(x)\) in the most general antiderivative \(\int f(x)\dx{x}\text{.}\) In that case equation (4.8) reduces to
    \begin{equation} \int_{x=a}^b f(x)\dx{x} = F(b)-F(a)\tag{4.9} \end{equation}
    The subscripts and superscripts on the integral symbol in equation (4.7) are called the upper and lower indices. Take careful note that we evaluate the antiderivative at the lower index and subtract from it the result of evaluating at the upper index. What happens if you do it backwards?
