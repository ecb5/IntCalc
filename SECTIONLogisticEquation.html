    Section 2.3 Heaviside’s Cover–up Method (HCUM)

    As you have just seen, integrating the left side of equation (2.32) is entirely within the range of our current skill set but it is rather daunting. And we’re not even finished yet. We must still solve equation (2.34) for \(P(t)\text{.}\) Before we do that we’d really like to find a simpler way to compute the integral.
    First notice that we could rewrite Equation 19.32 as
    \begin{equation} \frac{\dx{P}}{P\left(P-100\right)}=-0.0001\ \dx{t}\ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ (19.35)\tag{2.35} \end{equation}
    In point of fact, we could have done it before, but it would have led to a different trigonometric substitution. Again, integrating the right–hand side poses no problem as we would get
    \begin{equation} \int{\frac{\dx{P}}{P\left(P-100\right)}}=-0.0001\ t+C\tag{2.36} \end{equation}
    To compute the left-hand side, notice that
    \begin{equation} \frac{1}{P\left(P-100\right)}=\frac{-\frac{1}{100}}{P}+\frac{\frac{1}{100}}{P-100}\tag{2.37} \end{equation}

    Problem 2.3.0.1.

    (a)

    Confirm equation (2.37).

    (b)

    Use equation (2.37) to show that
    \begin{equation*} \int{\frac{\dx{P}}{P\left(P-100\right)}}=\frac{1}{100}{\mathrm{ln} \left(\frac{P-100}{P}\right)\ }+C_2 \end{equation*}

    (c)

    Use equation (2.36) and the result in part (b) to show that
    \begin{equation*} P\left(t\right)=\frac{100}{1+Ke^{-0.01t}} \end{equation*}
    where \(K\) is an arbitrary constant.

    (d)

    Solve equation (2.34) for \(P\) and show you obtain the same result.

    (e)

    The original IVP (2.31) had the initial condition \(P\left(0\right)=10\text{.}\) Use this to show that \(K=9\) and plot this graph to confirm that it has the same general shape that we discovered in Subsection 11.1.1
    This is certainly easier to deal with than a trigonometric substitution, but it begs the question, “Where did equation (2.37) \(\frac{1}{P\left(P-100\right)}=-\frac{\frac{1}{100}}{P}+\frac{\frac{1}{100}}{P-100}\) come from?” It is one thing to verify such an identity, but it is quite another to actually formulate one.
    We found equation (2.37) by an algebraic technique called the Partial Fraction Decomposition or PFD. There are several places in mathematics where it is useful to break a single rational expresstion into the sum of simpler rational expressions. We will be using it to simplify the integration when the integrand is a rational function.
    A fully general statement of the PFD is given in Theorem 2.5.0.2 but as you can see the general statement is quite comprehensive. Moreover the statement of the theorem doesn’t help us use it as a practical tool. So we will work through a number of increasingly complex examples to build things up slowly.
    To begin, if we have an expression of the form \(\frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}\) where \(a\text{,}\) and \(b\) are known constants, and \(a\neq b\text{,}\) then the PFD says that there are constants \(A\) and \(B\) such that as
    \begin{equation} \frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}=\frac{A}{x-a}+\frac{B}{x-b}\text{.}\tag{2.38} \end{equation}
    Our task is to find values for \(A\) and \(B\text{.}\)
    Take note of the following fact: The right side of equation (2.38) consists of rational functions where the degree of the numerator is one less than the degree of the denominator. Since the numerators are all constants (polynomials of degree zero) and the denominators are all linear (polynomials of degree one) this is, at the moment, a rather bombastic way to state this fact. However in [cross-reference to target(s) "SUBSECTIONPFDNonSimple" missing or not unique] we will find it very useful.
    There are a couple of ways we can find \(A\) and \(B\text{.}\) What we’d like to do is find the simplest, least difficult to implement method we can. After all, we’re not here to do fraction decompositions. This is just one step toward computing an integral and we’d like for the decomposition step to be as simple and painless as possible.
    The most direct way to find \(A\) and \(B\) is to add the fractions on the right-hand side of equation \eqref{GrindEQ__19_37_} and compare the results.
    \begin{equation*} \frac{0x+1}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)}=\frac{A\left(x-b\right)+B\left(x-a\right)}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)}=\frac{\left(A+B\right)x+\left(-Ab-Ba\right)}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)} \end{equation*}
    Comparing coefficients leads to the system of two equations in the two unknowns \(A,\ B\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} A+B\amp{}=0\\ -Ab-Ba\amp{}=1 \end{align*}

    Problem 2.3.0.2.

    Solve these two equations for \(A\) and \(B\) to show that \(A=\frac{1}{a-b}\) and \(B=\frac{1}{b-a}\text{.}\)
    This was very straightforward and not too tedious, but what if we tried to do the same with three distinct factors
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)\left(x-c\right)}=\frac{A}{x-a}+\frac{B}{x-b}+\frac{C}{x-c} \end{equation*}
    The fact that this can be done will follow from Theorem 2.5.0.2, but for now let’s assume that a PFD works for three or more linear factors.
    Combining this together would lead to
    \begin{align*} \amp{}\frac{0x^2+0x+1}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)\left(x-c\right)}\\ \amp{}=\frac{A\left(x-b\right)\left(x-c\right)+B\left(x-a\right)\left(x-c\right)+C\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)\left(x-c\right)}\\ \amp{}=\frac{\left(A+B+C\right)x^2-\left(A\left(b+c\right)+B\left(a+c\right)+C\left(a+b\right)\right)x+\left(Abc+Bac+Cab\right)}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)\left(x-c\right)} \end{align*}
    Equating coefficients leads to the following system of three linear equations in the three unknowns \(A,B,C\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} A+B+C=0 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} A\left(b+c\right)+B\left(a+c\right)+C\left(a+b\right)=0 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} Abc+Bac+Cab=1 \end{equation*}
    This is certainly doable, but very tedious. Could you imagine if we had four factors?!
    Using the mantra ``Work smarter, not harder!’’, let’s go back to the case of two distinct linear factors and see if we can avoid some of this algebra. Notice that when we had
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)}=\frac{A\left(x-b\right)+B\left(x-a\right)}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)} \end{equation*}
    It was the numerators that mattered at this point. Thus, we must have
    \begin{equation*} 1=A\left(x-b\right)+B\left(x-a\right) \end{equation*}
    In fact, this is what we did to create our two equations in two unknowns. However, instead of expanding as we did before, let’s notice that this equation must work for all values of \(x\text{.}\) Armed with this simple observation, if we let \(x=a\) then we would get \(1=A\left(a-b\right)+B(a-a)\) or \(A=\frac{1}{a-b}\text{.}\)
    \textbf{Problem. }What value of \(x\) should we substitute into \(1=A\left(x-b\right)+B\left(x-a\right)\) to determine \(B\) with as little work as possible. In fact, if you choose wisely, you can determine the value of \(B\) in your head. Compare the values for \(A,\ B\) to what you had before.

    Problem 2.3.0.5.

    As we’ve mentioned before, all mathematical models make simplifying assumptions. It is important to know what are the simplifications are so we know the conditions under which the model is unrealistic. For example, [cross-reference to target(s) "EQUATIONFishery1" missing or not unique] works fine in the short term but in the long term it predicts explosive growth which is not sustainable. We addressed this when we generated the [cross-reference to target(s) "EQUATIONLogEq" missing or not unique] by including as a factor, \(\left( 1-\frac{P}{100}\right)\text{.}\) That modification put an upper bound on the population size as we have seen.
    However the Logistic Equation doesn’t put any lower bound on the population size. This is also unrealistic since we know that if a population of critters is too small they tend to die out. The following tweak to the logistic model:
    \begin{equation} \dfdx{P}{t}=0.01P\left(1-\frac{P}{100}\right)\left(1-\frac{10}{P}\right),\tag{2.39} \end{equation}
    forces a population which is too small to die out. In this case, \(10\) is called the minimum viability level of the population.

    (a)

    Find the PFD of
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{(P-100)(P-10)} \end{equation*}
    and verify that your decomposition is correct.

    (b)

    Use the result of part (a) to show that
    \begin{equation*} \dfdx{P}{t} = 0.01P\left(1-\frac{P}{100}\right)\left( 1-\frac{10}{P}\right) = -0.0001(P-100) \end{equation*}
    is satisfied by
    \begin{equation*} P(t)= \frac{100-10Ke^{0.009t}}{1-Ke^{-0.009t}} \end{equation*}
    where \(K\) is an arbitrary constant.

    (c)

    Use the graphical techniques of [cross-reference to target(s) "SECTIONExponentialGrowth" missing or not unique] to sketch a qualitative graph of the solution of equation (2.39).
    Does the curve you get resemble the graph of \(P(t)\text{?}\)
    Explain what happens if our population drops below the minimum viability level
    When he was computing some PFDs related to his work with differential equations Oliver Heaviside
     7 
    https://mathshistory.st-andrews.ac.uk/Biographies/Heaviside/
    (1850—1925) made the following observation. Look closely at the numerators of the fractions the right side of
    \begin{equation} \frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}= \frac{\frac{1}{a-b}}{x-a}+\frac{\frac{1}{b-a}}{x-b}\tag{2.40} \end{equation}
    (this is [cross-reference to target(s) "EQUATIONPFD3" missing or not unique]). Do you see that the numerator above \(x-a\) is what we would get if we simply replaced \(x\) with \(a\) on the left and “covered up,” (or ignored) the factor \(x-a\text{?}\)
    That is, if \(\frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}= \frac{A}{x-a}+\frac{B}{x-b}\) then
    \begin{equation*} A=\eval{\frac{1}{(\text{covered})(x-b)}}{x}{a}=\frac{1}{a-b}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Similarly
    \begin{equation*} B=\eval{\frac{1}{(x-a)(\text{covered})}}{x}{b}=\frac{1}{b-a}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This is known as Heaviside’s Cover–up Method (HCUM) and it is the simplest way we know to compute the coefficients of the PFD of the rational functions we’ve seen so far.
    The HCUM is a very convenient shortcut. But it is necessary to understand why any shortcut works. Otherwise all we have is the shortcut. If we encounter a problem where the shortcut wont’t work but the underlying principle will, and all we know is the shortcut we can’t make any progress.
    To see why the HCUM works take another look at the decomposition
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}=\frac{A}{x-a}+\frac{B}{x-b}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    If we multiply both sides by \(x-a\text{,}\) we get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{x-b}=A+\frac{B(x-a)}{x-b} \end{equation*}
    which holds as long as \(x\ne b\text{.}\) Notice that \(\frac{1}{x-b} = \frac{1}{(\text{covered})(x-b)}\text{.}\) Next, if we take \(x=a\) we get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{(\text{covered})(a-b)} =\frac{1}{a-b}=A+\frac{B(a-a)}{a-b}=A\text{.} \end{equation*}
