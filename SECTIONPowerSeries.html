    Section 22.2 Representing the Natural Exponential Function by a Power Series

    As we said in the last section, one way to approximate integrals such as \(\int^{\frac{4}{3}}_{z=\frac{2}{3}}{e^{-{\frac{1}{2}z}^2}\dx{z}}\) is to replace the integrand with a function which is easier to integrate. The easiest functions to integrate are polynomials, but alas, an exponential function is not a polynomial. This is evident from our work in Section 12.4 where we showed that the exponential function \(e^x\) outgrows any polynomial. However, we did lay some groundwork for replacing the exponential function by an “infinite polynomial.” A lot of ideas that we touched upon before will be brought together here.
    🔗

    Subsection 22.2.1 The Natural Exponential as a Power Series

    Recall that in Chapter 8, we introduced the natural exponential function \(y=e^x\) as the solution to the IVP
    \begin{align*} \dfdx{y}{x}=y,\amp{}\amp{} y\left(0\right)=1 \end{align*}
    🔗
    We also showed in Problem 8.3.0.3 that if we differentiated
    \begin{equation*} y=1+\frac{x}{1!}+\frac{x^2}{2!}+\frac{x^3}{3!}+\dots \end{equation*}
    term by term then it will satisfy the same IVP and produced the representation
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} e^x=1+\frac{x}{1!}+\frac{x^2}{2!}+\frac{x^3}{3!}+\dots \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The right-hand side of this equation is called a power series to emphasize that it is not actually a polynomial. It may seem strange to represent a function by a power series, but to mathematicians in the 18th century, this was quite natural and quite useful. For example, if we substitute \(x=1\) into the above expression, we get
    \begin{equation*} e=1+1+\frac{1}{2!}+\frac{1}{3!}+\dots \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Though we can’t actually add all of the terms on the right-hand side, we can add a finite number of them to approximate the value of \(e\) to any degree of accuracy we wish. The table below demonstrates this.
    🔗
    \(x_n\) \(1+\frac{1}{1!}+\frac{1}{2!}+\dots +\frac{1}{n!}\)
    \(1\) \(2\)
    \(2\) \(2.5\)
    \(3\) \(2.66667\)
    \(4\) \(2.70833\)
    \(5\) \(2.71667\)
    \(6\) \(2.71806\)
    \(7\) \(2.71825\)
    \(8\) \(2.71828\)
    \(9\) \(2.71828\)
    As you can see from the table, adding just the first ten terms of the series will get an approximation for \(e\) accurate to within five decimal places. In the days when calculations had to be completed by hand, such fast-converging representations were much more practical than the actual function representation. The invention of calculus made power series an important theoretical tool as well. Differentiating and integrating a power series term-by-term proved very useful for solving various differential and integral equations. For example, consider our problem of computing
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int^{\frac{4}{3}}_{z=\frac{2}{3}}{e^{-{\frac{1}{2}z}^2}\dx{z}} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    If we substitute \(x=-\frac{1}{2}z^2\) into our power series representation for \(e^x\text{,}\) we get
    🔗
    \begin{align*} \int^{\frac{4}{3}}_{z=\frac{2}{3}}{e^{-{\frac{1}{2}z}^2}\dx{z}}=\amp{}\int^{\frac{4}{3}}_{z=\frac{2}{3}}{\left(1-\frac{z^2}{1!\cdot 2}+\frac{z^4}{2!\cdot 2^2}-\frac{z^6}{3!\cdot 2^3}+\frac{z^8}{4!\cdot 2^4}-\dots \right)\dx{z}}\\ =\amp{}\int^{\frac{4}{3}}_{z=\frac{2}{3}}{1\dx{z}}-\frac{1}{1!\cdot 2}\int^{\frac{4}{3}}_{z=\frac{2}{3}}{z^2\dx{z}}+\frac{1}{2!\cdot 2^2}\int^{\frac{4}{3}}_{z=\frac{2}{3}}{z^4\dx{z}}\\ \amp{} -\frac{1}{3!\cdot 2^3}\int^{\frac{4}{3}}_{z=\frac{2}{3}}{z^6\dx{z}}+\frac{1}{4!\cdot 2^4}\int^{\frac{4}{3}}_{z=\frac{2}{3}}{z^8\dx{z}}-\dots \\ =\amp{}\left(\frac{4}{3}-\frac{2}{3}\right)-\frac{1}{1!\cdot 2\cdot 3}\left({\left(\frac{4}{3}\right)}^3-{\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)}^3\right)\\ \amp{} +\frac{1}{2!\cdot 2^2\cdot 5}\left({\left(\frac{4}{3}\right)}^5-{\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)}^5\right)-\frac{1}{3!\cdot 2^3\cdot 7}\left({\left(\frac{4}{3}\right)}^7-{\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)}^7\right)\\ \amp{} +\frac{1}{4!\cdot 2^4\cdot 9}\left({\left(\frac{4}{3}\right)}^9-{\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)}^9\right)-\dots \end{align*}
    🔗
    By summing a finite number of these terms together we can obtain an approximation to our integral which we can make as close as we wish, provided we add enough of them. For example, just adding these first 5 terms gives an approximation of \(0.404769\) whereas a computer algebra system gives that the integral is approximately \(0.404272\text{.}\) It is possible that a computer algebra system could be adding more terms of this series to obtain its answer.
    🔗
    Notice how we didn’t multiply things out so we could better see the pattern. For example, if we multiplied the numbers out, could you tell that the next term would be \(-\frac{1}{5!\cdot 2^5\cdot 11}\left({\left(\frac{4}{3}\right)}^{11}-{\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)}^{11}\right)\text{?}\) Remember this as you do the following problems.

    Subsection 22.2.2 Summation Notation

    Recall that Leibniz used a stylized Latin S to represent a sum of differentials in his notation \(\int{y \dx{x}}\text{.}\) In Section 20.1 we introduced the Greek letter sigma \(\Sigma\) to represent a sum of finite quantities. Later when introducing definite integrals, we made this more precise with the notation \(\int^b_{x=a}{y \dx{x}}\) to indicate summing differentials for all real numbers between \(x=a\) and \(x=b\text{.}\) With this in mind, we will use the following notation.
    🔗
    Let \(a\) and \(b\) be integers with \(a\le b\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} \sum^b_{n=a}{y(n)}=y\left(a\right)+y\left(a+1\right)+y\left(a+2\right)+\dots +y(b) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Since we will eventually be applying our notation to power series, it is customary to write \(y(n)\) as \(y_n\) and to write
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sum^b_{n=a}{y_n}=y_a+y_{a+1}+y_{a+2}+\dots +y_b \end{equation*}
    We can also write
    \begin{equation*} \sum^{\infty }_{n=a}{y_a}=y_a+y_{a+1}+y_{a+2}+\dots \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Subsection 22.2.3 Geometric Series

    Likely the first repeating decimal you encountered was \(\frac{1}{3}=0.3333\dots \) and you probably accepted this without question. But what if we multiply both sides by \(3\) to obtain

    \begin{equation*} 1=0.9999\dots \end{equation*}
    Are you as accepting of this result? Perhaps we need to be a little more careful. How about this?
    🔗
    Let
    \begin{equation*} s=0.3333\dots \end{equation*}
    then
    \begin{equation*} 10s=3.3333\dots \end{equation*}
    so
    \begin{equation*} 10s-s=3.3333\dots -0.3333\dots \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 9s=3 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 0.3333\dots =s=\frac{3}{9}=\frac{1}{3} \end{equation*}
    This agrees with what you were taught in school.
    🔗
    Let’s do this again with \(s=0.9999\dots \)
    \begin{equation*} 10s=9.9999\dots \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 10s-s=9.9999\dots -0.9999\dots \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 9s=9 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} 0.9999\dots =s=\frac{9}{9}=1 \end{equation*}
    Still skeptical? A big question might be how we started by flippantly saying, “Let \(s=0.9999\dots \) .” We should be a bit more careful about this. If we carefully look at our decimal representation \(0.9999\dots \) , we really have
    \begin{align*} 0.9999\dots \amp{}=\frac{9}{10}+\frac{9}{{10}^2}+\frac{9}{{10}^3}+\dots\\ \amp{} =\frac{9}{10}\left(1+\frac{1}{10}+{\left(\frac{1}{10}\right)}^2+{\left(\frac{1}{10}\right)}^3+\dots \right)\\ \amp{} =\frac{9}{10}\left(\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{{\left(\frac{1}{10}\right)}^n}\right). \end{align*}
    🔗
    It may sound a bit strange, but things will be easier to deal with if we denote \(\frac{1}{10}\) by \(x\) and look at the power series
    \begin{equation*} 1+x+x^2+x^3+\dots =\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{x^n} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Since an infinite sum is difficult to grasp, let’s first consider the finite sum
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 1+x+x^2+\dots +x^N=\sum^N_{n=0}{x^n} \end{equation*}

    🔗
    🔗
    Now be honest, would you have ever thought that the right-hand side would converge to \(\pi \text{?}\)
    🔗
    The above series for the arctangent was known to James Gregory (1638-1675) before the invention of calculus. Leibniz independently discovered the series by examining the area of a circle and communicated his ideas for determining areas via series to Newton through an intermediary, John Collins, who also was in contact with Gregory and communicated his ideas to Newton.
    🔗
    It is true that the value of \(\pi \) can be approximated to any degree of accuracy by summing a finite number of terms of the series
    \begin{equation*} 4\left(\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{\frac{{\left(-1\right)}^n}{2n+1}}\right)=\frac{4}{1}-\frac{4}{3}+\frac{4}{5}-\frac{4}{7}+\frac{4}{9}-\dots \end{equation*}
    but, unfortunately, this converges very slowly and it takes adding an enormous amount of terms to get any accuracy. For example, we have the approximation
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 4\left(\sum^{1000}_{n=0}{\frac{{\left(-1\right)}^n}{2n+1}}\right)=\frac{4}{1}-\frac{4}{3}+\frac{4}{5}-\frac{4}{7}+\frac{4}{9}-\dots +\frac{1}{2001}\approx 3.142591654 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    which is only accurate to two decimal places. While Newton praised the elegance of Leibniz in his methods, he set about to find a series approximation for \(\pi \) which would converge faster. We will use modern terminology to streamline Newton’s ideas. First notice that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\pi }{4}=\int^1_{x=0}{\sqrt{1-x^2}} \dx{x} \end{equation*}
    as this integral gives the area of one quarter of the unit circle. The trick now is to find a power series which represents \(\sqrt{1-x^2}\text{.}\) We will start with something you may or may not have seen in precalculus: the Binomial Theorem.
    🔗
    It is pretty straightforward to expand \({\left(1+x\right)}^2=1+2x+x^2\) but let’s do it carefully to recognize the pattern.
    \begin{equation*} {\left(1+x\right)}^2=\left(1+x\right)\left(1+x\right)=1\left(1+x\right)+x\left(1+x\right)=\left(1+x\right)+\left(x+x^2\right) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =1+\left(x+x\right)+x^2=1+2x+x^2 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Proceeding in a similar manner, we get
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} {\left(1+x\right)}^3=\left(1+x\right){\left(1+x\right)}^2=\left(1+x\right)\left(1+2x+x^2\right) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =1\left(1+2x+x^2\right)+x\left(1+2x+x^2\right)=\left(1+2x+x^2\right)+\left(x+2x^2+x^3\right) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =1+\left(2+1\right)x+\left(1+2\right)x^2+x^3=1+3x+3x^2+x^3 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} {\left(1+x\right)}^4=\left(1+x\right)\left(1+3x+3x^2+x^3\right)=1\left(1+3x+3x^2+x^3\right)+x\left(1+3x+3x^2+x^3\right) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\left(1+3x+3x^2+x^3\right)+\left(x+3x^2+3x^3+x^4\right)=1+\left(3+1\right)x+\left(3+3\right)x^2+\left(1+3\right)x^3+x^4 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =1+4x+6x^2+4x^3+{1x}^4 \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Subsection 22.2.7 The Taylor Series

    As you can see, we can represent a lot of functions by power series if we are “clever enough” to see how to manipulate existing series. But what if we are not clever enough on a particular day. Is there a systematic way to construct the power series for a given function? As mathematicians in the 18\({}^{th}\) century saw how power series could be a powerful tool for the, then new, calculus, they sought such a systematic way. A number of them found such a way. They were all basically the same, and finally a mathematician named Brook Taylor (1685-1731) wrote down this systematic method in a book in 1715. Taylor wrote his result utilizing Newton’s dot notation (somewhat) and it is not easy to read. Even using Leibniz’ differential notation becomes cumbersome when doing this. As such we will state Taylor’s result using the prime notation \(\left(y^{\prime}\left(x\right)=\dfdx{y}{x}\right)\) developed by Joseph Louis Lagrange (1736-1813) in 1797. In fact, Lagrange developed this notation specifically with power series in mind. Lagrange’s idea was to define functions by power series in an attempt to find a more rigorous foundation for calculus than infinitely small differentials. Here is Taylor’s idea with Lagrange’s notation.
    🔗
    Suppose we could write a function \(f(x)\) as a power series expanded about \(a\text{.}\) That is suppose
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f\left(x\right)=a_0+a_1\left(x-a\right)+a_2{\left(x-a\right)}^2+a_3{\left(x-a\right)}^3+\dots \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The goal is to find out what the coefficients \(a_0, a_1, a_2, a_3, \dots \)are in a systematic way. First notice that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f\left(a\right)=a_0+a_1\left(a-a\right)+a_2{\left(a-a\right)}^2+a_3{\left(a-a\right)}^3+\dots =a_0+0+0+0+\dots =a_0 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    so \(a_0=f(a)\text{.}\) Computing the derivative, we have
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f^{\prime}\left(x\right)=1a_1+2a_2\left(x-a\right)+3a_3{\left(x-a\right)}^2+4a_4{\left(x-a\right)}^3+\dots \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Substituting \(x=a\) again, we get
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f^{\prime}\left(a\right)=1a_1+2a_2\left(a-a\right)+3a_3{\left(a-a\right)}^2+4a_4{\left(a-a\right)}^3+\dots =1a_1+0+0+\dots =1a_1 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    so \(a_1=\frac{f^{\prime}\left(a\right)}{1}\text{.}\) Computing the next derivative and then substituting \(x=a\text{,}\) we have
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f^{\prime\prime}\left(x\right)=2\cdot 1a_2+3\cdot 2a^3\left(x-a\right)+4\cdot 3a_4{\left(x-a\right)}^2+5\cdot 4a_5{\left(x-a\right)}^3+\dots \end{equation*}
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f^{\prime\prime}\left(a\right)=2\cdot 1a_2+3\cdot 2a^3\left(a-a\right)+4\cdot 3a_4{\left(a-a\right)}^2+5\cdot 4a_5{\left(a-a\right)}^3+\dots =2\cdot 1a_2+0+0+0+\dots =2\cdot 1a_2 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    So \(a_2=\frac{f^{\prime\prime}\left(a\right)}{2\cdot 1}=\frac{f^{\prime\prime}\left(a\right)}{2!}\text{.}\)
    🔗

    🔗
    🔗
    You will notice that we were careful not to multiply things out and were very careful about putting in 1’s etc. This was not only because of laziness, but it was also to see the pattern developing. The fact that \(4!=24 \)is immaterial and in fact would hide the pattern, which goes against our goal of finding a systematic method. Following this pattern, we have that if \(f(x)\) is going to be represented as a power series expanded about \(a\text{,}\) then it must be
    \begin{equation*} f\left(x\right)=f(a)+\frac{f^{\prime}\left(a\right)}{1!}\left(x-a\right)+\frac{f^{\prime\prime}\left(a\right)}{2!}{\left(x-a\right)}^2+\frac{f^{\prime\prime\prime}\left(a\right)}{3!}{\left(x-a\right)}^3+\frac{f^{\prime\prime\prime\prime}\left(a\right)}{4!}{\left(x-a\right)}^4+\dots \end{equation*}
    🔗
    This is called the Taylor series expansion of \(f(x)\) expanded about \(a\text{.}\) In the particular case when \(a=0\text{,}\) we have
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f\left(x\right)=f\left(0\right)+\frac{f^{\prime}\left(0\right)}{1!}x+\frac{f^{\prime\prime}\left(0\right)}{2!}\ x^2+\frac{f^{\prime\prime\prime}\left(0\right)}{3!}\ x^3+\frac{f^{\prime\prime\prime\prime}\left(0\right)}{4!} x^4+\dots \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The specific Taylor series expanded about \(a=0\) is often called the Maclaurin series of \(f(x)\) in honor of the mathematician Colin Maclaurin (1698-1746). Both Taylor and Maclaurin were writing systematic expositions of Newton’s version of the calculus when they formulated these series expansions. Again, the notation is Lagrange’s and comes later. Before we go any further, let’s introduce some notation to make writing this a little less tedious and allow us to utilize summation notation. For example, if we follow the pattern in Taylor’s formula, we would have the coefficient of the 100th term look like this.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} a_{100}=\frac{f^{^{\prime\prime\prime\prime\prime\prime}\cdots \prime}\left(a\right)}{100!} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    where we have 100 slashes (primes) in the exponent. We need something a little more compact than \(f^{\prime\prime\prime\prime\prime\prime\prime\prime\prime\prime\prime\prime\prime}\left(a\right).\) The notation for the \(n^{th}\) derivative is \(f^{\left(n\right)}(a)\text{.}\) For this one you need to be careful. \({\boldsymbol{f}}^{\left(\boldsymbol{2}\right)}\left(\boldsymbol{a}\right)\boldsymbol{\neq }{\boldsymbol{f}}^{\boldsymbol{ }\boldsymbol{2}}\left(\boldsymbol{a}\right).\) The first one is the second derivative.
    \begin{equation*} f^{\left(2\right)}\left(a\right)=f^{\prime\prime}(a) \end{equation*}
    The second one is the function squared.
    \begin{equation*} f^2\left(a\right)={\left(f\left(a\right)\right)}^2 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    With these notations, Taylor’s formula looks like this.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f\left(x\right)=f\left(a\right)+\frac{f^{\left(1\right)}\left(a\right)}{1!}\left(x-a\right)+\frac{f^{\left(2\right)}\left(a\right)}{2!}{\left(x-a\right)}^2+\frac{f^{\left(3\right)}\left(a\right)}{3!}{\left(x-a\right)}^3+\frac{f^{\left(4\right)}\left(a\right)}{4!}{\left(x-a\right)}^4+\dots =f(a)+\sum^{\infty }_{n=1}{\frac{f^{\left(n\right)}\left(a\right)}{n!}{\left(x-a\right)}^n} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    so the hundredth term in this series would look like this.
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{f^{\left(100\right)}\left(a\right)}{100!}{\left(x-a\right)}^{100} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    To streamline this even further, we will denote \(f\left(a\right)=f^{\left(0\right)}\left(a\right)\) so our Taylor series representation becomes
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f\left(x\right)=\frac{f^{\left(0\right)}\left(a\right)}{0!}+\frac{f^{\left(1\right)}\left(a\right)}{1!}\left(x-a\right)+\frac{f^{\left(2\right)}\left(a\right)}{2!}{\left(x-a\right)}^2+\frac{f^{\left(3\right)}\left(a\right)}{3!}{\left(x-a\right)}^3+\frac{f^{\left(4\right)}\left(a\right)}{4!}{\left(x-a\right)}^4+\dots =\sum^{\infty }_{n=0}{\frac{f^{\left(n\right)}\left(a\right)}{n!}{\left(x-a\right)}^n}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Let’s test this systematic approach on something we already know. The geometric series
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{1-x}=1+x+x^2+x^3+\dots \end{equation*}
    is valid for \(-1\lt x\lt 1\text{.}\) Suppose we use this Taylor “machine” to generate the power series of \(f\left(x\right)=\frac{1}{1-x}\) expanded about \(a=0\text{.}\) We will do this systematically.

    Subsection 22.2.8 Power Series Solutions of Differential Equations

    As we have seen throughout the book, many physical phenomena can be modeled as the solution of a differential equation. This is a primary reason why calculus grew into the powerful tool we have today. We have presented some applications where the related differential equation can be solved by separating the variables and integrating. However, you should not be lulled into thinking that all differential equations can be solved in with this method. Solving differential equations by various means is a course in itself and even then, there are differential equations that the techniques taught in that course cannot handle. Mathematicians recognized this, and this is where power series really shone. In fact, we started this section by noting that
    \begin{equation*} e^x=1+\frac{x}{1!}+\frac{x^2}{2!}+\frac{x^3}{3!}+\dots\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Because they both satisfy the IVP
    \begin{equation*} \dfdx{y}{x}=y\ \ \ \ \ \ \ \ y\left(0\right)=1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Again, not all differential equations are easily solved with power series, but they do provide a powerful tool for handling a number of them. For example, consider the differential equation
    \begin{equation*} \dfdxn{y}{x}{2}=-4y \end{equation*}
    You may or may not recognize the solution to this, but in case you do, pretend that you don’t. Assume that the solution to this can be written as a power series
    \begin{equation*} y=a_0+a_1x+a_2x^2+a_3x^3+\dots \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The trick here is to substitute this power series into the differential equation and see what pattern emerges from the coefficients. Since we are looking for patterns, we will refrain from doing any “simplifications” which will hide the patterns. Our experience is that students need to be told this since “simplifying” has been ingrained into your heads. Differentiating our power series twice, we have
    \begin{equation*} \dfdx{y}{x}=1a_1+2a_2x+3a_3x^2+4a_4x^3+5a_5x^4+\dots \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \dfdxn{y}{x}{2}=2\cdot 1a_2+3\cdot 2a_3x+4\cdot 3a_4x^2+5\cdot 4a_5x^3+6\cdot 5a_6x^4+\dots \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Notice that we wrote out enough terms to establish a definite pattern and we didn’t hide it. Now let’s substitute into our differential equation.
    \begin{equation*} 2\cdot 1a_2+3\cdot 2a_3x+4\cdot 3a_4x^2+5\cdot 4a_5x^3+6\cdot 5a_6x^4+\dots =\dfdxn{y}{x}{2} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =-4y=-4\left(a_0+a_1x+a_2x^2+a_3x^3+a_4x^4+\dots \right) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =-4a_0-4a_1x-4a_2x^2-4a_3x^3-4a_4x^4-\dots \end{equation*}
    Equating coefficients, we have
    \begin{align*} 2\cdot 1a_2\amp{}=-4a_0 \amp{} a_2\amp{}=-\frac{4}{2!}a_0\\ 3\cdot 2a_3\amp{}=-4a_1 \amp{}a_3\amp{}=-\frac{4}{3!}a_1\\ 4\cdot 3a_4\amp{}=-4a_2 \amp{} a_4=-\frac{4}{4\cdot 3}a_2\amp{}=-\frac{4}{4\cdot 3}\left(-\frac{4}{2!}a_0\right)=\frac{4^2}{4!}a_0\\ 5\cdot 4a_5\amp{}=-4a_3 \amp{} a_5=-\frac{4}{5\cdot 4}a_3\amp{}=-\frac{4}{5\cdot 4}\left(-\frac{4}{3!}a_1\right)=\frac{4^2}{5!}a_1\\ 6\cdot 5a_6\amp{}=-4a_4 \amp{} a_6=-\frac{4}{6\cdot 5}a_4\amp{}=-\frac{4}{6\cdot 5}\left(\frac{4^2}{4!}a_0\right)=-\frac{4^3}{6!}a_0\\ 7\cdot 6a_7\amp{}=-4a_5 \amp{} a_7=-\frac{4}{7\cdot 6}a_5\amp{}=-\frac{4}{7\cdot 6}\left(\frac{4^2}{5!}a_1\right)=-\frac{4^3}{7!}a_1\\ \vdots \amp{}\amp{}\amp{}\vdots \end{align*}
    🔗
    Substituting these coefficients back into our power series, we get that the solution to the differential equation
    \begin{equation*} \dfdxn{y}{x}{2}=-4y \end{equation*}
    is given by the power series
    \begin{equation*} y=a_0+a_1x-\frac{4}{2!}a_0x^2-\frac{4}{3!}a_1x^3+\frac{4^2}{4!}a_0x^4+\frac{4^2}{5!}a_1x^5-\frac{4^3}{6!}a_0x^6-\frac{4^3}{7!}a_1x^7+\dots \end{equation*}
    where \(a_0\) and \(a_1\) are arbitrary constants.
    🔗
