    Section 2.2 Projectile motion: Another Look at Galileo’s Work

    In Section 2.1 we analyzed the fall of a dropped ball. But suppose the ball isn’t simply dropped. Suppose instead that we throw the ball straight up into the air. For the moment, we continue to ignore air resistance. How high will the ball go? How long will it take the ball to hit the ground? What will be its impact velocity? Suppose you throw the ball up with twice the initial speed. Will the ball go twice as high?
    These are not a new problems for us but when we addressed them using the techniques of Differential Calculus in [cross-reference to target(s) "SECTIONGalileoDrops" missing or not unique] our approach was a bit indirect. We return to them here because (1) We can attack them much more directly using Integral Calculus and (2) They serve as a good jumping off point for the more interesting problems to come. If you don’t recall what we did in [cross-reference to target(s) "SECTIONGalileoDrops" missing or not unique] you should review that section before proceeding.
    Thanks to Galileo we know that the acceleration of an object falling near the surface of the earth due to gravity is constantly \(9.8 \frac{\text{meters}}{\text{second}^2}\text{.}\) Thanks to Newton we know that velocity is the antiderivative of acceleration, so it would appear that
    \begin{equation} \text{velocity} = \int \text{acceleration}=\int{9.8}.\tag{2.7} \end{equation}
    This is essentially correct but we are not using our notation correctly. Do you see the problem?
    First, an acceleration is measured in \(\frac{\text{meters}}{\text{second}^2}\) but velocity is measured in \(\left(\frac{\text{meters}}{\text{second}}\right)\text{.}\) Since the dimensions don’t match equation (2.7) is not properly formed.

    Advise.

    Dimensions like \(\frac{\text{meters}}{\text{second}}\) or \(\frac{\text{newtons}}{\text{meter}^2}\text{,}\) or whatever, always come up in real world problems. It is only when we abstract away from the real world and do “pure” mathematics that they are not present. When they are present ignoring dimensions is almost always dangerous. Don’t do it.
    Second, even if we ignore the dimensions of our quantities, the expression \(\int 0.9\) asks us to sum infnitely many copies of \(9.8\text{.}\) This sum will clearly get arbitrarily large. But we know from our own experience living in the world that the velocity of a falling object is not infinite. The problem this time is that \(9.8\) is not a differential. Recall that we observed at the end of Subsection 1.1.2 that we can only integrate differentials.
    But notice how easy it was to pass lightly over equation (2.7) and believe that it was valid. Be careful. Always make sure the expressions you write down are sensible; that the dimensions match and that what you are integrating is a differential.
    Careful use of Leibniz’ differential notation will address both of these issues. Since we know that acceleration is the derivative of velocity we have
    \begin{equation} \dfdx{v}{t} = 9.8.\tag{2.8} \end{equation}
    Checking the dimensions we see that \(v\) is measured in \(\frac{\text{meters}}{\text{second}}\) and that \(t\) is measured in seconds. So \(\dfdx{v}{t} \) is measured in \(\frac{\frac{\text{meters}}{\text{seconds}}} {\text{seconds}}\) which, for convenience, we notate as \(\frac{\text{meters}}{\text{second}^2} \text{.}\)
    On the other hand, if we multiply both sides of equation (2.8) by \(\dx{t} \) seconds we get
    \begin{equation*} \dx{v} \frac{\text{meters} }{\text{second} }=9.8\frac{\text{meters}}{\text{second}\cdot{}\cancel{\text{second}}}\cdot \dx{t}\cancel{\text{ seconds}}= 9.8\dx{t}\frac{\text{meters}}{\text{second}} \end{equation*}
    In order to get a velocity by integration (summation) each of the terms being summed (the integrand) must be a velocity. Summing (integrating) both sides gives
    \begin{equation*} \int\dx{v} \frac{\text{meters} }{\text{second} }=\int 9.8\dx{t}\frac{\text{meters}}{\text{second}} \end{equation*}
    Including all of the dimensions in a computation can be very distracting so we will frequently leave them out. But this can be a deceptive practice so be careful. If you are ever unsure whether you are integrating the correct expression, check the dimensions, but don’t forget that the differentials also have dimensions. In this case \(\dx{t}\) measures time (in seconds).

    Example 2.2.0.1.

    Let’s say that the ball is thrown upward with an initial velocity of \(19.6\) meters per second (roughly \(44\) miles per hour) and is released at a point \(2\) meters above the ground. We’d like to answer these questions:
    1. How high will the ball go?
    2. When will it hit the ground?
    3. What will be the impact velocity?
    Suppose \(y=y(t)\) gives the height of the ball in meters and \(t\) is measured in seconds. Then we have the following information
    \begin{align*} \underbrace{\dfdxn{y}{x}{2}=-9.8\frac{\text{m}}{\text{s}^2}}_{\text{acceleration}}, \amp{}\amp{} \underbrace{\eval{\dfdx{y}{x}}{t}{0}=19.6\frac{\text{m}}{\text{s}}}_{\text{Inintial Velocity}}, \text{ and }\underbrace{y(0)=2 \text{ meters}}_{\text{Initial Position}}. \end{align*}
    Notice that the acceleration is constant and negative since we’ve assumed that down is the negative direction. We want to find
    1. \(y\) when \(\dfdx{y}{t}=0\) (height at the top of the flight, when the ball’s velocity is zero),
    2. \(t\) when \(y=0\) (the time when the ball hits the ground),
    3. \(\eval{\dfdx{y}{t}}{y}{0}\) (the impact velocity when the ball strikes the ground).
    Second derivatives are a little scary so, to avoid frightening ourselves, we make the substitution \(v=\dfdx{y}{t}\text{,}\) where \(v\) denotes velocity. If we let \(a\) denote acceleration we have
    \begin{equation} a=\dfdx{v}{t}=\dfdxn{y}{t}{2}\tag{2.9} \end{equation}
    Starting with our constant acceleration \(a=-9.8\text{,}\) integrating equation (2.9) once will give us the velocity, \(v(t)\text{.}\) Integrating a second time will give the position, \(y(t)\text{.}\) Once we find these, then we can answer the questions posed.

    Notation.

    More formally we have
    \begin{equation*} v(t)=\int y(t)\dx{t} \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \alpha{}(t)=\int\left(\int y(t)\dx{t}\right)\dx{t} \end{equation*}
    This last equation actually correct but it is way too scary to think about right now so we’ll keep things simple for now.
    We know that \(a=\dfdxn{y}{t}{2}=\dfdx{v}{t}=-9.8 \text{.}\) so
    \begin{equation*} \dx{v} =-9.8\dx{t} \text{.} \end{equation*}
    This says that the differential \(\dx{v} \) is equal to the differential \(-9.8\dx{t}\text{.}\) It follows that equality is maintained if we sum up all such differentials. So Therefore
    \begin{align*} \int\dx{v}\amp =\int-9.8\dx{t}\\ v+C_1\amp =-9.8t+C_2\\ v\amp =-9.8t+(\underbrace{C_2-C_1}_{= C}) \end{align*}
    To find \(c\) in this case, we observe that \(v(0)=19.6\text{.}\) Thus
    \begin{equation*} 19.6=v(0)=-9.8(0)+C =C. \end{equation*}
    So our velocity is given by
    \begin{equation*} v=-9.8t+19.6. \end{equation*}
    We next integrate \(v=\dfdx{y}{t} \) to find the ball’s position. Since velocity is the derivative of position we have
    \begin{align} v= \dfdx{y}{t}\amp =-9.8t+19.6\notag\\ \dx{y}\amp =\left(-9.8t+19.6\right)\dx{t}\notag\\ \int\dx{y}\amp =\int\left(-9.8t+19.6\right)\dx{t}\tag{2.10}\\ y\amp =-9.8\left(\frac{t^2}{2}\right)+19.6t+c\notag \end{align}

    Notation.

    Notice that in equation (2.10) we placed parentheses around the integrand: \(-9.8t+19.6\text{.}\) In this context this makes sense because we are thinking of \(\dx{t}\) as an infinitely small number so we need to indicate that the distributive law is in play.
    However, in a more modern formulation we don’t think of \(\dx{t}\) as a number. Instead the construct \(\int \cdots \dx{t}\) is all one abstract symbol, and the integrand is everything that appears where we have dots. In that formulation the parentheses are unnecessary. Thus we will frequently omit them here as well so that you can become accustomed to seeing integrals written this way.
    This time to find the constant \(C\) we observe that since \(y(0)=2\) we have
    \begin{equation*} y(0)=2=\eval{-4.9t^2+19.6t+C}{t}{0} =C. \end{equation*}
    Thus the ball’s position is given by: \(y(t)=-4.9t^2+19.6t+2\text{.}\)
    Once we know the acceleration, velocity, and position of the ball we can answer any question about it’s motion.
    At the top of its flight the velocity of the ball is zero so we set \(v=0\text{.}\) Solving \(v=0=-9.8t+19.6 \) for \(t\) gives \(t=2 \text{ seconds}\text{.}\) Thus the ball’s maximum height occurs two seconds after it is released and it will reach a height of
    \begin{equation*} y(2)=-4.9(2^2)+19.6(2)+2 = 21.6 \text{ meters}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    To find the impact velocity, we first need to find the time \(t\) when the ball hits the ground (when \(y(t)=0\)). Solving \(y(t)=0=-4.9t^2+19.6t+2\) for \(t\) gives
    \begin{align*} t\amp =\frac{-9.6\pm\sqrt{9.6^2-4(-4.9)(2)}}{(2)(-4.9)}\text{.} \end{align*}
    Thus we see that both \(t=4.1\) and \(t=-0.1\) solve our equation. However snce the negative value represents time in the past, so we can say that the ball will hit the ground \(t=4.1\) seconds after it is released. At that time the impact velocity is
    \begin{equation*} v(4.1) = -9.8(4.1)+19.6= -20.58 \frac{\text{m}}{\text{s}}. \end{equation*}
    The negative answer makes sense as the ball is traveling in the negative direction (down).

    Problem 2.2.0.2.

    Suppose we throw two balls straight up. Suppose the first has an initial velocity of \(v_0\) and the second has an initial velocity of \(2v_0\text{.}\) The maximum height of the second ball either be
    1. twice the maximum height of the first or,
    2. four times the maximum height of the first or,
    3. eight times the maximum height of the first.

    (a)

    Which of the options listed does your intuition say is correct?

    (b)

    Either show that your intuition is correct or show that it is incorrect. (Again, we ignore air resistance.)

    Problem 2.2.0.3.

    Automobile safety experts say that a skilled driver in a car with good tires and dry pavement can safely brake to a stop at a rate of \(20 \frac{\text{feet}}{\text{second}^2}\text{.}\)
    Suppose it takes you \(160\) feet to stop your car on a dry road where the speed limit is \(55 \frac{\text{miles}}{\text{hour}}\text{.}\) Were you speeding? Explain.
    Of course, the same principles will work in two dimensions as with a single dimension. We just treat the horizontal and vertical directions separately.

    Problem 2.2.0.4.

    Consider a projectile launched from the origin with an initial velocity of \(v_0\) at an angle of elevation \(\theta\text{.}\)
    Assume that there is no air resistance and the acceleration due to gravity is \(-g\text{.}\)

    (a)

    Show that the horizontal velocity is given by \(\dfdx{x}{t}=v_0\cos (\theta )\text{.}\)

    (b)

    Show that the vertical velocity is given by \(\dfdx{y}{t}=v_0\sin (\theta )-gt\text{.}\)

    (c)

    Find the parametric function \(P(t)=\ParamEqTwo{x(t)}{y(t)}\) which gives the projectile’s position as a function of time (it’s trajectory).
    Hint.
    You will need to use entries Integration Rule (1.11) and Integration Rule (1.12).

    (d)

    Show that the trajectory can also be given as the graph of
    \begin{equation} y=\frac{-g}{2v_0\cos(\theta)}x^2 +\tan(\theta)x.\tag{2.11} \end{equation}

    (e)

    Use equation (2.11) to show that the distance from its starting point where the projectile returns to the ground is given by
    \begin{equation*} \frac{v_0^2}{g}\sin(2\theta) \text{.} \end{equation*}

    (f)

    Show that the maximum range occurs when \(\theta=\frac{\pi}{4}\text{.}\)
    Mathematical Terminology.
    The distance a projectile flies is called its “range”. This is not to be confused with the range of a function.

    (g)

    On Feb. 6, 1971, astronaut Alan Shepard 1  pulled out a makeshift six-iron he smuggled on board Apollo \(14\) and hit two golf balls on the lunar surface 2 , becoming the first, and so far the only, person to play golf anywhere other than earth. Assuming that the gravity on the moon is \(1/6\) that of earth and that Shepard can hit the ball identically on the earth and the moon, and ignoring air resistance on the earth, would the ball go \(6\) times farther before it returns to the surface on the moon than on the earth? Justify your answer.
