    Section 20.2 Substitution

    The easiest way to compute an integral is to simply know what it is in the first place. For example, Integration Rule (18.11) tells us that, \(\int\cos(x){\dx{x} = \sin(x)+C }\text{.}\) But with a little cleverness we can use Integration Rule (18.11) to evaluate other integrals as well.
    We will need to be able to find antiderivatives of wide variety of functions. But so far we only know the thirteen listed in Section 20.2. A significant portion of any course in Integral Calculus is devoted to extending the list of known antiderivatives. We begin that process in this section.
    We start by simply extending the rules we already know. For example at first it seems that Integration Rule (18.11) only tells us that an antiderivative of \(\cos(x) \) is \(\sin(x)\text{,}\) but with a little cleverness, and basic Algebra, we can use it to compute other integrals that are “close to” \(\int \cos(x)\dx{x}\) as in the following example.

    Example 20.2.0.1. Integration By Simple Substitution.

    For example, to compute
    \begin{equation*} \int\cos(5x) \dx{x}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    first make the substitution \(u=5x\text{.}\) In that case \(\dx{u}=5\dx{x}\text{.}\) Substituting these into our integral we have
    \begin{equation} \int\cos(5x) \dx{x}= \frac15\int\cos(u) \dx{u}\text{,}\tag{20.7} \end{equation}
    and Integration Rule (18.11) tells us that
    \begin{equation*} \int\cos(5x) \dx{x} =\frac15\sin(u) +c\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Substituting back to the original variable (unsubstituting?) we have
    \begin{equation*} \int\cos(5x) \dx{x}= \frac15\sin(5x)+c\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Drill 20.2.0.2.

    Do all of the algebra necessary to show that equation (20.7) is correct. Explain each step.

    Drill 20.2.0.3.

    2. \(\displaystyle \int \sec(ax)\tan(ax)\dx{x}\)
    3. \(\displaystyle \int \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+a^2x^2}}\dx{x}\)
    4. \(\displaystyle \displaystyle\int \frac{1}{1+ax^2}\dx{x}\)

    Problem 20.2.0.4.

    Integration by substitution seems to be a fairly simple technique when you first encounter it. But it will turn out to be a very sophisticated technique. We will explore this a bit here.

You will save yourself a great deal of time and frustration if you don’t try to anticipate the solution. And only skip the steps that you are very confident of.

    In the modern world the value of a published table of integrals has diminished considerably due to the creation of several very sophisticated mathematical software packages that will perform integrations, sometimes even very complex integrations very quickly. Most such packages are able to compute all of the integrals you will see in this course.
    That being so, students are often left wondering why we insist that you memorize the simpler integrals and learn the basice techniques that allow you to compute more complex integrals.
    There are a couple of reasons.
    Convenience
    Integrals are not stand–alone problems. Except in this course, they will always come to you as part of a larger, more complicated problem. If you are working on such a problem and the expression \(\int_{-a}^{a} (x^2+4)\sin(x)\dx{x}\) comes up you’d like to know that this is equal to zero without interupting your work to enter this integral into some software package so you can move on to the next step.
    Eventually it will be obvious that
    \begin{equation*} \int_{x=-a}^{a} (x^2+4)\sin(x)\dx{x}=0 \end{equation*}
    but it certainly is not obvious right now. For one thing we haven’t yet shown you what it means when we attach symbols to the top and bottom of the integral symbol: \(\int_{x=-a}^a{}\) means. We’ll come back to that in [provisional cross-reference: SECTIONDefiniteIntegrals].
    You need to be able to use your tools smoothly and efficiently when you are working, and integration is one of your tools. Having to stop working in the middle of every large computation you encounter in order to enter the smaller computions into software is like having to stop to watch a video explaining every step needed to do a small carpentry job on your home. It can be done, but it will be an onerous task at best.
    Intuition (or the lack thereof)
    A similar question can be asked about arithmetic. Was it really necessary for you to learn the arithmetic rules in grade school? After all, any calculuation you need to do now, as an adult, can be done more reliably by posing the question to your phone. Was learning arithmetic a wasted effort?
    Suppose that,
    • you had never learned any arithmetic,
    • you are planning a road trip of \(2,380\) miles, and
    • there is no source of fuel on the trip.
    If your car gets \(23.8\) miles per gallon of fuel. How much fuel do you need to bring? You ask your phone to compute \(2380/23.8\) and it tells you that you need one hundred gallons of fuel. Easy right?
    But remember you don’t know how to do this calculation yourself. You must rely completely on your phone for the answer so you can only pray that you posed the the problem correctly because — and this is the point — never having done any arithmetic computations yourself you have know way of knowing whether the computation should yield \(100\) gallons or \(1/100\) of a gallon.
    If you hadn’t spent large chucks of your childhood doing these kinds of computations yourself you’d have no intuition to bring to bear on the problem and you’d be unable to see that \(1/100\) gallon is far too small.
    Mistakes happen, even with software. When they do you need to have enough insight into the problem to know when the numbers are coming out wrong. A very good way to develop that kind of insight is to do problems without any assistance (software or otherwise).
    Software Doesn’t Always Give a Useful Answer
    The most sophisticated mathematical software has the cumulative experience and knowledge of the entire mathematical community built into it. As a result such software will often give answers which, although correct, are not helpful. Especially for a beginner.
    For example we entered the integral
    \begin{equation*} \int e^{-x^2}\dx{x} \end{equation*}
    into one such system. The answer we got back was
    \begin{equation*} \frac12 \sqrt{\pi }\erf(x) +C \end{equation*}
    which is correct, but what does it mean? If you can’t tell it is probably because you don’t know what \(\erf(x)\) means.
    You will see the function \(\erf(x) \) again in Problem 20.8.6.27 where we will explain what it means.
    The same system returned \(x_2\text{F}_1\left(-\frac12, \frac13; \frac43; x^3\right) \) when fed it the integral
    \begin{equation*} \int\sqrt{1-x^3}\dx{x} \text{.} \end{equation*}
    Even we (the authors) needed to do some investigating to figure out what this means as the symbolism is not part of our core knowledge. It turns out that \(\text{F}_1\left(-\frac12, \frac13; \frac43; x^3\right)\) is a particular Appell hypergeometric function.
    Does that help you to understand?
    The good news is that we’re just making a point. You don’t need to know what an Appell hypergeometric function is for this course.
    This is not to say that you should not use these tools at all. Mathematical software is available and it can be a marvelous help. But it is no substitute for skill, knowledge, or understanding and that is what you need to gain from this course. So use the software to help you learn. Use it to check your computations after you’ve completed them yourself. When you and the software disagree take the time to figure out what is wrong and who is right.

    Example 20.2.0.7.

    Suppose we need to solve the differential equation
    \begin{equation} \dfdx{y}{x} = \tan(x)\text{.}\tag{20.8} \end{equation}
    We proceed as above.
    \begin{align*} \dfdx{y}{x} \amp{}= \tan(x)\\ \dx{y} \amp{}= \tan(x)\dx{x}\\ \int\dx{y} \amp{}= \int\tan(x)\dx{x}\\ y \amp{}= \int\tan(x)\dx{x}\text{.} \end{align*}
    But of course we haven’t really made much progress until we can evaluate \(\int\tan(x)\dx{x}\text{.}\)

    Example 20.2.0.8. The Integral of the Tangent Function.

    Use Integration Rule (18.12) to compute \(\int\tan(x)\dx{x}\) by making the substitution
    \begin{gather*} z=\cos(x),\\ \end{gather*}

    which means that

    \begin{gather*} \dx{z}=-\sin(x)\dx{x}\\ \end{gather*}

    or

    \begin{gather*} \textcolor{red}{-\dx{z}=\sin(x)\dx{z}}\text{.} \end{gather*}
    Thus
    \begin{align*} \int\tan(x)\dx{x} = \int\frac{\textcolor{red}{\sin(x)}}{\cos(x)}\textcolor{red}{\dx{x}} \amp =-\int\frac{\dx{z}}{z}\\ \amp =-\ln(z)+C\\ \amp =(-1)\ln(\cos(x))+C\\ \amp =\ln\left((\cos(x))^{-1}\right)+C\\ \amp =\ln\left(\sec(x)\right)+C\text{.} \end{align*}

    (b)

    Find \(C\) if \(y(0)=2\text{.}\)

    Substitution, the Good, the Bad, and the Ugly.

    Making a substitution in an integral is just the first step. We still have to compute the integral. Our substitution just makes it a little simpler to apply formulas and techniques with which we are already familiar. As simple as it is, substitution is no mere trick. It is a very powerful technique, not just in differentiation and but in all of mathematics.
    The good news is that you can do any substitution you wish and as long as it is performed correctly, it is not wrong.
    But the bad news is that you can do any substitution you wish and as long as it is performed correctly, it is not wrong.
    That is to say, you can make any substitution you wish. As long as all calculations are done correctly, it will not be wrong in any absolute sense. But it may not be helpful either and it won’t always be easy to tell until you’ve done a lot of computations whether the substitution you chose is viable. This issue will be mitigated as you gain experience but, especially in the beginning, be careful. It is very easy to make what looks like a reasonable substitution which is either unworkable or, if it is workable, is much harder to do than different substitution would be.
    For example, when computing \(\int\tan(x)\dx{x}\text{,}\) we could have let \(z=x\text{.}\) In that case we would have \(\int\tan(x)\dx{x}=\int\tan(z)\dx{z}\) which is true, but it is not helpful.
    Another approach would be to let \(u=\tan(x)\) so that \(\dx{u}=\sec^2(x)\dx{x}\) or \(\dx{x} =\frac{\dx{u} }{\sec^2(x) }\text{.}\) In that case we have
    \begin{align} \int\tan(x)\dx{x}\amp{}=\int\frac{u}{\sec^2(x)}\dx{u}\notag\\ \amp{}=\int\frac{u}{1+\tan^2(x)}\dx{u}\notag\\ \end{align}

    so that

    \begin{align} \int\tan(x)\dx{x}=\amp{}=\int\frac{u}{1+u^2}\dx{u}\text{.}\tag{20.9} \end{align}
    This is a true statement — we haven’t made any computational errors — but it appears that after the substitution we will need to evaluate \(\int \frac{u}{1+u^2}\dx{y}\text{,}\) which looks hard. It is actually possible to compute this integral using the knowledge you now have. It is simply a matter of finding the right substitution. However, since we have already computed \(\int \tan(x) \dx{x}\) in Example 20.2.0.8 it is pointless to continue with this example.
    A comparably bad choice is \(u=\sin(x)\text{.}\) With this substitution we have \(\dx{u}=\cos(x)\dx{x}\) or \(\dx{x}=\frac{\dx{u}}{\cos(x)}\text{.}\) In this case we have
    \begin{align} \int\tan(x)\dx{x} =\amp{} \int\frac{\sin(x)}{\cos(x)}\dx{x}\notag\\ \amp{}= \int\left(\frac{u}{\cos(x)}\cdot\frac{\dx{u}}{\cos(x)}\right) =\int\frac{u}{1-u^2}\dx{u}\tag{20.10} \end{align}
    which is nearly the same, formally, as equation (20.9). The only difference seems to be that the “\(+\)” in the denominator in equation (20.9) became a “\(-\)” in equation (20.10).

    Observation.

    We say “formally” because they aren’t really the same. We’ve used the substitution variable \(u\) to represent two entirely different functions in each of these formula.

    Problem 20.2.0.11.

    On the other hand we know from Example 20.2.0.8 that \(\int\tan(x)\dx{x}=\ln(\sec(x)+c\) and we can use this knowledge.
    Suppose we need to compute \(\int\frac{u}{1+u^2}\dx{u}\) or \(\int\frac{u}{1-u^2}\dx{u}\text{.}\) The same substitution we used to transform \(\int\tan(x)\dx{x}\) these into the integrals (seen in equation (20.9) and equation (20.10)) will work in reverse.
    (a)
    Use the substitution \(u=\tan(x)\) to show that:
    \begin{equation*} \int\frac{u}{1+u^2}\dx{u} = \ln\left(\sec\left(\inverse\tan(u)\right)\right)+c. \end{equation*}
    Now use your Algebra and Trigonometry skills to show that \(\sec\left(\inverse\tan(u)\right) = \sqrt{1+u^2}\) so that
    \begin{equation*} \int\frac{u}{1+u^2}\dx{u} = \frac12\ln\left(1+u^2\right)+c\text{.} \end{equation*}
    (b)
    Use the substitution \(u=\sin(x)\) to show that
    \begin{equation*} \int\frac{u}{1-u^2}\dx{u}= \ln\left(\sec\left(\inverse\sin(u)\right)\right)+c. \end{equation*}
    Now use your Algebra and Trigonometry skills to show that \(\sec\left(\inverse\sin(u)\right) = \sqrt{1-u^2}\) so that
    \begin{equation*} \int\frac{u}{1-u^2}\dx{u} = -\frac{1}{2}\ln\left(1-u^2\right)+c\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Advice and Encouragement.

    You have surely noticed that the calculations you had to do to evaluate the integrals in Problem 20.2.0.11 were quite intricate. Not hard, per se, but there were a lot of steps and at each point you had to draw on just the right tool from Algebra, or Trigonometry, or Differential Calculus.
    For example, to get the one-half factor out in front you needed first to recognize that \(\sqrt{x}=x^{1/2}\) and then you needed the following property of logarithms: \(\ln(a^b)=b\ln(a)\text{.}\)
    This is what your math courses are going to be like from now on. You will continue to learn new techniques and acquire new tools. But the new material will frequently be fairly simple to understand but not so simple to implement. When struggling with a problem it is easy to make the mistake of thinking that you must have misunderstood the new material, but it will frequently be the case that you you really just don’t see which is the right tool to use, or how to use it.
    This will be frustrating at times. Don’t give up. Ask for help if you need it. But be patient with yourself. When you are deep into a problem it is easy to overlook simple things, and then to be embarrassed when your teacher or a classmate shows you that all you had to do was factor out an \(x\text{.}\) When this happens just learn the lesson, remember it, and move on. Embarassment is a wast of waste your time. And it will sap your self-confidence. We’ve all been there. Don’t waste your time on embarassment.

    Problem 20.2.0.12.

    The integrals in Problem 20.2.0.11.a and Problem 20.2.0.11.b can be evaluated by means of several different substitutions as we’ve seen. There are others that will also work but our principle point for now is that no substitution is “wrong”, some result in a new integral that is easier than the original and some will not. If our goal is simply to evaluate the integral we’ll want to look for the simplest method we can find.
    (a)

    \begin{equation*} \int\frac{x}{1+x^2}\dx{x} \end{equation*}
    Hint.

    (b)

    Hint.

    A good Rule of Thumb 1  for choosing a substitution is to look a the integrand (the part between \(\int \) and the differential) and see if you can find an expression and its derivative. That is how we chose the substitution \(z=\cos(x) \) in Example 20.2.0.8, and what the hints in Problem 20.2.0.12 tried to lead you to. If you can’t find an expression and its derivative try to find an expression and almost its derivative.

    Example 20.2.0.13.

    (a) An Unhelpful Substitution.
    For example, consider the integral
    \begin{equation*} \int x^2\sqrt{x^3+1}\dx{x} \end{equation*}
    We let \(u = x^2\) so that \(\dx{u} = 2x \dx{x}\text{.}\) This gives
    \begin{align*} \int x^2\sqrt{x^3+1}\dx{x}= \int u\sqrt{u^{3/2}+1}\left(\frac{\dx{u}}{2x}\right)\\ \end{align*}

    Notice that we can bring the \(1/2\) outside the integral because it is a constant, but since \(\frac{1}{x}\) is not a constant (it depends on \(u\)) we cannot do the same with with it so

    \begin{align*} \amp{}= \frac12\int\frac{u\sqrt{u^{3/2}+1}}{x}\dx{u}.\\ \end{align*}

    We could rewrite \(x = \sqrt{u}\) , so that

    \begin{align*} \int x^2\sqrt{x^3+1}\dx{x}\amp{}= \frac12\int\frac{u\sqrt{u^{3/2}}}{\sqrt{u}}\dx{u}\\ \amp = \frac12 \int\sqrt{u}\sqrt{u^{3/2}+1}\dx{u}\\ \amp =\frac12\int\sqrt{u^{5/2}+u}\dx{u}. \end{align*}
    This is all correct, but leads to an integral far worse than the original, But — and this is the point of this example — we have no way of seeing that this particular substitution will not be helpful until after we’ve done the computations above.
    The lessons here are (1) Be patient with yourself, because this is hard, and (2) Don’t give up, because this is hard.
    (b) A Helpful Substitution.
    But notice that \(x^2\) is almost the derivative of \(x^3 + 1\text{.}\) So let’s make \(y = x^3 + 1\) instead. Thus \(\dx{y} = 3x^2 \dx{x}\text{.}\) Will that extra factor of \(3\) be a problem for us? Let’s see.
    Making the substitution we have
    \begin{align*} \int x^2\sqrt{x^3+1}\dx{x}\amp =\int x^2\sqrt{y}\left(\frac{\dx{y}}{3x^2}\right)\\ \amp =\frac13\int y^{1/2}\dx{y}\\ \amp =\frac13\left(\frac{y^{3/2}}{3/2}\right)+c\\ \amp =\frac29\left(x^3+1\right)^{3/2}+c \end{align*}
    So what’s the method for making a substitution? Unfortunately, there is none. Basically, you can try anything. If it works, great! If it doesn’t, don’t do something incorrectly to try and force it. Try something else. Our Rule of Thumb will help, but there are no guarantees. This is what makes integration harder than differentiation, much as long division is harder than multiplication.
    The problem will not solve itself. Try something! As you practice, you will gain more experience. Look for formulas, methods, and ideas that you have seen before. Maybe they can be tweaked to address your problem. Maybe they can be applied directly. You won’t know until you try.
    And Practice, Practice, Practice!
    As a general rule the more integrals you know the better. This is because the integration techniques you will be learning do not allow you to compute an integral. They merely show you how to transform one integral into another. You saw this in equation (20.9) when we transformed \(\int\tan(x)\dx{x}\) into \(\int\frac{u}{1+u^2}\dx{u}\) and in Example 20.2.0.8 when we transformed \(\int\tan(x)\dx{x}\) into \(-\int\frac{\dx{z}}{z}\text{.}\) We did not compute these integrals, we merely transformed them. In Example 20.2.0.8 we transformed \(\int\tan(x)\dx{x} \) into an integral that we already knew.
    For that reason it is best to simply know — that is, to have memorized — as many integrals as you can. Each integral you know provides a target, something you can aim for as you try to transform a new integral into one you know.

    DIGRESSION: The Integral of an Arbitrary Polynomial in \(\sec(x) \) and \(\tan{x} \).

    In many Calculus textbooks the integral of the secant function is developed using the following 2  trick:
    \begin{align*} \int\sec(x)\dx{x} \amp{}= \int\sec(x)\underbrace{\frac{\tan (x)+\sec (x)}{\tan (x)+\sec (x)}}_{=1}\dx{x}.\\ \end{align*}

    Now let \(u=\tan(x)+\sec(x) \) so that \(\dx{u} =\sec(x)\tan(x)+\sec^2(x) \) so that

    \begin{align*} \amp{}=\int\frac1u\dx{u}\\ \amp{}=\ln\abs{u}+C\\ \amp{}=\ln\abs{\sec(x)+\tan(x)} +C \end{align*}
    Clearly whoever first computed the value of this integral found it by some other means (possibly by guessing) and then realized that multiplying by \(\frac{\tan (x)+\sec (x)}{\tan(x)+\sec (x)}\) and using the substitution \(u=\tan(x)+\sec(x) \) would suffice, even if it is not very illuminative.
    As an means of computing the integral this is fine. But as a teaching example it leaves a lot to be desired. In particular, there is no discernible reason for multiplying the integrand by one in this particular form. It is clearly a trick that was devised after the value of the integral had already been determined.

    Comment.

    An old joke says that a “technique” is a “trick” that you use more than once.
    We have called this a trick, but we can turn it into a technique by finding the underlying structure of the problem. We begin by defining:
    \begin{align} u\amp{}=\sec(x)+\tan(x)\tag{20.11}\\ v\amp{}=\sec(x)-\tan(x)\tag{20.12} \end{align}
    from which we see that
    \begin{align} \sec(x) \amp{}=\frac12 (u+v)\tag{20.13}\\ \tan(x) \amp{}=\frac12 (u-v).\tag{20.14} \end{align}

    Drill 20.2.0.15.

    (a)

    \begin{equation} uv=1.\tag{20.15} \end{equation}
    (b)

    \begin{equation} \sec(x)\dx{x} = \frac1u \dx{u} = - \frac1v \dx{v}. \tag{20.16} \end{equation}

    Example 20.2.0.16.

    (a)
    For our first example we will compute
    \begin{align*} \int\sec^3(x)\dx{x} \amp{}= \int \sec^2(x)\sec{x}\dx{x} \end{align*}
    From equation (20.13) we have
    \begin{align*} \int\sec^3(x)\dx{x}\amp{}=\frac14\int(u+v)^2\sec(x)\dx{x}\\ \amp{}=\frac14\int(u^2+2uv+v^2)\sec(x)\dx{x} \end{align*}
    and from equation (20.15) we have
    \begin{align*} \int\sec^3(x)\dx{x}\amp{}=\frac14\int(u^2+2+v^2))\sec(x)\dx{x}\\ =\amp{}\frac14\int u^2\left(\frac{1}{u}\dx{u}\right) -\frac14 \int v^2\left(\frac{1}{v}\dx{v}\right)+\frac12\int\sec(x)\dx{x}\\ =\amp{}\frac18\left(u^2-v^2\right)+\frac12\int\sec(x)\dx{x}\\ =\amp{}\frac18\left[\sec(x)+\tan(x)\right]^2 -\frac18\left[\sec(x)-\tan(x)\right]^2 +\frac12\abs{\sec(x)+\tan(x)}+C\\ =\amp{}\frac18\left[\sec^2(x)+2\sec(x)\tan(x) +\tan^2(x)\right]\\ \amp{}-\frac18\left[\sec^2(x)-2\sec(x)\tan(x) +\tan^2(x)\right]\\ \amp{}+\frac12\abs{\sec(x)+\tan(x)}+C. \end{align*}
    So, finally,
    \begin{equation*} \int\sec^3(x)\dx{x}=\frac12\left(\sec(x)\tan(x)+\abs{\sec(x)+\tan(x)}\right)+C. \end{equation*}
    In fact, in the paper cited above, McCammond shows that if \(P(s,t)\) is any polynomial in two variables then
    \begin{equation*} \int P\left(\sec(x), \tan(x)\right)\dx{x} \end{equation*}
    can be evaluated using the substitutions in equations (20.11) and (20.12), their properties in equations (20.13) and (20.14) above, and the judicious use of the Pythagorean trigonometric identity
    \begin{equation} \tan^2(x)+1=\sec^2(x).\tag{20.17} \end{equation}
