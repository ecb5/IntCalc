    Section 1.1 A Quick Review of Differential Calculus

    In Differential Calculus: From Practice to Theory we saw that the notation for an infinitesimal difference, “\(\dx{x} \text{,}\)” can be thought of as a refinement of the “change of \(x\)” symbol \(\Delta x\) which you learned when you were studying straight lines. The distinction is that we think of \(\Delta x = x_2-x_1\) as a measurable distance between \(x_2\) and \(x_1\) whereas \(\dx{x}\) represents and infinitesimal distance. To be sure we also learned in Differential Calculus: From Practice to Theory that the notion of an infinitesimal is very problematic as a logical foundation of our investigations. But as a way of thinking; as an appeal to intuition, it can be very useful. Naturally, any conclusions we draw from our intuitive approach will have to be confirmed logically but we needn’t fuss about that yet.
    By thinking in this way we found that we could extend the idea of the slope of a straight line to curved lines. The slope of a straight line is given by \(\frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}\) (the change of \(y\) over the change of \(x\)) so the slope of a curved line at the point \(x=a\) is given by the notation n
    \begin{equation*} \eval{\dfdx{y}{x}}{x}{a} \text{.} \end{equation*}
    It is necessary to specify the slope of the curve at the particular point \(x=a\) because it is clear that the slope of a curved line might change from point to point.
    This generalization of the notion of slope is very useful because our interpretation of the differential ratio, \(\dfdx{y}{x}\text{,}\) will vary depending on what physical quantities \(x\) and \(y\) happen to represent. In one simple interpretation if the variable \(y\) measures a vertical displacement and the variable \(x\) measures the passage of time then \(\dfdx{y}{t}\) represents the velocity in the vertical direction 1 . For example, the position of a ball tossed vertically with an initial velocity \(v_0\text{,}\) from an initial height \(y_0\text{,}\) will be
    \begin{equation*} y(t)=-4.9t^2+v_0t+y_0 \end{equation*}
    if \(y\) is measured in meters and \(t\) is measured in seconds.
    Thus the ball’s velocity will be
    \begin{equation*} v(t) = \dfdx{y}{t} = -9.8t+v_0 \end{equation*}
    and its acceleration will be
    \begin{equation*} a(t)=\dfdx{v}{t}=\dfdxn{y}{t}{2}=-9.8, \end{equation*}
    which is, of course, the rate of acceleration of an object falling near the surface of the earth, as determined experimentally by Galileo 2 
    One exercise in Differential Calculus asked you to show that if \(y(x)\) represents the shape of a catenary curve then \(y(x)\) will satisfy the differential equation
    \begin{equation} \dfdxn{y}{x}{2} =\frac{w}{H}\sqrt{1+\left(\dfdx{y}{x}\right)^2}.\tag{1.1} \end{equation}

    Drill 1.1.

    A later exercise had you show that in fact the function
    \begin{equation*} y(x)=\frac{H}{w}\left(\frac{e^{\frac{wx}{H}}+e^{\frac{-wx}{H}}}{2}\right) \end{equation*}
    satisfies equation (1.1). Repeat that computation by computing \(\dfdx{y}{x} \) and \(\dfdxn{y}{x}{2} \) and showing that \(y(x)\) satisfies equation (1.1).
    A natural question is “How did we find \(y(x)\text{?}\)” Answering that question is our current topic.
    In its simplest formulation Differential Calculus is about subtraction — differences. Thus is seems reasonable to suppose that the reverse process, called Integral Calculus is about addition — sums. That is, given a function \(y=y(x)\text{,}\) when we differentiate it we find the infinitesimal difference —\(\dx{y}\)— between two “infinitely close” points. In its simplest formulation integration is the process of adding up all of the differentials of \(y\) to recover \(y\) itself.

    Integral Notation.

    The summation of differentials is called integration and is denoted by
    \begin{equation*} \int\dx{y}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    An integral consists of two parts: The symbol “\(\int \)” which indicates summation, and the differential, \(\dx{y}\) in this case.

    A Little History.

    To a modern eye the integral sign looks like a stylized letter “S,” and that is often how it is described. But it isn’t stylized at all. When Calculus was invented in the seventeenth century the letter “S” typically had one of several forms, depending on where it appeared in a word. One of these forms was elongated to extend above and below the line of text. Leibniz adopted this as the integral sign. So, it is not really stylized. It is just archaic.
    Leibniz chose the letter “S” because it is the first letter of Latin word summa, from which we get the English word “sum.”
    For the early practitioners of Calculus an integral was always conceived of as simply a sum of differentials. In modern times, and especially since the work of Henri Lebesgue 3  integration theory has expanded considerably beyond this initial understanding. But the underlying notion of a sum is still very helpful and we will rely on it rather heavily, at least at first.

    Example 1.2. We Integrate Differentials, Not Functions.

    It is common to speak informally of “the integral of the function \(f(x)=2x\)” but this is not the correct usage. When we write \(\int 2x\dx{x}\) we are summing the differential  4  \(2x\dx{x} (=\dx{y})\text{,}\) not the function \(2x\text{.}\) It is important to keep this basic fact in mind as we go forward because it will save us much confusion later.
    If \(f(x)\) is some function the expression
    \begin{equation*} \int f(x) \end{equation*}
    is meaningless, despite the fact that nearly everyone occasionally abuses the notation in this way. The integral sign is intended to express the summation of differentials. Any other usage is meaningless, no matter how convenient it might be.
    Suppose that \(y=x^2\) then \(\dx{y} =2x\dx{x}\text{.}\) Then \(\int 2x\dx{x}\) makes sense because the product \(2x\dx{x}=\dx{y}\) is a differential. But \(\int 2x\) is nonsense because \(2x\) alone is not a differential.
    In particular, \(\int 2x\) is meaningless. However if \(y=x^2\) then what is probably meant is
    \begin{equation} \int 2x\dx{x} = \int \dx{y}\tag{1.2} \end{equation}
    which is equal to \(y\) since \(\int \dx{y}\) means “add up the differentials of \(y\text{.}\)”
    In equation (1.2) we confidently asserted that
    \begin{equation*} \int \dx{y}=y\text{.} \end{equation*}
    But this is not quite true. Or rather, it is too vague because we have not define our notation clearly yet. The problem, however is not in our understanding. It is certainly true that the sum of all of the differentials of \(y\) will be \(y\) itself. It is in our sloppy use of notation.

    Integral Notation Can Be Difficult to Use.

    Suppose a point \(P\) on the real line begins at \(x_0\) and traces out a line segment of some unknown length, \(x\text{.}\)
    Notice that \(x\) represents the length of the line segment which \(P\) has traced out. In particular \(x\) is Not the position of \(P\) on the line.

    Mathematical Notation.

    Sometimes we will call the line “the \(x\)-axis.” But this is a bit confusing as we’ve already used \(x\) to indicate a position on the line. Using \(x\) in two incompatible ways like this is not precise enough for mathematics and we shouldn’t really do it. Nor should you. But, for better or worse mathematics is done, and learned, by flawed and imprecise human creatures so we have to find ways to cope with our foibles.
    To avoid confusion you should always question the meaning of the symbols being used. If you can’t state the meaning precisely then you’re going to have trouble understanding what is being said. We must be very careful about the meanings of our symbols.
    We partition the line segment between \(x_0\) and \(x\) (which we have also called \(x_n\) in the sketch below) into subintervals as seen in sketch below.
    Representing the first subinterval of \(x\) with \(\Delta x_1\text{,}\) the second with \(\Delta x_2\text{,}\) and in general if \(\Delta{x_i}\) represents the \(i\)th subinterval of \(x\) then clearly the length of the line segment is
    \begin{gather*} \Delta{x_1}+\Delta{x_2}+\Delta{x_3} +\cdots +\Delta{x_{n-1}}+\Delta{x_n}\\ = (x_1-x_0)+(x_2-x_1)+(x_3-x_2) +\cdots + (x_{n-1}-x_{n-2})+(x_n-x_{n-1})\\ =x_n-x_0. \end{gather*}
    If the subintervals are all the same length, say \(\Delta{x}\text{,}\) then we no longer need the subscripts to distinguish them, and we have a compact symbol for indicating summation. We may as well use it and write
    \begin{equation} \underbrace{\Delta{x}+\Delta{x} + \cdots{}+\Delta{x}}_{\text{n copies}} = \int{}\Delta{x}=x_n-x_0 \tag{1.3} \end{equation}
    instead.
    We assume, with Leibniz, that equation (1.3) remains true when the finite differences (\(\Delta{x}\)) are replaced with infinitesimal differences (\(\dx{x}{}\)):
    \begin{equation*} \int{\dx{x}{}}=x_n-x_0. \end{equation*}
    So the statement \(\int\dx{x}=x_n-x_0\) says that if we add up the lengths of all of the differentials that make up the line segment from \(x_0\) to \(x_n\) we will get its length, \(x_n-x_0\text{,}\) which we called \(x\) above. Therefore
    \begin{equation*} \int\dx{x}=x \end{equation*}
    and there is no ambiguity.
    In the early nineteenth century Joseph Fourier found that Leibniz’ original integral symbol was not quite descriptive enough for his purposes. In particular since we’ve dropped the subscripts the expression \(\int{\dx{x}}\) gives no clue where we need to start, and stop, summing the differentials. To clarify Fourier modified Leibniz’ notation by inserting both a subscript and a superscript as follows:
    \begin{equation} \int_{x_0}^{x_n}\dx{x}=x_n-x_0\text{.}\tag{1.4} \end{equation}
    Equation (1.4) says that we begin summing the differentials at \(x_0\text{,}\) and end at \(x_n\text{,}\) resulting in the difference \(x_n-x_0\text{.}\)

    Example 1.3. The Problem of Zero Differentials.

    Naturally, things are not quite this simple. Consider
    \begin{align*} f_1(x)\amp{}=x^2+7 \text{ and}\\ f_2(x)\amp{}=x^2+4. \end{align*}
    Clearly \(\dx{f_1}=\dx{f_2}=2x\) so the sum of \(\dx{f_1}\) and the sum of \(\dx{f_2}\) would seem to be the same as. But clearly
    \begin{equation*} \text{ the sum of the}\dx{y_1}=x^2+7 \end{equation*}
    whereas
    \begin{equation*} \text{ the sum of the}\dx{y_2}=x^2+5\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This apparent contradiction needs to be resolved.
    One way to resolve it is to form the function
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=y_1(x)-y_2(x) \end{equation*}
    and notice that since \(\dx{y_1}=\dx{y_2} \) it follows that
    \begin{equation*} \dx(y_1-y_2)=\dx{y_1}-\dx{y_2}=0. \end{equation*}
    Dividing by \(\dx{x}\) and thinking of \(\dfdx{y_1}{x} \) and \(\dfdx{y_2}{x} \) as slopes, we have
    \begin{equation*} \dfdx{f}{x}= \dfdx{y_1}{x} -\dfdx{y_2}{x} =\dfdx{(y_1-y_2)}{x}=0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This says that the curve \(f(x) = y_1(x) - y_2(x)\) has a slope which is constantly equal to zero. A moment’s thought will suggest that this curve must be a horizontal line for if not then somewhere it should have a nonzero slope. This argument can be made rigorous, but we will not fuss over that right now.
