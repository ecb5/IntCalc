    Section 1.4 Accounting for Resistance – This Looks Like a Job for Substitution!

    To keep things simple we have ignored air resistance so far. But, depending on the conditions, resistance can have a substantial effect on a body’s motion. So we need to account for it in our model.
    In general, the faster the body is moving through a fluid (like air or water) the more the fluie tends to resist the motion. Hence the resistive force, or drag, is most simply modeled as a function of the body’svelocity. You can experience this directly by holding your hand out a car window. The faster the car moves, the more force you will feel on your hand. Similarly, the faster you move your hand under water, the more force you will feel resisting the motion.
    Many phenomena will affect drag (speed, viscosity of the medium, shape and size of the object, turbulence). For a medium with a relatively high viscosity and a relatively small object the assumption that the drag is proportional to the velocity of the object makes for a reasonably accurate model. An example of this is a grain of sand falling in water.
    For a medium with a relatively low viscosity, such as air, and a relatively large object, the drag is assumed to be proportional to the square of the velocity. This would be the model to use for a baseball falling in the air.
    Of course to get a really accurate picture, we could use both a linear term and a quadratic term to represent the drag, but we will keep things as simple as possible for now. In our first example we’ll assume that the resistive force is proportional to velocity. This is called linear drag.

    Example 1.4.0.1.

    To model a grain of sand with mass \(m\) descending in water we let \(y(t)\) be distance the sand has fallen (so the positive \(y\) axis is pointing downward), with \(y(0)=0\) representing the surface of the water. From the diagram below we see that there are three forces acting on the sand: (1) the weight of the sand grain, (2) the buoyancy of the sand grain, and (3) the drag that the water imposes on the sand grain.
    The total force acting on the grain, \(F=m\dfdxn{y}{t}{2} \text{,}\) will be equal to the sum of these three forces.
    As always we denote the acceleration due to gravity by \(g\text{.}\) Then the weight of the sand is \(mg\text{.}\) The drag, which we assume is linear, is proportional to the velocity, so we will denote this by \(-Rv\text{,}\) where \(R\) is a positive constant and \(v=\dfdx{y}{t}\) is the velocity. Notice that this force is negative as the drag is necessarily in the direction opposite of the motion.
    Since we have not discussed buoyancy before, a short explanation is in order. The buoyancy of an object is force that a surrounding fluid applies to it. This force is equal to the weight of the fluid being displaced by the object. If the density of the object is less than the density of water then the weight of the object and it’s boyancy will balance before the object is completely covered by the fluid. That is, the object floats.

    Substitution, the Good, the Bad, and the Ugly.

    Problem 1.4.0.2 uses the technique of substitution. When we were learning differentiation we called this “making things easier on the eyes” because in that context the substitutions we made almost always resulted in simpler formulas that were easier to deal with than the originals..
    Our goal when making a substitution is to simplify things eventually. But sometimes things will get more complicated first. We will proceed slowly.

    Example 1.4.0.3. The Integral of the Tangent Function.

    One of the entries in Table 1.2.0.1, was \(\int\sin(x)\dx{x} = -\cos(x)+c\text{.}\) We can use this to compute \(\int\tan(x)\dx{x}\) by making the substitution
    \begin{gather*} z=\cos(x),\\ \end{gather*}

    which means that

    \begin{gather*} \dx{z}=-\sin(x)\dx{x}\\ \end{gather*}

    or

    \begin{gather*} \textcolor{red}{-\dx{z}=\sin(x)\dx{z}}\text{.} \end{gather*}
    Thus
    \begin{align*} \int\tan(x)\dx{x} = \int\frac{\textcolor{red}{\sin(x)}}{\cos(x)}\textcolor{red}{\dx{x}} \amp =-\int\frac{\dx{z}}{z}\\ \amp =-\ln(z)+c\\ \amp =(-1)\ln(\cos(x))+c\\ \amp =\ln\left((\cos(x))^{-1}\right)+c\\ \amp =\ln\left(\sec(x)\right)+c\text{.} \end{align*}

    Drill 1.4.0.4.

    Use a similar substitution to show that
    \begin{equation*} \int\cot(x)\dx{x}=-\ln(\csc(x)+c \end{equation*}
    Making a substitution in an integral is just the first step. We still have to compute the integral. Our substitution just makes it a little simpler to apply formulas and techniques with which we are already familiar. As simple as it is, substitution is no mere trick. It is a very powerful technique, not just in differentiation an integration but in all of mathematics.
    The good news is that you can do any substitution you wish and as long as it is performed correctly, it is not wrong.
    But the bad news is that you can do any substitution you wish and as long as it is performed correctly, it is not wrong.
    That is to say, you can make any substitution you wish. As long as all calculations are done correctly, it will not be wrong in any absolute sense. But it may not be helpful either and it won’t always be easy to tell until you’ve done a lot of computations whether the substitution you chose is viable. This issue will be mitigated as you gain experience but, especially in the beginning, be careful. It is very easy to make what looks like a reasonable substitution which is either unworkable or, if it is workable, is much harder to do than different substitution would be.
    For example, when computing \(\int\tan(x)\dx{x}\text{,}\) we could have let \(z=x\text{.}\) In that case we would have \(\int\tan(x)\dx{x}=\int\tan(z)\dx{z}\) which is true, but it is not helpful.
    Another approach would be to let \(u=\tan(x)\) so that \(\dx{u}=\sec^2(x)\dx{x}\) or \(\dx{x} =\frac{\dx{u} }{\sec^2(x) }\text{.}\) In that case we have
    \begin{align} \int\tan(x)\dx{x}\amp{}=\int\frac{u}{\sec^2(x)}\dx{u}\notag\\ \amp{}=\int\frac{u}{1+\tan^2(x)}\dx{u}\notag\\ \end{align}

    so that

    \begin{align} \int\tan(x)\dx{x}=\amp{}=\int\frac{u}{1+u^2}\dx{u}\text{.}\tag{1.10} \end{align}
    This is a true statement — we haven’t made any computational errors — but it appears that after the substitution we will need to evaluate \(\int \frac{u}{1+u^2}\dx{y}\text{,}\) which looks hard. It is actually possible to compute this integral using the knowledge you now have. It is simply a matter of finding the right substitution. However, since we have already computed \(\int \tan(x) \dx{x}\) in Example 1.4.0.3 it is pointless to continue with this example.
    A comparably bad choice is \(u=\sin(x)\text{.}\) With this substitution we have \(\dx{u}=\cos(x)\dx{x}\) or \(\dx{x}=\frac{\dx{u}}{\cos(x)}\text{.}\) In this case we have
    \begin{align} \int\tan(x)\dx{x} =\amp{} \int\frac{\sin(x)}{\cos(x)}\dx{x}\notag\\ \amp{}= \int\left(\frac{u}{\cos(x)}\cdot\frac{\dx{u}}{\cos(x)}\right) =\int\frac{u}{1-u^2}\dx{u}\tag{1.11} \end{align}
    which is nearly the same, formally, as equation (1.10). The only difference seems to be that the “\(+\)” in the denominator in equation (1.10) became a “\(-\)” in equation (1.11).

    Observation.

    We say “formally” because they aren’t really the same. We’ve used the substitution variable \(u\) to represent two entirely different functions in each of these formula.

    Problem 1.4.0.5.

    On the other hand we know from Example 1.4.0.3 that \(\int\tan(x)\dx{x}=\ln(\sec(x)+c\) and we can use this knowledge.
    Suppose we need to compute \(\int\frac{u}{1+u^2}\dx{u}\) or \(\int\frac{u}{1-u^2}\dx{u}\text{.}\) The same substitution we used to transform \(\int\tan(x)\dx{x}\) these into the integrals (seen in equation (1.10) and equation (1.11)) will work in reverse.
    (a)
    Use the substitution \(u=\tan(x)\) to show that:
    \begin{equation*} \int\frac{u}{1+u^2}\dx{u} = \ln\left(\sec\left(\inverse\tan(u)\right)\right)+c. \end{equation*}
    Now use your Algebra and Trigonometry skills to show that \(\sec\left(\inverse\tan(u)\right) = \sqrt{1+u^2}\) so that
    \begin{equation*} \int\frac{u}{1+u^2}\dx{u} = \frac12\ln\left(1+u^2\right)+c\text{.} \end{equation*}
    (b)
    Use the substitution \(u=\sin(x)\) to show that
    \begin{equation*} \int\frac{u}{1-u^2}\dx{u}= \ln\left(\sec\left(\inverse\sin(u)\right)\right)+c. \end{equation*}
    Now use your Algebra and Trigonometry skills to show that \(\sec\left(\inverse\sin(u)\right) = \sqrt{1-u^2}\) so that
    \begin{equation*} \int\frac{u}{1-u^2}\dx{u} = -\frac{1}{2}\ln\left(1-u^2\right)+c\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Advice and Encouragement.

    You have surely noticed that the calculations you had to do to evaluate the integrals in Problem 1.4.0.5 were quite intricate. Not hard, per se, but there were a lot of steps and at each point you had to draw on just the right tool from Algebra, or Trigonometry, or Differential Calculus.
    For example, to get the one-half factor out in front you needed first to recognize that \(\sqrt{x}=x^{1/2}\) and then you needed the following property of logarithms: \(\ln(a^b)=b\ln(a)\text{.}\)
    This is what your math courses are going to be like from now on. You will continue to learn new techniques and acquire new tools. But the new material will frequently be fairly simple to understand but not so simple to implement. When struggling with a problem it is easy to make the mistake of thinking that you must have misunderstood the new material, but it will frequently be the case that you you really just don’t see which is the right tool to use, or how to use it.
    This will be frustrating at times. Don’t give up. Ask for help if you need it. But be patient with yourself. When you are deep into a problem it is easy to overlook simple things, and then to be embarrassed when your teacher or a classmate shows you that all you had to do was factor out an \(x\text{.}\) When this happens just learn the lesson, remember it, and move on. There is no need for embarassment and it will only waste your time and sap your self-confidence. We’ve all been there.

There are others that will also work but our principle point for now is that no substitution is “wrong”, some result in a new integral that is easier than the original and some will not. If our goal is simply to evaluate the integral we’ll want to look for the simplest method we can find.

    A good Rule of Thumb 2  for choosing a substitution is to look a the integrand (the part between \(\int \) and the differential) and see if you can find an expression and its derivative. That is how we chose the substitution \(z=\cos(x) \) in Example 1.4.0.3, and what the hints in Problem 1.4.0.6 tried to lead you to. If you can’t find an expression and its derivative try to find an expression and almost its derivative.

    Example 1.4.0.7.

    (a) An Unhelpful Substitution.
    For example, consider the integral
    \begin{equation*} \int x^2\sqrt{x^3+1}\dx{x} \end{equation*}
    We let \(u = x^2\) so that \(\dx{u} = 2x \dx{x}\text{.}\) This gives
    \begin{align*} \int x^2\sqrt{x^3+1}\dx{x}= \int u\sqrt{u^{3/2}+1}\left(\frac{\dx{u}}{2x}\right)\\ \end{align*}

    Notice that we can bring the \(1/2\) outside the integral because it is a constant, but since \(\frac{1}{x}\) is not a constant (it depends on \(u\)) we cannot do the same with with it so

    \begin{align*} \amp{}= \frac12\int\frac{u\sqrt{u^{3/2}+1}}{x}\dx{u}.\\ \end{align*}

    We could rewrite \(x = \sqrt{u}\) , so that

    \begin{align*} \int x^2\sqrt{x^3+1}\dx{x}\amp{}= \frac12\int\frac{u\sqrt{u^{3/2}}}{\sqrt{u}}\dx{u}\\ \amp = \frac12 \int\sqrt{u}\sqrt{u^{3/2}+1}\dx{u}\\ \amp =\frac12\int\sqrt{u^{5/2}+u}\dx{u}. \end{align*}
    This is all correct, but leads to an integral far worse than the original, But — and this is the point of this example — we have no way of seeing that this particular substitution will not be helpful until after we’ve done the computations above.
    The lessons here are (1) Be patient with yourself, because this is hard, and (2) Don’t give up, because this is hard.
    (b) A Helpful Substitution.
    But notice that \(x^2\) is almost the derivative of \(x^3 + 1\text{.}\) So let’s make \(y = x^3 + 1\) instead. Thus \(\dx{y} = 3x^2 \dx{x}\text{.}\) Will that extra factor of \(3\) be a problem for us? Let’s see.
    Making the substitution we have
    \begin{align*} \int x^2\sqrt{x^3+1}\dx{x}\amp =\int x^2\sqrt{y}\left(\frac{\dx{y}}{3x^2}\right)\\ \amp =\frac13\int y^{1/2}\dx{y}\\ \amp =\frac13\left(\frac{y^{3/2}}{3/2}\right)+c\\ \amp =\frac29\left(x^3+1\right)^{3/2}+c \end{align*}
    So what’s the method for making a substitution? Unfortunately, there is none. Basically, you can try anything. If it works, great! If it doesn’t, don’t do something incorrectly to try and force it. Try something else. Our Rule of Thumb will help, but there are no guarantees. This is what makes integration harder than differentiation, much as long division is harder than multiplication.
    The problem will not solve itself. Try something! As you practice, you will gain more experience. Look for formulas, methods, and ideas that you have seen before. Maybe they can be tweaked to address your problem. Maybe they can be applied directly. You won’t know until you try.
    And Practice, Practice, Practice!
    As a general rule the more integrals you know the better. This is because the integration techniques you will be learning do not allow you to compute an integral. They merely show you how to transform one integral into another. You saw this in equation (1.10) when we transformed
    \begin{equation*} \int\tan(x)\dx{x} \end{equation*}
    into
    \begin{equation*} \int\frac{u}{1+u^2}\dx{u} \end{equation*}
    and in Example 1.4.0.3 when we transformed
    \begin{equation*} \int\tan(x)\dx{x} \end{equation*}
    into
    \begin{equation*} -\int\frac{\dx{z}}{z}. \end{equation*}
    We did not compute these integrals, we merely transformed them. In Example 1.4.0.3 we transformed \(\int\tan(x)\dx{x} \) into an integral that we knew from our work in differentiation, but this was information that we simply drew from our memory.
    For that reason it is best to simply know — that is, to have memorized — as many integrals as you can. Each integral you know provides a target, something you can aim for as you try to transform a new integral into one you know.

    DIGRESSION: The Integral of an Arbitrary Polynomial in \(\sec(x) \) and \(\tan{x} \).

    In many Calculus textbooks the integral of the secant function is developed using the following 3  trick:
    \begin{align*} \int\sec(x)\dx{x} \amp{}= \int\sec(x)\underbrace{\frac{\tan (x)+\sec (x)}{\tan (x)+\sec (x)}}_{=1}\dx{x}.\\ \end{align*}

    Now let \(u=\tan(x)+\sec(x) \) so that \(\dx{u} =\sec(x)\tan(x)+\sec^2(x) \) so that

    \begin{align*} \amp{}=\int\frac1u\dx{u}\\ \amp{}=\ln\abs{u}+c\\ \amp{}=\ln\abs{\sec(x)+\tan(x)} +c \end{align*}
    Clearly whoever first computed the value of this integral found it by some other means (possibly by guessing) and then realized that multiplying by \(\frac{\tan (x)+\sec (x)}{\tan(x)+\sec (x)}\) and using the substitution \(u=\tan(x)+\sec(x) \) would suffice, even if it is not very illuminative.
    As an means of computing the integral this is fine. But as a teaching example it leaves a lot to be desired. In particular, there is no discernible reason for multiplying the integrand by one in this particular form. It is clearly a trick that was devised after the value of the integral had already been determined.

    Comment.

    An old joke says that a “technique” is a “trick” that you use more than once.
    We have called this a trick, but we can turn it into a technique by finding the underlying structure of the problem. We begin by defining:
    \begin{align} u\amp{}=\sec(x)+\tan(x)\tag{1.12}\\ v\amp{}=\sec(x)-\tan(x)\tag{1.13} \end{align}
    from which we see that
    \begin{align} \sec(x) \amp{}=\frac12 (u+v)\tag{1.14}\\ \tan(x) \amp{}=\frac12 (u-v).\tag{1.15} \end{align}

    Drill 1.4.0.9.

    (a)
    Show that
    \begin{equation} uv=1.\tag{1.16} \end{equation}
    (b)
    Show that
    \begin{equation} \sec(x)\dx{x} = \frac1u \dx{u} = - \frac1v \dx{v}. \tag{1.17} \end{equation}

    Example 1.4.0.10.

    (a)
    For our first example we will compute
    \begin{align*} \int\sec^3(x)\dx{x} \amp{}= \int \sec^2(x)\sec{x}\dx{x} \end{align*}
    From equation (1.14) we have
    \begin{align*} \int\sec^3(x)\dx{x}\amp{}=\frac14\int(u+v)^2\sec(x)\dx{x}\\ \amp{}=\frac14\int(u^2+2uv+v^2)\sec(x)\dx{x} \end{align*}
    and from equation (1.16) we have
    \begin{align*} \int\sec^3(x)\dx{x}\amp{}=\frac14\int(u^2+2+v^2))\sec(x)\dx{x}\\ =\amp{}\frac14\int u^2\left(\frac{1}{u}\dx{u}\right) -\frac14 \int v^2\left(\frac{1}{v}\dx{v}\right)+\frac12\int\sec(x)\dx{x}\\ =\amp{}\frac18\left(u^2-v^2\right)+\frac12\int\sec(x)\dx{x}\\ =\amp{}\frac18\left[\sec(x)+\tan(x)\right]^2 -\frac18\left[\sec(x)-\tan(x)\right]^2 +\frac12\abs{\sec(x)+\tan(x)}+c\\ =\amp{}\frac18\left[\sec^2(x)+2\sec(x)\tan(x) +\tan^2(x)\right]\\ \amp{}-\frac18\left[\sec^2(x)-2\sec(x)\tan(x) +\tan^2(x)\right]\\ \amp{}+\frac12\abs{\sec(x)+\tan(x)}+c. \end{align*}
    So, finally,
    \begin{equation*} \int\sec^3(x)\dx{x}=\frac12\left(\sec(x)\tan(x)+\abs{\sec(x)+\tan(x)}\right)+c. \end{equation*}
    In fact, in the paper cited above, McCammond shows that if \(P(s,t)\) is any polynomial in two variables then
    \begin{equation*} \int P\left(\sec(x), \tan(x)\right)\dx{x} \end{equation*}
    can be evaluated using the substitutions in equations (1.12) and (1.13), their properties in equations (1.14) and (1.15) above, and the judicious use of the Pythagorean trigonometric identity
    \begin{equation} \tan^2(x)+1=\sec^2(x).\tag{1.18} \end{equation}
