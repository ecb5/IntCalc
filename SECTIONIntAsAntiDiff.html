    Section 1.2 Integration as Antidifferentiation

    Definition 1.2.

    Suppose we have a function
    \begin{equation} y=F(t)\tag{1.5} \end{equation}
    whose derivative is \(\dfdx{y}{t}=f(t)\text{.}\) Then \(F(t)\) is called an antiderivative of \(f(t)\text{.}\)
    It follows immediately that if a function has one antiderivative then it has infinitely many since, by the Constant Rule, adding a constant to a function will not change its derivative. Thus if we know that \(F(x)\) is one antiderivative of \(f(x)\text{,}\) then we know all (infinitely many) of the antiderivatives of \(f(x)\) because we get a (slightly) different antiderivative each time we add a constant to \(F(x)\text{.}\) Remember this. We will return to it when we discuss equation (6.3) below.
    Returning to equation (1.5), we know from our study of Differential Calculus that
    \begin{equation*} \dx{y}=F^\prime(t)\dx{t}=f(t)\dx{t}, \end{equation*}
    and we know from our work in Section 1.1 that
    \begin{equation} y=\int\dx{y}\text{.}\tag{1.6} \end{equation}
    Therefore
    \begin{equation*} y=\int\dx{y}= \int f(t)\dx{t}. \end{equation*}
    Observe that since \(\dx{y} \) is a differential it follows that \(f(t)\dx{t}\) is also a differential so the expression \(\int f(t)\dx{t}\) is a valid integral.
    Thus it appears that if \(\dx{y}=f(t)\dx{t} \) is known then \(y=F(t)\text{,}\) will be the antiderivative of \(f(t)\text{.}\) That is to say, integration appears to be “differentiation run backwards.”

    We Integrate Differentials, Not Functions.

    Integration can be usefully thought of as either summation or antidifferentiation. But the integral sign is designed to indicate the summation of differentials, not antidifferentiation. It is thus very easy to use the notation improperly. Sooner or later most people will fall into the habit of dropping the final “\(\dx{t}{}\)”, writing
    \begin{align*} \amp{}\int f(t) \amp{}\text{ instead of }\amp{}\amp{}\int f(t)\dx{t}, \end{align*}
    and calling them both the integral of the function \(f(t)\text{.}\) This is an unfortunate, but common, abuse of the notation. We strongly suggest that you take care to avoid this bad habit for as long as you can. Especially when the concepts are still new it can be very confusing.

    Mathematical Notation.

    By calling \(\int f(t)\) meaningless we have overstated the situation a bit. If you study mathematics long enough you will encounter situations where this is a perfectly reasonable notation to use. But we will not encounter them here, and it is best if you don’t fall into this habit while you are still learning the basics.
    Remember that
    \begin{equation*} \int f(t) \end{equation*}
    is meaningless no matter how convenient it might be because it indicates that we are summing a function, not a differential.. But the meaning of
    \begin{equation*} \int f(t)\dx{t} \end{equation*}
    is clear: We’re summing up all of the differentials of the form \(f(t)\dx{t} \text{.}\)
    This is true as far as it goes, but remember that the title of this chapter is “Integration in its Simplest Form.” Running differentiation backwards is not as straightforward as it first appears to be.

    Example 1.3. A Simple Integration.

    For example, can you find a formula for this integral?
    \begin{equation*} \int2t\dx{t} \end{equation*}
    In this example clearly, \(f(t)=2t\text{.}\) Thus from Definition 1.2 we see that \(F(t) = \frac{t^2}{2}\) is an antiderivative, and we can capture all of the antiderivatives by adding an arbitrary constant, traditionally called “\(C\text{,}\)” and we write
    \begin{equation*} \int 2t\dx{t} = \frac{t^2}{2} + C. \end{equation*}
    As this example shows the expression \(\int f(t)\dx{t} \) represents a multifunction like the “\(\text{arc}\)” functions we encountered when we learned to differentiate the trigonometric functions and their inverses.
    There will be times when we will want to choose only one of the functions represented by \(\int f(t)\dx{t}\text{.}\) This will consist entirely of choosing the value of \(C\) that works for our problem. None of the complex domain and range difficulties that caused do many headaches with the inverse trigonometric functions will be in play here. Since the multifunction that will come up for us are so simple, we will dispence with the formal language and call \(F(t) = \frac{t^2}{2}+C\) the most general antiderivative of \(f(t) = t^2\text{,}\) but it is really a multfunction since “\(C\)” is unspecified.
    Modern integration theory has expanded considerably beyond what we will be learning in this course. But the underlying notions of the integral as a sum and the integral as an antiderivative are still very helpful and we will rely on them rather heavily, at least at first.

    Find the most general antiderivative.
    1. \(\displaystyle \int t^2\dx{t}\)
    2. \(\displaystyle \int 3t-2\dx{t}\)
    3. \(\displaystyle \int 7\dx{t}\)
    4. \(\displaystyle \int \sin(t)\dx{t}\)
    5. \(\displaystyle \int \cos(t)\dx{t}\)
    6. \(\displaystyle \int \sec(t)\tan(t)\dx{t}\)
    7. \(\displaystyle \int e^t\dx{t}\)
    8. \(\displaystyle \int t^{-1}\dx{t}\)

    Problem 1.5.

    Show that the following statements are true.

    (a)

    \(\int 2te^{t^2}\dx{t} = e^{t^2} +C\)

    (b)

    \(\int te^t\dx{t} = (t-1)e^t+C\)

    (c)

    \(\int \sin(x)\cos(x)\dx{x} =\frac12\sin^2(x)+C\)

    (d)

    \(\int \sin(x)\cos(x)\dx{x} = -\frac12\cos^2(x)+C\)
    Notice that we’ve used \(x\) instead of \(t\) for our variable. Does that matter? Explain.

    (e)

    \(\int u\cos(u) \dx{u} = u\sin(u)+\cos(u)+C\)

    (f)

    \(\int x\ln(x) \dx{x} = x(\ln{x}-1)+C\)
