    Section 1.2 Integration as Antidifferentiation

    Definition 1.2.

    Suppose we have a function
    \begin{equation} y=F(t)\tag{1.6} \end{equation}
    whose derivative is \(\dfdx{y}{t}=f(t)\text{.}\) Then \(F(t)\) is called an antiderivative of \(f(t)\text{.}\)
    It follows immediately from the Constant Rule that a function, say \(f(t)\) has more than one antiderivative. For example if \(F_1(x) = F(x) + 5\) then from the Constant Differentiation Rule \(F_1^\prime(x) = F^\prime (x) = f(x)\text{.}\)
    And there is nothing special about \(5\text{.}\) If \(C\) is any constant and
    \begin{equation*} F_1(x)=F(x)+C \end{equation*}
    then
    \begin{equation*} F_1^\prime(x)=F^\prime(x)=f(x)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and it follows that \(F_1(x)\) is an antiderivative of \(f(x)\text{.}\) Thus, if we know that \(F(x)\) is one antiderivative of \(f(x)\text{,}\) then we know all (infinitely many) of the antiderivatives of \(f(x)\) because we get a (slightly) different antiderivative each time we add a constant to \(F(x)\text{.}\) Remember this. We will return to it when we discuss equation (1.9) below.
    We know from our study of Differential Calculus that
    \begin{equation*} \dx{y}=F^\prime(t)\dx{t}=f(t)\dx{t}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and we know from our work in Section 1.1 that
    \begin{equation} y=\int\dx{y}\text{.}\tag{1.7} \end{equation}
    Observe that since \(\dx{y} \) is a differential it follows that \(f(t)\dx{t}\) is also a differential.

    Comment.

    In practice, integration is always a summation between two points so there really should be indices on our integral sign in equation (1.7). We have discarded them for now because they would only distract from the larger point we’re about to make. We will circle back to this issue in the next DIGRESSION.
    Next starting at some fixed point \(c\) we lay all of the differentials, \(\dx{y} =f(x)\dx{x}\text{,}\) end to end, forming the partitioned interval \([c,x]\) as shown in the diagram below.
    Next we define the function,
    \begin{equation} \alpha (x)=\int_{t=c}^{t=x}f(t)\dx{t} \tag{1.8} \end{equation}
    which sums the differentials \(f(t)\dx{t}=\dx{y} \) from a fixed point \(c\) to a variable point \(x\text{.}\) There is a lot going on in equation (1.8) but it is important that you understand very clearly the nature of the function \(\alpha(x)\text{.}\) We will proceed slowly.
    Starting at the number \(c\text{,}\) \(\alpha(x)\) sums all of the differentials \(\dx{y}=f(x)\dx{x} \) up to the point \(x\text{.}\) Thus \(\alpha (x_1)\) will be the sum of the differentials up to \(x_1\text{,}\) \(\alpha (x_2)\) is the sum up to \(x_2\text{.}\) Generally, \(\alpha(x)\) is the sum of all of the differentials from \(c\) to \(x\text{,}\) so \(\alpha\) depend on the value of \(x\text{.}\) Which is to say that \(\alpha \) is a function of \(x\text{.}\)

    Drill 1.3.

    Explain why \(\alpha (c)=0\text{.}\)
    From equation (1.5) we see that \(\int_{t=c}^{t=x}\dx{y} = y(x)-y(c)\text{.}\) Therefore
    \begin{equation*} \alpha (x)=\int_{t=c}^{t=x}f(t)\dx{t}= \int_{t=c}^{t=x}\dx{y} = y(x)-y(c). \end{equation*}
    Finally, since \(y(t)=F(t)\) (see equation (1.6)) we see that
    \begin{equation} \alpha (x)=F(x)-F(c).\tag{1.9} \end{equation}
    Despite its apparent simplicity Equation (1.9) is quite profound. First, observe that \(F(c)\) is a constant. To emphasize this we set \(F(c)=C\text{,}\) so that it looks like a constant.

    A Little Algebra.

    \(F(c)\) is a constant for the same reason that if \(g(x)=x^2\) then \(g(2)\) is a constant. When we evaluate a function at a single element of its domain the result will be a single element of its range, i.e., a constant..
    With this notational change we have \(\alpha(x)+C=F(x) \text{.}\) From our observations at the begining of this section this means that
    \begin{equation*} \alpha (x)=\int_{t=c}^{t=x}f(t)\dx{t} \end{equation*}
    is also an antiderivative of \(f(x)\text{.}\)
    But equation (1.9) tells us more than that. It says that if if we know another antiderivative of \(f(x)\text{,}\) say \(F(x)\) we can find \(\alpha (x)\) by computing
    \begin{equation} \alpha (x)=\int_{t=c}^{t=x}f(t)\dx{t} = F(x)-C,\tag{1.10} \end{equation}
    Equation (1.10) is an informal statement of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus. We will now state it formally.

    Theorem 1.4. The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (FTC).

    Let \(c\) be any constant. If \(\alpha (x)\) is defined by
    \begin{equation*} \alpha (x) = \int_{t=c}^{t=x} f(t)\dx{t} \end{equation*}
    then \(\alpha(x)\) is an antiderivative of \(f(x)\text{.}\)
    In the FTC, notice where, and how, we used the variable name “\(x\)” and where we used “\(t\text{.}\)” It is important to keep the distinction clear in your mind.

    Corollary 1.5.

    From Theorem 1.4 and equation (1.9) it follows that if \(F(x)\) is any other antiderivative of \(f(x)\) then
    \begin{equation} F(x)=\alpha (x)+C.\tag{1.11} \end{equation}

    Corollary 1.6. Definite Integration.

    Let \(c\) and \(b\) be constants. Then from Theorem 1.4 and (1.10) it follows that
    \begin{equation} \int_{t=c}^{t=b} f(t)\dx{t} = F(c)-F(b).\tag{1.12} \end{equation}

    Example 1.7. We Integrate Differentials, Not Functions.

    The integral sign is intended to indicate the summation of differentials, but it is very easy to use the notation improperly. Sooner or later most people will fall into the habit of dropping the final “\(\dx{x}{}\)” and writing
    \begin{align*} \amp{}\int f(x) \amp{}\text{ instead of }\amp{}\amp{}\int f(x)\dx{x}\ \end{align*}
    and calling them both the integral of the function \(f(x)\text{.}\) This is an unfortunate, but common, abuse of the notation. We strongly suggest that you take care to avoid this bad habit for as long as you can. Especially when the concepts are still new it can be very confusing.

    Mathematical Customs.

    In practice, if they are not relevant to the discussion at hand the indices are frequently left out entirely.
    Remember that
    \begin{equation*} \int f(x) \end{equation*}
    is meaningless no matter how convenient it might be. But the meaning of
    \begin{equation*} \int f(x)\dx{x} \end{equation*}
    is clear: We’re summing up all of the differentials of the form \(f(x)\dx{x} \text{.}\)
    Suppose that \(y=x^2\) then \(\dx{y} =2x\dx{x}\text{.}\) Then \(\int 2x\dx{x}\) makes sense because the product \(2x\dx{x}=\dx{y}\) is a differential. But \(\int 2x\) is nonsense because \(2x\) alone is not a differential.
    In particular, \(\int 2x\) is meaningless. However if \(y=x^2\) then what is probably meant is
    \begin{equation} \int 2x\dx{x} = \int \dx{y}\tag{1.13} \end{equation}
    which is equal to \(y\) since \(\int \dx{y}\) means “add up the differentials of \(y\text{.}\)”

    Example 1.8. The Problem of Zero Differentials.

    Naturally, things are not quite this simple. Consider
    \begin{align*} f_1(x)\amp{}=x^2+7 \text{ and}\\ f_2(x)\amp{}=x^2+4. \end{align*}
    Clearly \(\dx{f_1}=\dx{f_2}=2x\dx{x}\) so the sum of \(\dx{f_1}\) and the sum of \(\dx{f_2}\) would seem to be the same as. But clearly
    \begin{equation*} \text{ the sum of the}\dx{y_1}=x^2+7 \end{equation*}
    whereas
    \begin{equation*} \text{ the sum of the}\dx{y_2}=x^2+5\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This apparent contradiction needs to be resolved.
    One way to resolve it is to form the function
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=y_1(x)-y_2(x) \end{equation*}
    and notice that since \(\dx{y_1}=\dx{y_2} \) it follows that
    \begin{equation*} \dx(y_1-y_2)=\dx{y_1}-\dx{y_2}=0. \end{equation*}
    Dividing by \(\dx{x}\) and thinking of \(\dfdx{y_1}{x} \) and \(\dfdx{y_2}{x} \) as slopes, we have
    \begin{equation*} \dfdx{f}{x}= \dfdx{y_1}{x} -\dfdx{y_2}{x} =\dfdx{(y_1-y_2)}{x}=0\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This says that the curve \(f(x) = y_1(x) - y_2(x)\) has a slope which is constantly equal to zero. A moment’s thought will suggest that this curve must be a horizontal line for if not then somewhere it should have a nonzero slope. This argument can be made rigorous, but we will not fuss over that right now.
