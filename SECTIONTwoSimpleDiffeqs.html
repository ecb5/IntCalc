    Section 2.1 Differential Equations

    Sometimes the terminology we invent in mathematics is very simple minded. For example, equations which have differentials in them are called differential equations, and this has been true from the beginnning. As we observed in [cross-reference to target(s) "EXAMPLEDifferentialEquation" missing or not unique] every time you differentiate some algebraic equation, you create a differential equation. For example, suppose we have
    \begin{equation*} y=3x^2+2x-7\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Differentiating yields the differential equation
    \begin{equation} \dx{y}=6x\dx{x}+2\dx{x}=(6x+3)\dx{x}\text{.}\tag{2.1} \end{equation}
    Probably a better name would be “derivative equation” since the differentials involved can always be rearranged until we have differential ratios, or derivatives. Observe that equation (2.1) can be rearranged as
    \begin{equation*} \dfdx{y}{x} =(6x+3)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Many natural phenomena can be modeled by a differential equation. You’ve already seen this a couple of times. For example, you saw in [cross-reference to target(s) "PROBLEMSpinCast1" missing or not unique] that the differential equation
    \begin{equation} \dfdx{y}{x} =\frac{\omega^2}{g}x\tag{2.2} \end{equation}
    models the force on a spinning vat of liquid glass. By recognizing that the parabola \(y=\frac{{\omega }^2}{2g}x^2\) is one solution of equation (2.2) we concluded that forming a telescope lens by spin casting would guarantee a parabolic shape.
    We obtained our solution in [cross-reference to target(s) "PROBLEMSpinCast1" missing or not unique], essentially, by guessing. As we’ve said before guessing is an important tool and we encourage you to use it. But, as we’ve also said, it is not enough to guess and move on if we happen to make a correct guess. We need to stop and examine the intuition that gave us this guess.
    In particular in this case, we want to recast this problem in integral form so we can use the more descriptive, and helpful, notation of integration. We can rewrite equation (2.2) as
    \begin{align*} \dx{y}\amp{}=\frac{{\omega}^2}{g}x\dx{x}\\ \end{align*}

    or

    \begin{align*} y=\int\dx{y}\amp{}=\int\frac{{\omega}^2}{g}x\dx{x} \end{align*}
    As you can see this is the same problem — we need to find an antiderivative of \(\frac{{\omega}^2}{g}x\) — but the integral notation clearly suggests what we need to do whereas differential notation was less clear. Intuition is useful and important but it will only take us so far. We can attack a wider variety of problems if we have a standard methodology and a clear notation.

    Problem 2.1.0.1.

    Use the rules of integration to finish solving equation (2.2). Notice that when you integrate, you will have the arbitrary constant \(C\) in your answer. We will determine this constant later. Can you guess what it must be and what factors will determine it’s value?
    Again, the point is that integration gives us a more systematic way of solving differential equations. The solution strategy we used when we rewrote equation (2.2) is called separation of variables and it will enable us to solve a great many simple differential equations.
    To see this consider the differential equation
    \begin{equation} \dfdx{y}{x} =xy\text{.}\tag{2.3} \end{equation}

    Drill 2.1.0.2.

    Before we continue see if you can come up with a solution of equation (2.3) on your own. Notice how it is similar to, and different from, [cross-reference to target(s) "IVPExpGeneral" missing or not unique]. Does that help?

    Example 2.1.0.3. Separation of Variables.

    To find a way to approach this problem we will take our cue from basic Algebra. Suppose we want to solve the algebraic equation
    \begin{equation} y^3=xy\tag{2.4} \end{equation}
    for \(y\text{.}\) One obvious solution is \(y=0\text{.}\) This is a valid solution, but it is uninteresting, so we will not consider it. Assuming that \(y\neq 0\text{,}\) we can divide by \(y\) obtaining
    \begin{equation*} y^2=x \end{equation*}
    Once the variables have been separated like this, we can take the square root of each side and obtain \(y=\pm \sqrt{x}\text{.}\)
    Notice that it was helpful to put all of the “\(y\)” variables on one side, and all of the “\(x\)” variables on the other. It was not immediately clear that “separating the variables” would be helpful but once it was done, the next step was clear.
    Similarly it is not clear that separating the variable in equation (2.4) will help, but let’s try it and see what happens. Separating the variables, we see that
    \begin{equation*} \dfdx{y}{x}=x\dx{x} \end{equation*}
    which says that the differentials on either side of the equation are the same. Integrating (summing) both sides gives
    \begin{equation} \int{\frac{1}{y}} \dx{y}=\int{x\dx{x}}\text{.}\tag{2.5} \end{equation}

    Problem 2.1.0.4.

    (a)

    Integrate both sides of equation (2.5) and then show that \(y\) must satisfy
    \begin{equation*} \ln \abs{y}+C_1=\frac{x^2}{2}+C_2 \end{equation*}
    where \(C_1,\ C_2\) are arbitrary constants.

    (b)

    Show that
    \begin{equation*} y=\pm \left(e^{C_2-C_1}\right)e^{\frac{x^2}{2}} \end{equation*}
    Since \(C_1,\ C_2\) are arbitrary constants, it follows that \(e^{C_2-C_1}\) is also an arbitrary constant. (Why?) For simplicity we will set \(A=e^{C_2-C_1}\text{,}\) so \(y=\pm Ae^{\frac{x^2}{2}}\) provides the general solution for the original differential equation.
    Separation of variable is not always possible in either an Algebra or a Calculus setting. For example, try separating the variables in \(y=\sin(xy)\) or \(\dfdx{y}{x}\text{.}\)
    Even when it can be done it is not always helpful For example, suppose we had the algebraic equation
    \begin{equation*} y^3=x+y \end{equation*}
    Separating the variables gives \(y^3-y=x\text{,}\) but simply taking the cube root of both sides won’t work. Try it and see. It would take an application of the Cubic Formula which is not so trivial. The Cubic Formula is like the Quadratic Formula, only more fun.
    Similarly, try as you might you can not separate the variables in the differential equation
    \begin{equation*} \dfdx{y}{x} =x+y\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Try it and see.
    There are whole books and college courses (undergraduate and graduate) on the solution of various kinds of differential equations, and it is still an active area of mathematical research. But by and large, in this book we will only see separable differentiable equations. But even that takes work. For example,

    Example 2.1.0.5. A Hard Differential Equation.

    In [cross-reference to target(s) "PROBLEMHangingChain" missing or not unique] of [cross-reference to target(s) "SECTIONhyperb-trig-hang" missing or not unique] we saw that if \(y(x)\) is the shape that a hanging chain naturally settles into under the pull of gravity then \(y(x)\) will necessarily be a solution of the second order differential equation:
    \begin{equation} \dfdxn{y}{x}{2} = \frac{w}{H}\sqrt{1+\left(\dfdx{y}{x}\right)^2}.\tag{2.6} \end{equation}
    which is a model for the Hanging Chain problem. That is, the graph of a function which solves equation (2.6) will be the shape of a hanging chain.
    It is one thing to confirm a given solution, as you did in [cross-reference to target(s) "PROBLEMHangingChain" missing or not unique], but finding the solution in the first place is not at all a simple task. A start might be to let \(z=\dfdx{y}{x}\text{,}\) so we have
    \begin{align} \dfdx{z}{x}\amp=\frac{w}{H}\sqrt{1+z^2}\notag\\ \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+z^2}}\dx{z}\amp =\frac{w}{H}\dx{x}\text{.}\tag{2.7} \end{align}
    Adding all of the differentials on both sides of equation (2.7), we see that
    \begin{equation} \int\frac{1}{\sqrt{1+z^2}}\dx{z}=\int\frac{w}{H}\dx{x}\text{.}\tag{2.8} \end{equation}
    As you can see, this is separable, and we can certainly integrate the right-hand side of equation (2.8). When we do that we get
    \begin{equation*} \int{\frac{w}{H}dx}=\frac{w}{H}x+C \end{equation*}
    .
    We still need to determine the arbitrary constant \(C\text{,}\) but that is the least of our worries because nothing in our table of integrals from Section 1.2 quite matches the right hand side of equation (2.8) We’ll need to develop more integration techniques before we can show that
    \begin{equation*} y=\frac{H}{2w}\left(e^{\frac{wx}{H}}+e^{-\frac{wx}{H}}\right) \end{equation*}
    is the solution ofequation (2.8). For now, let’s get grounded with a topic we examined in [cross-reference to target(s) "SECTIONGalileoDrops" missing or not unique]. This time we will look at it from our integration perspective.
