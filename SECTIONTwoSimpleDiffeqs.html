    Section 1.2 Separation of Variables

    A very useful strategy for solving differential equations (when it works) is called separation of variables and it will enable us to solve a great many simple differential equations.
    Separating variables is not a new idea. You’ve been using it all of your life. This is how you were taught to solve algebraic equations. For example, suppose we want to solve the algebraic equation
    \begin{equation} y^2-x^2y - 6y=0\tag{1.6} \end{equation}
    for \(y\text{.}\) When \(y=0\) the equation is true so the set of all points with coordinates \((x,y)=(x,0)\) are valid solutions. But they are not all of the solutions (and they are not interesting), so we will look for others. Assuming that \(y\neq 0\text{,}\) we can divide by \(y\) obtaining
    \begin{equation*} y-x^2-6=0 \end{equation*}
    Next we separate the variables by rearranging the terms algebraically so that all of the terms involving \(y\) are on on one side, and all of the terms involving \(x\) (or involving neither \(x\) nor \(y\)) on the other
    \begin{equation*} y=x^2+6\text{.} \end{equation*}
    So the set of all points with coordinates \((x,x^2+6)\) are the remaining solutions of equation (1.6).
    To see this in the context of a differential equation, consider
    \begin{equation} \dfdx{y}{x} =xy\text{.}\tag{1.7} \end{equation}
    Before we continue see if you can come up with a solution of equation (1.7) on your own.
    Separating the variables we have
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\dx{y}}{y}=x\dx{x}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Integrating both side gives
    \begin{equation} \ln\abs{y} +C_1= \frac{x^2}{2}+C_2\tag{1.8} \end{equation}
    where \(C_1\) and \(C_2\) are the integration constants arising from the integrations.

    Problem 1.2.0.1.

    (a)

    Explain why the formula
    \begin{equation} \ln\abs{y} = \frac{x^2}{2}+C\tag{1.9} \end{equation}
    is equivalent to equation (1.8) .

    (b)

    Solve equation (1.8) for \(y(x)\text{.}\)
    Hint.
    Igore the absolute value. It doesn’t matter in this problem. We will talk more about this later.
    It is not immediately clear that separating the variables in equation (1.7) will help, but let’s try it and see what happens. Separating the variables, we see that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\dx{y}}{y}=x\dx{x} \end{equation*}
    which says that the differentials on either side of the equation are the same. Integrating (summing) both sides gives
    \begin{equation} \int{\frac{1}{y}} \dx{y}=\int{x\dx{x}}\text{.}\tag{1.10} \end{equation}

    Problem 1.2.0.2.

    (a)

    Integrate both sides of equation (1.10) and then show that \(y\) must satisfy
    \begin{equation*} \ln \abs{y}+C_1=\frac{x^2}{2}+C_2 \end{equation*}
    where \(C_1,\ C_2\) are arbitrary constants.

    (b)

    Show that
    \begin{equation*} y=\pm \left(e^{C_2-C_1}\right)e^{\frac{x^2}{2}}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since \(C_1,\ C_2\) are arbitrary constants, it follows that \(e^{C_2-C_1}\) is also an arbitrary constant. (Why?) For simplicity we will set \(A=e^{C_2-C_1}\text{,}\) so \(y=\pm Ae^{\frac{x^2}{2}}\) provides the general solution for the original differential equation.
    It is not always possible to separate variables. For example, try separating the variables in \(y=\sin(xy)\) or \(\dfdx{y}{x}=\cos(xy)\text{.}\) You won’t get far.
    Even when it is possible it is not always helpful. For example, suppose we had the algebraic equation
    \begin{equation*} y^3=x+y\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Separating the variables gives \(y^3-y=x\text{,}\) but it would take an application of the Cubic Formula which is not so trivial.

    Comment.

    The Cubic Formula is like the Quadratic Formula, only more fun.
    There are whole books and college courses, at both the undergraduate and graduate levels, on the solution of various kinds of differential equations, and it is still an active area of mathematical research. Separable differential equations are among the simplest to solve but even these can be quite challenging at times.

    Example 1.2.0.5.

    We spent a great deal of effort in [cross-reference to target(s) "SECTIONExponentialGrowth" missing or not unique] analyzing the equation
    \begin{equation} \dfdx{y}{t} = Ky\tag{1.11} \end{equation}
    for various values of \(K\text{.}\) But in that section our efforts were based on guessing the form of the solution. We no longer need to guess because equation (1.11) is a separable equation and we can solve it algorithmically. To keep things simple for now we’ll take \(K=1\text{.}\) That is, we want to solve
    \begin{equation*} \dfdx{y}{t}=y \text{.} \end{equation*}
    First we separate the variables
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\dx{y}}{y}=\dx{t}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    then we integrate both sides, giving
    \begin{equation*} \ln\abs{y} = t + C\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Solving for \(\abs{y}\) next we get
    \begin{equation*} \abs{y} = Ke^t \end{equation*}
    where \(K=e^C\text{.}\) Finally, since this is a model of a population and populations are never negative we see that the absolute value bars are superfluous here so
    \begin{equation*} y(t) = Ke^t \end{equation*}
    where \(K=e^C\text{,}\) just as we saw in [cross-reference to target(s) "SECTIONExponentialGrowth" missing or not unique].

    Example 1.2.0.7. The Hanging Chain, again.

    In [cross-reference to target(s) "PROBLEMHangingChain" missing or not unique] of [cross-reference to target(s) "SECTIONhyperb-trig-hang" missing or not unique] we saw that if the graph of \(y(x)\) is the shape that a hanging chain settles into naturally under the pull of gravity then \(y(x)\) will necessarily be a solution of the second order differential equation:
    \begin{equation} \dfdxn{y}{x}{2} = \frac{w}{H}\sqrt{1+\left(\dfdx{y}{x}\right)^2}.\tag{1.12} \end{equation}
    which is a model for the Hanging Chain problem. That is, the graph of a function, \(y(x)\text{,}\) which solves equation (1.12) will be the shape of a hanging chain. In particular note that \(y(x)\) represents the vertical position of the chain at the point \(x\text{.}\)
    It is one thing to confirm a given solution, as you did in [cross-reference to target(s) "PROBLEMHangingChain" missing or not unique], but as you’ve seen finding the solution in the first place is not always a simple task. We can’t actually derive the solution of this equation yet, but we have developed enough tools to get started.
    First we let \(z=\dfdx{y}{x}\text{,}\) so that
    \begin{equation} \dfdx{z}{x}=\frac{w}{H}\sqrt{1+z^2}.\tag{1.13} \end{equation}
    Separating the variables we see that
    \begin{equation} \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+z^2}}\dx{z} =\frac{w}{H}\dx{x}\text{.}\tag{1.14} \end{equation}
    Integrating both sides of equation (1.14), we see that
    \begin{equation} \int\frac{1}{\sqrt{1+z^2}}\dx{z}=\int\frac{w}{H}\dx{x} = \frac{\omega}{H}x+C\text{.}\tag{1.15} \end{equation}
    Unfortunately nothing in our table of integrals quite matches the left hand side of equation (1.15) so we can’t take this problem any further right now. We’ll return to it after we have developed more integration techniques.
    However we can confirm the solution if it is already known. We did this in [cross-reference to target(s) "PROBLEMHangingChain" missing or not unique].

    Problem 1.2.0.9. A Homogeneous Equation.

    Consider the differential equation
    \begin{equation} y^2\dfdx{y}{t}=2ty+y^2 \text{.}\tag{1.16} \end{equation}
    As stated this is not a separable equation nor is there any other obvious way to find \(y(t)\text{.}\) But with a little cleverness we can turn it into a separable equation.

    (a)

    Show that if \(y=ut\) then \(\dfdx{y}{t} = u+t\dfdx{u}{t}\text{.}\)
    This substitution is the “cleverness” we referred to above. The rest is computation.

    (b)

    Use the substitution from part (a) to reduce equation (1.16) to
    \begin{equation*} t\dfdx{u}{t} = u+u^2 \end{equation*}
    which is separable.

    (c)

    Now separate the variables in the equation in part (b) and integrate to obtain
    \begin{equation*} \int \frac{1}{u(u+1)}\dx{u} =\ln\abs{t}+C \end{equation*}

    (d)

    Show that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{u(u+1)}=\frac1u-\frac{1}{u+1} \end{equation*}
    and use that knowledge to compute the integral in part (c).

    (e)

    Finally show that
    \begin{equation*} y(t)=\frac{\kappa t^2}{1-\kappa t} \end{equation*}
    where \(\kappa{}\) is an arbitrary constant.
    Hint.
    Unlike parts (a) through (d) there is more than one step to complete part (e). Several more, in fact. Proceed carefully and check your work.
    It should be clear to you that the substitution \(y=ut\) is what made this solution possible. Without that we had no place to begin. However, once the substitution was made the path ahead was clear.
    But as you’ve just seen even when the path ahead is clear, computing the solution of a differential equation can be a tedious and painstaking process. Proceed slowly, check your work frequently, and be careful.
    Equation (1.16) is an example of a homogeneous equation. Unfortunately it investigating this idea any further would take us too far afield so we won’t. See if you can make up other differential equations where that substitution will also work. Then look at what they have in common. You just might be able to work out the meaning of “homogeneous” in this context on your own.

    DIGRESSION: How Does Anyone Think of These Things?

    Although the substitution we suggested at the beginning of Problem 1.2.0.9 worked it was almost certainly not clear that it would work until it finally did. We (the authors) do not know who first tried to make such a substitution or why, but we believe that it was probably just a guess, at first.
    This is the way mathematics works. Indeed, it is the way problem–solving of any kind works. If you don’t know what else to do you take your best guess and use that to learn more about the problem. We discussed this in [cross-reference to target(s) "DIGRESSIONGuessingEffectively" missing or not unique].
    Let’s take another look at equation (1.16) and see how this process works. First, we imagine that whoever first solved this equation had first tried many other ideas and guesses which did not work before finally hitting on the idea of substituting \(y=ut\text{.}\)
