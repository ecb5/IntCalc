    Section 25.1 Limits of Integration

    Starting at some fixed point \(c\) we lay all of the differentials, \(\dx{y} =f(t)\dx{t}\text{,}\) end to end, forming the partitioned interval \([c,x]\) as shown in the diagram below.

    Comment.

    In practice, integration is always a summation between two points so there really should be indices on our integral sign . We have discarded them for now because they would only distract from the larger point we’re about to make. We will circle back to this issue in the next DIGRESSION.
    In the early nineteenth century Joseph Fourier
     1 
    https://mathshistory.st-andrews.ac.uk/Biographies/Fourier/
    (1768–1830) found that Leibniz’ original integral symbol was not quite descriptive enough for his purposes. In particular the expression \(\int{\dx{x}}\) gives no clue where we need to start, or stop, summing the differentials. To clarify things Fourier modified Leibniz’ notation by inserting upper and lower indices on the integral sign as follows:
    \begin{equation} \int_{x_0}^{x_n}\dx{x}=x_n-x_0\text{.}\tag{25.1} \end{equation}
    equation (25.1) is equivalent to equation (20.2) but it has the advantage that it clearly indicates the limits of integration. We start summing the differentials at \(x_0\text{,}\) and end at \(x_n\text{,}\) resulting in the difference \(x_n-x_0\text{.}\)
    Summation of differentials is called integration. An integral consists of two parts: The integral sign, \(\int \text{(with or without indices)}\text{,}\) which indicates summation, and the differentials to be summed — represented by \(\dx{y}\) in this case.
