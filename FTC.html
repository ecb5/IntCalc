    Chapter 1 The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

    Suppose a point \(P\) begins at \(x_0\) and traces out a line segment of some unknown length, \(x\text{.}\) We partition the line segment between \(x_0\) and \(x\) into subintervals as seen in figure . . . If the symbol \(\Delta x_1\) represents the first subinterval of \(x\text{,}\) \(\Delta x_2\) represents the second subinterval, and in general if \(\Delta{x_i}\) represents the \(i\)th subinterval of \(x\) then clearly the length of the line segment is
    \begin{gather*} \Delta{x_1}+\Delta{x_2}+\Delta{x_3} +\cdots +\Delta{x_{n-1}}+\Delta{x_n}\\ = (x_1-x_0)+(x_2-x_2)+(x_3-x_2) +\cdots + (x_{n-1}-x_{n-2})+(x_n-x_{n-1})\\ =x_n-x_0 \end{gather*}
    If the subintervals are all the same length, say \(\Delta{x}\text{,}\) then we no longer need the subscripts to distinguish them. This makes our notation a little less cumbersome, as follows:
    \begin{equation} \underbrace{\Delta{x}+\Delta{x}+\cdots{}+\Delta{x}}_{\text{n copies}}=x_n-x_0\tag{1.1} \end{equation}
    But it is silly to write "\(+\Delta{x}\)" repeatedly so we define \(\Delta{x}+\Delta{x}+\cdots{}+\Delta{x}\) to be \(\int{}\Delta{x}\) and write
    \begin{equation*} \int{}\Delta{x}=x_n-x_0 \end{equation*}
    instead. The "\(\int{}\) symbol is called an integral sign.
    We assume, with Leibniz, that equation equation (1.1) remains true when the finite differences, \(\Delta{x}\) are replaced with infinitesimal differences:
    \begin{equation*} \int{\dx{x}{}}=x_n-x_0. \end{equation*}
    Leibniz invented the integral sign in the seventeenth century. It is often described as an elongated S, but in those days a printed S was elongated compared to other letters. The integral sign is really just the letter S. Leibniz chose this symbol to indicate addition because it is the first letter of the Latin word "summa," meaning total.
    In any case the statement \(\int\dx{x}=x_n-x_0\) says that if we add up the lengths of all of the differentials that make up the line segment from \(x_0\) to \(x_n\) we will get its length: \(x_n-x_0\text{.}\)
    This is surely not surprising and you are probably wondering why we taken the trouble to wrap such an obvious statement in this obscure notation. The reason is this: Leibniz’ integral notation has proven over time to be extremely versatile and expressive.

    Note to self.

    Confirm that it was Fourier who put the indices on integrals.
    In the early nineteenth century Joseph Fourier found that Leibniz’ original integral symbol was not quite descriptive enough for his purposes. In particular since we’ve dropped the subscripts the expression \(\int{\dx{x}}\) gives no clue where we need to start, and stop, summing the differentials. To clarify Fourier modified Leibniz’ notation by inserting both a subscript and a superscript as follows:
    \begin{equation} \int_{x_0}^{x_n}\dx{x}=x_n-x_0\text{.}\tag{1.2} \end{equation}
    Equation (1.2) says that we begin summing the differentials at \(x_0\text{,}\) and end at \(x_n\text{,}\) resulting in the difference \(x_n-x_0\text{.}\)
