    Section 19.3 Running the Product Rule Backwards: Integration By Parts

    Example 19.3.0.1. Population Dynamics: A Non–Separable Differential Equation.

    Recall that in Example 18.2.0.5 we solved the exponential growth equation
    \begin{equation*} \dfdx{y}{t}=Ky \end{equation*}
    by separating the variables and then integrating. For reasons that will become clear shortly we re–express this equation as
    \begin{equation} \dfdx{y}{t}-Ky =0\tag{19.58} \end{equation}
    .
    🔗
    Equation (19.58) assumes that the relative growth rate, \(K\) in this case, is constant. But that is almost never true. All biological processes including reproduction tend to slow down when the ambient temperature is cooler and to speed up when it is warmer.
    🔗
    So suppose that we have a population of algae growing in a pond. At night when the temperature is cooler the rate of growth will slow and during the daytime it will speed up. To simplify our demonstration we’ll suppose that the “speeding up and slowing down” takes the form of a cosine wave, \(\cos(t) \text{.}\) This means that the rate of change of our algae population will be
    \begin{equation*} \dfdx{y}{t}-Ky=\cos (t)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    To complete our model we’ll assume that initally there are \(20\) kilograms of algae in the pond. Thus to find the population as a function of time we need to solve the IVP
    \begin{align} \dfdx{y}{t} - Ky \amp{}=\cos(t),\amp{} y(0)=20.\tag{19.59} \end{align}
    🔗
    The differential equation part of IVP (19.59) is not separable but it is very similar to equation (19.58) whose solution
    \begin{equation} y(t)=Ae^{Kt}\tag{19.60} \end{equation}
    is known. Since the equations are similar it seems reasonable to suppose that their solutions will also be similar so the question is, “How do we tweak solution (19.60)) so as to obtain a solution of IVP (19.59)?”
    🔗
    Observe that \(y(t)=Ae^{Kt}\) is the product of the constant \(A\) and the function \(e^{Kt}\text{,}\) One possibility is to suppose that the solution of IVP (19.59) is also a product. But since this is a slightly more complex problem than equation (19.58) we’ll need a slightly more complex product. So let’s guess that
    \begin{equation*} \bar{y}(t)=A(t)\cdot e^{Kt} \end{equation*}
    where \(A(t)\) is a differentiable function rather than a constant.
    🔗

    Aside: Comment.

    We have called this a guess because ultimately, that’s all it really is. As such it is simply a trick. But this particular “trick” turns out to be succesful in more than one context so it has been elevated to the status of “technique” and given the name “Variation of Parameters.” Notice that it consists entirely of allowing the (constant) parameter \(A\) to vary (be variable).
    🔗
    Having made our guess we next try it in IVP (19.59) and see what happens. From the Product Rule we see that
    \begin{equation*} \dfdx{\bar{y}}{t}=A(t)Ke^{Kt}+\dfdx{A}{t}e^{Kt}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Putting this into our differential equation in IVP (19.59) we have
    \begin{align*} \underbrace{\textcolor{red}{AKe^{Kt}}+\dfdx{A}{t}e^{Kt}}_{=\dfdx{\bar{y}}{t}}-\underbrace{\textcolor{red}{KA(t)e^{Kt}}}_{=K\bar{y}}\amp{}=\cos(t)\\ \dfdx{A}{t}e^{Kt}\amp{}=\cos{t}. \end{align*}
    So apparently all we have left to do is compute the integral
    \begin{equation} A(t) = \int e^{-Kt}\cos (t)\dx{t}\tag{19.61} \end{equation}
    to find \(A(t)\) and the solution of (19.59) will be
    \begin{equation*} \bar{y}=A(t)e^{Kt}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Unfortunately this is well beyond our ability at the moment so we will back up a bit and look at some slightly simpler integrals first.
    🔗
    🔗
    Consider the integral
    \begin{equation} \int x\cos(x)\dx{x} \text{.}\tag{19.62} \end{equation}
    Try as you might you will find that no substitution you can find will work here. Nor will the Partial Fraction Decomposition help.
    🔗

    Aside: Comment.

    Actually, we should be more careful. There is always a substitution that will work. For example, the substitution
    \begin{equation*} u=x\sin(x) +\cos (x) \end{equation*}
    will work here.
    🔗
    The question is, can we find it? How do you think we found this one?
    🔗

    DIGRESSION: How Not to Integrate a Product.

    In the beginning a common error is to assume that we need only integrate the two factors separately. If we do that with formula (19.62) we’ll get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x^2}{2}\sin(x) +C\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This is not correct as we can easily verify by differentiation. In fact it is not generally true that
    \begin{equation} \int f(x)\cdot g(x)\dx{x}=\left(\int f(x)\dx{x}\right)\cdot \left(\int g(x) \dx{x}\right) \text{.}\tag{19.63} \end{equation}
    Of course, we already knew that
    \begin{equation*} \left(f\cdot g\right)^\prime(x) \neq f^\prime(x)\cdot g^\prime(x) \text{,} \end{equation*}
    so on second thought it may seem like it was silly to assume that equation (19.64) is valid. But it was not. It is actually quite a natural assumption to make. But assumptions can be treacherous as we’ve seen. They need to be examined closely. In this case our assumption comes up short as we have just seen.
    🔗
    But it is not silly, it is just wrong.
    🔗
    So when the integrand is the product of two functions we need a new idea.
    🔗

    Problem 19.3.0.2. When the Derivative of a Product is the Product of the Derivatives.

    Before we leave this topic behind entirely here is a curious fact. There are certain special pairs of functions for which it is true that
    \begin{equation} \left(f\cdot g\right)^\prime(x) = f^\prime(x)\cdot g^\prime(x) \text{.}\tag{19.64} \end{equation}
    In this problem we will find a few examples of such pairs.
    🔗
    (a)
    Suppose \(f^\prime(x)\neq{}f(x)\text{.}\) Show that if \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) satisfy equation (19.64) then
    \begin{equation} g(x)=\exp\left(\int\frac{1}{1-\frac{f(x)}{f^\prime (x)}}\dx{x}\right)\text{.}\tag{19.65} \end{equation}
    🔗
    Why did we require that \(f^\prime(x)\neq{}f(x)\text{?}\) Are any other constraints on the functions needed?
    🔗
    🔗
    (b)
    Explain how you can tell that
    \begin{equation} f(x)=\exp\left(\int\frac{1}{1-\frac{g(x)}{g^\prime (x)}}\dx{x}\right)\tag{19.66} \end{equation}
    is also true. Is it necessary to repeat the computations you did in part (a)?
    🔗
    🔗
    (c)
    For each of these pairs of functions either use equation (19.65) to derive \(g(x)\) from \(f(x)\) or use equation (19.66) to derive \(f(x)\) from \(g(x)\text{.}\) Assume that \(\alpha{}\) is an arbitrary constant.
    1. \(f(x)=e^{\alpha x}\text{,}\) \(g(x)=e^{\frac{\alpha }{\alpha -1}x}\) \(\alpha{}\neq1\text{.}\)
      🔗
    2. \(f(x)=\left(\frac{\alpha}{\alpha -x}\right)^\alpha\text{,}\) \(g(x)= x^\alpha\)
      🔗
    3. \(f(x) = x+\alpha \text{,}\) \(g(x)=\frac{1}{(1-\alpha )-x}\)
      🔗
    4. \(f(x)=xe^x\text{,}\) \(g(x)=e^{\frac{x^2}{2}+x} \)
      🔗
    5. \(f(x)=e^{\frac{x}{2}}\sqrt{\frac{\sec(x) }{1-\tan(x) }}\text{,}\)\(g(x)=\sin(x) \)
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    (d)
    For each pair of functions in part (c) confirm that they satisfy equation (19.64) by computing the derivatives and comparing them
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    END OF DIGRESSION
    🔗
    🔗
    In Table 18.1.0.4 we listed the integration rules corresponding to the Constant Rule (#1), the Sum Rule (#2), the Constant Multiple Rule (#3), and Power Rule (#4), but not the Product Rule?
    🔗

    Aside: Comment.

    We didn’t give an integration rule corresponding to the Quotient Rule either. But we don’t need that one since the Quotient Rule can be thought of as a rearrangement of the Product Rule.
    🔗
    It is time to fill in that blank so we will try to “run the Power Rule backward,” if we can. In differential form Product Rule formula is
    \begin{equation*} \dx(uv)=v\dx{u}+u\dx{v}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    To run this backwards we integrate both sides giving
    \begin{equation*} \int \dx(uv)=\int v\dx{u}+\int u\dx{v}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    which is equivalent to
    \begin{equation} \int u\dx{v}=\int \dx(uv)-\int v\dx{u}\text{.}\tag{19.67} \end{equation}
    Observing that \(\int \dx(uv)=uv\text{,}\) yields the Integration by Parts formula (in differential form):
    \begin{equation*} \int{}u\dx{v} =uv-\int{}v\dx{u}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Aside: Comment.

    Strictly speaking \(\int \dx(uv)=uv\) plus an arbitrary constant. We are ignoring the constant for reasons that will be clear soon.
    🔗

    Example 19.3.0.3.

    To integrate \(\int x\cos (x)\dx{x}\) by parts we set
    \begin{align*} u=x\amp{}\amp{}\text{ and } \amp{}\amp{} \dx{v}=\cos(x)\dx{x}\\ \end{align*}
    so that
    \begin{align*} \dx{u}=\dx{x} \amp{}\amp{}\text{ and } \amp{}\amp{} v=\int\cos(x)\dx{x} = \sin(x). \end{align*}
    Making these substitutions gives
    \begin{align*} \int x\cos(x)\dx{x} = \int u\dx{v}\amp{}=uv-\int v\dx{u}\\ \amp{}=x\sin(x) -\int \sin (x)\dx{x}.\\ \amp{}=x\sin(x) + \cos (x). \end{align*}
    and, finally
    \begin{equation} \int x\cos(x)\dx{x} = x\sin(x) + \cos (x) +C\tag{19.68} \end{equation}
    🔗
    🔗
    It is worth taking a moment to notice a few facts about Integration by Parts.
    Integration by Parts is Not a Substitution.
    🔗
    After \(u\) and \(\dx{v}\) are chosen we do not get an integral in terms of \(u\) and \(v\) which we then need to transform back into an integral in terms of \(x\text{,}\) the way we did with Integration by Substitution. The variables \(u\) and \(v\) were introduced just to keep track of the Integration by Parts formula. The resulting integral will be in terms of the original variable.
    🔗
    In Choosing \(u\) and \(\dx{v}\) We Used Up the Entire Integrand and the Differential.
    🔗
    That is, in Example 19.3.0.3 the expression \(u\dx{v}\) is exactly equal to \(x\cos(x)\dx{x}\text{.}\) Nothing was left out except the integral sign, \(\int{}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    As a practical matter this means that once you have decided what to call \(u\) then \(\dx{v}\) is everything else to the right of the \(\int \) symbol.
    🔗
    Integration By Parts Does Not Compute the Integral.
    🔗
    Rather, it replaces that integral with another, hopefully one that is easier to compute. (This is actually true of all of the integration techniques. They do not compute the integral, they just to replace it with another integral. Hopefully, one that is easier to compute.)
    🔗
    In Example 19.3.0.3, for instance, we could have let \(u=\cos(x){}\) and \(\dx{u}=x\dx{x}\text{.}\) This would give \(v=x^2/2\text{.}\) Substituting these into \(\int x\cos(x) \dx{x}\) we get
    \begin{equation*} \int x\cos(x)\dx{x}=\frac{x^2}{2}\cos(x)+\frac{1}{2}\int x^2\sin(x)\dx{x}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Everything we’ve done is valid, but the integral on the right is actually harder to evaluate than the one we started with.
    🔗
    What this means in practice is that as long as the integration by parts technique is applied correctly, it is not wrong in any absolute sense. But it might not be very helpful in computing the integral in front of us. So if your first attempt to compute an integral doesn’t seem to be going anywhere step back and ask yourself if another choice of \(u\) and \(\dx{v}\text{,}\) or even a different integration technique might work better.

    🔗

    Theorem 19.3.0.5. Integration by Parts.

    • Differential Form:
      \begin{equation} \int{}u\dx{v} =uv-\int{}v\dx{u}\tag{19.69} \end{equation}
      .
      🔗
      🔗
    • Functional Form:
      \begin{equation} \int{}f(x)g^\prime(x)\dx{x} = f(x)\cdot g(x)-\int g(x)f^\prime(x) \dx{x}\tag{19.70} \end{equation}
      .

    Drill 19.3.0.10.

    As we saw in Problem 19.3.0.9 sometimes we have to use the same integration technique more than once. If you perform integration by parts twice, be sure not to switch the roles of “\(u\)” and “\(v\text{.}\)” This is not incorrect, but the second integration will simply undo the first integration and circle back to where you started.
    🔗
    To see what we mean integrate \(\int x^2\sin(x) \dx{x}\) by parts twice. The first time let \(u=x^2\) and \(\dx{v}=\sin(x)\dx{x}\) and the second time let \(u=\cos(x)\) and \(\dx{v}=x\dx{x}\) to confirm that the second Integration by Parts simply undoes the first.
