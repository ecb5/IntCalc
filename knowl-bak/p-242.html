Notice that since \(y\dx{x}=\dx{A}{}\text{,}\) then this says that the area of the box with width \(\dx{x}{}\) and height \(y\) in the first diagram is the same as the length \(\dx{A}{}\) of the segment in the second diagram. Of course, these represent corresponding generic boxes and segments. If we add all of these together, the sum of the areas of the boxes in the first diagram will provide the area of the region; the sum of the lengths of the segments in the second diagram will provide the length of the segment from \(A(a)\) to \(A(b)\text{.}\) In other words, we have
\begin{equation*} \int_{x=a}^by\dx{x}=\int_{x=a}^b\dx{A}=A(b)-A(a){} \end{equation*}
which is what the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus says.
