Suppose a point \(P\) begins at \(x_0\) and traces out a line segment of some unknown length, \(x\text{.}\) We partition the line segment between \(x_0\) and \(x\) into subintervals as seen in figure . . . If the symbol \(\Delta x_1\) represents the first subinterval of \(x\text{,}\) \(\Delta x_2\) represents the second subinterval, and in general if \(\Delta{x_i}\) represents the \(i\)th subinterval of \(x\) then clearly the length of the line segment is
\begin{gather*} \Delta{x_1}+\Delta{x_2}+\Delta{x_3} +\cdots +\Delta{x_{n-1}}+\Delta{x_n}\\ = (x_1-x_0)+(x_2-x_2)+(x_3-x_2) +\cdots + (x_{n-1}-x_{n-2})+(x_n-x_{n-1})\\ =x_n-x_0 \end{gather*}
If the subintervals are all the same length, say \(\Delta{x}\text{,}\) then we no longer need the subscripts to distinguish them. This makes our notation a little less cumbersome, as follows:
\begin{equation} \underbrace{\Delta{x}+\Delta{x}+\cdots{}+\Delta{x}}_{\text{n copies}}=x_n-x_0\tag{1.2} \end{equation}
But it is cumbersome to write "\(+\Delta{x}\)" repeatedly so we define \(\Delta{x}+\Delta{x}+\cdots{}+\Delta{x}\) to be \(\int{}\Delta{x}\) and write
\begin{equation*} \int{}\Delta{x}=x_n-x_0 \end{equation*}
instead. The "\(\int{}\) symbol is called an integral sign.
