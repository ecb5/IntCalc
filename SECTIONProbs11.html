    Section 7.6 Homework #11: Surface Area and Arc Length

    Recall that we mentioned that in his treatise On the Sphere and theCylinder, Archimedes not only showed that the sphere had \(2/3\) the volume of its circumscribing cylinder, but it also had \(2/3\) the surface area. Again, we will not duplicate how Archimedes did it, but we will verify it with Calculus.
    Surface area poses a bit more of a challenge than volume. This can be illustrated by the following two boxes
    The boxes have the same base and same height, so they have the same volume. However, the slanted box clearly has more surface and could be stretched as far as you like, so there really is no limit to how large the surface area could be made. The slant makes a difference in surface area. To see this more carefully, we will need to first develop the notion of arc length.
    You have already been exposed to arc length in differential calculus. Recall that in looking at the catenary, we had that the hanging chain satisfied the equation
    \begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{ws}{H}. \end{equation*}
    where \(w,H\) were the weight density of the chain and horizontal tension, respectively, and \(s\) is the length of the chain from the lowest point to \((x,y)\text{.}\) We then differentiated and applied the Pythagorean Theorem to the “differential” triangle
    \begin{equation*} \dx{s}=\sqrt{(\dx{x})^2 + (\dx{y}^2} \end{equation*}
    We will exploit this to look at the arc length of a curve. Specifically, suppose we have a curve from point \(A=(x_1,y_1)\) to point \(B=(x_2,y_2)\) and want to compute the length of this curve \(s\text{.}\)
    If we divide this curve into infinitely small segments as above, then we can compute its length by integrating the lengths of these segments. In other words, we have
    \begin{equation*} s=\int^B_{X=A}\dx{s} \end{equation*}
    where \(X\) denotes a generic point \((x,y)\) on the curve. Using our infinitesimal Pythagorean Theorem, we have
    \begin{equation*} s=\int^B_{X=A}\dx{s}=\int^B_{X=A}{\sqrt{{\left(dx\right)}^2+{\left(dy\right)}^2}} \end{equation*}
    To actually compute this integral (via the Fundamental Theorem), we need to put this integral into a form that we can anti-differentiate. For example, we could put everything in terms of \(x\) or in terms of \(y\) yielding these two possibilities
    \begin{equation*} s=\int^B_{X=A} \sqrt{\left(1+{\left(\dfdx{y}{x}\right)}^2\right)} \abs{\dx{x}}=\int^B_{X=A} {\sqrt{\left(\left(\dfdx{x}{y}\right)^2+1\right)}} \abs{\dx{y}} \end{equation*}
    The absolute value is necessary since we are factoring out \(\sqrt{{\left(dx\right)}^2}\) or \(\sqrt{{\left(dy\right)}^2}\text{.}\) In practice, if \(x\) is always increasing then \(dx>0\) and so the absolute value isn’t necessary. The same applies to \(y\text{.}\) In many applications, this will be the case, but you should be wary that it can happen.

    Problem 7.6.0.1.

    (a)

    Consider the quarter circle given by \(y=\sqrt{1-x^2},\) \(-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\le x\le \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\text{.}\) Since the circumference of the unit circle is \(2\pi \text{,}\) then the length of this quarter circle should be \(\frac{\pi }{2}\text{.}\) Use an integral to verify this result. [There is a reason we didn’t have you compute the length of the entire semicircle \(y=\sqrt{1-x^2},\) \(-1\le x\le 1\text{.}\) If you can’t see what it is, don’t worry, we will come back to it later.]

    (b)

    Suppose we tried to compute the length of a quarter of the circle in the following way.
    \begin{equation*} \int^{(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}},\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}})}_{X=(-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}},\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}})}{\sqrt{{\left(\frac{dx}{dy}\right)}^2+1}dy}=\int^{\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}}_{y=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}}{\sqrt{{\left(\frac{dx}{dy}\right)}^2+1}\dx{y}}=0 \end{equation*}
    What is the problem?
    Again, there is real temptation to try to memorize the two formulas
    \begin{equation*} s=\int^B_{X=A}{\sqrt{1+{\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)}^2}\ |dx|}=\int^B_{X=A}{\sqrt{{\left(\frac{dx}{dy}\right)}^2+1}\ |dy|} \end{equation*}
    but we urge you not to. First, why try to remember two formulas, when one just needs to remember the Pythagorean Theorem?
    \begin{equation*} s=\int^B_{X=A}{ds}=\int^B_{X=A}{\sqrt{{\left(dx\right)}^2+{\left(dy\right)}^2}} \end{equation*}
    This seems much more natural. At this point, it is just a matter of determining what variable you wish to put everything in terms of. The variable doesn’t even need to be \(x\) or \(y\text{.}\) For example, suppose we have \(x=x\left(u\right)\ y=y\left(u\right),u_0\le u\le u_1\text{.}\) So we don’t need to deal with absolute values, let’s assume that the parameter \(u\) is always increasing so that \(du>0\text{.}\) Then we could put everything in terms of \(u\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} s=\int^B_{X=A}{ds}=\int^B_{X=A}{\sqrt{{\left(dx\right)}^2+{\left(dy\right)}^2}}=\int^{u_1}_{u=u_0}{\sqrt{{\left(\frac{dx}{du}\right)}^2+{\left(\frac{dy}{du}\right)}^2}du} \end{equation*}

    Use the parameterization \(x=\cos( \theta)\text{,}\) \(y=\sin( \theta), 0\le \theta \le 2\pi \) to verify that the circumference of the unit circle is \(2\pi \text{.}\)

    Problem 7.6.0.3.

    Here is another interesting fact about the cycloid. Sir Christopher Wren (best known for the buildings he designed after the great fire of London in 1666, including St. Paul’s Cathedral), showed in 1658 that the length of one arch of the cycloid is 8 times the radius of the generating circle. He did this without calculus, but we will use integration to verify his result. Consider the following cycloid
    \begin{equation*} x=a\left(\theta -\sin(\theta)\right)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} y=a\left(1-\cos( \theta)\right) \end{equation*}
    Show that the length of one arch of the cycloid is \(8a\text{.}\)
