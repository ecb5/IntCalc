    Section 2.2 Two Examples of Differential Equations

    We saw some examples of Differential Equations when we studied differentiation. For example, we saw in [cross-reference to target(s) "SECTIONGalileoDrops" missing or not unique] that Galileo found empirically that a body falling freely near the surface of the earth would accelerate downward at a constant rate we’ve called \(g\text{.}\) And Newton’s Second Law of Motion states that if we have a body with mass equal to one kilogram (\(m=1\)) falling freely under the force of the earth’s gravitational attraction then its acceleration, \(a\) is the derivative of its velocity, \(v\text{.}\) So: \(a=\dfdx{v}{t}\text{.}\) This is fully described by the differential equation
    \begin{equation} \dfdx{v}{t}=g,\tag{2.9} \end{equation}
    which is a simple model of the motion of a falling body.

    Example 2.2.0.1. Falling Down: A Very Simple Differential Equation.

    The simplest differential equations to solve are those like equation (2.9), where the derivative of a function, say velocity as a function of time, or \(v(t)\text{,}\) is given entirely in terms of of its variable, \(t\) in this case. In general such equations can be written in the form
    \begin{equation} \dfdx{v}{t} = f(t)\tag{2.10} \end{equation}
    where \(f(t)\) is some known function. Equation (2.9) is particularly simple since \(f(t)=g\text{,}\) is a constant function.
    We solve this differential equation as follows. First multiply both sides by \(\dx{t}\text{,}\) giving
    \begin{align} \dfdx{v}{t}\amp{}= g\notag\\ \dx{v}\amp{}=g\dx{t}\text{.}\tag{2.11} \end{align}
    Equation (2.11) says that the differential \(\dx{v} \) is equal to the differential \(g\dx{t} \text{.}\) Adding all of the differentials on both sides of equation (2.11) we have
    \begin{equation*} \int \dx{v}=\int g\dx{t} \end{equation*}
    and from this we see that
    \begin{equation*} v(t)=\int g\dx{t}{}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Thus to solve equation (2.9) we need only find an antiderivative of \(g\text{.}\)
    From (2.9) we can recover functions which tell us the velocity and the position of a body with a mass of one kilogram (falling freely near the surface of the earth and disregarding air resistance) at any time and under any starting conditions (see [cross-reference to target(s) "SECTIONGalileoDrops" missing or not unique]). We will have more to say about this particular model in Section 2.3.

    Problem 2.2.0.2.

    Emulate the computations in Example 2.2.0.1 to show that the solution of equation (2.10) can be written in the form
    \begin{equation*} v=\int f(t)\dx{t}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Example 2.2.0.3. A Hard Differential Equation.

    In [cross-reference to target(s) "PROBLEMHangingChain" missing or not unique] of [cross-reference to target(s) "SECTIONhyperb-trig-hang" missing or not unique] we saw that if \(y(x)\) is the shape that a hanging chain naturally settles into under the pull of gravity then \(y(x)\) will necessarily be a solution of the second order differential equation:
    \begin{equation} \dfdxn{y}{x}{2} = \frac{w}{H}\sqrt{1+\left(\dfdx{y}{x}\right)^2}.\tag{2.12} \end{equation}
    which is a model for the Hanging Chain problem. That is, the graph of a function which solves equation (2.6) will be the shape of a hanging chain.
    It is one thing to confirm a given solution, as in Problem 2.2.0.4, but finding the solution in the first place is not at all a simple task. A start might be to let \(z=\dfdx{y}{x}\text{,}\) so we have
    \begin{align} \dfdx{z}{x}\amp=\frac{w}{H}\sqrt{1+z^2}\notag\\ \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+z^2}}\dx{z}\amp =\frac{w}{H}\dx{x}\text{.}\tag{2.13} \end{align}
    Adding all of the differentials on both sides of equation (2.7), we see that
    \begin{equation} \int\frac{1}{\sqrt{1+z^2}}\dx{z}=\int\frac{w}{H}\dx{x}\text{.}\tag{2.14} \end{equation}
    The right-hand side of equation (2.8) doesn’t pose any problems. Since \(w\) and \(H\) are both constants, it follows that \(\frac{w}{H}\) is also a constant. From entry #3 of (1.7) we have \(\int \frac{w}{H}\dx{x} = \frac{w}{H}x+C \text{.}\) We still need to find the value of the constant \(C\text{,}\) but that is usually pretty straighforward as you will see soon.
    But the left-hand side isn’t as easy. It doesn’t match anything in [cross-reference to target(s) "TABLEBasicIntegration" missing or not unique] so we currently have no way to address it. We need a new idea.
    We will come back to this once we’ve developed some more sophisticated techniques. In the meantime see if you can find a way to compute \(\int\frac{1}{\sqrt{1+z^2}}\dx{z}\text{.}\) Come up with an idea, give it a try, and see what happens. The learning is in the trying, not the succeeding.
    Our goal in Chapter 3 will be to develop techniques that will enable us to compute various integrals like the ones we’ve just seen. These techniques will turn out to be very useful for other kinds of problems as well.
