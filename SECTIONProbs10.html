    Section 5.5 Homework #10

    Volumes: As we stated before and as you have seen, we have been focusing on areas to help us understand properties of definite integrals. However, this idea of adding infinitesimal quantities can be applied in a number of other applications. Keep in mind our general strategy of applying a relatively simple formula on an infinitely small piece and then integrating (adding) these infinitely small quantities. Take, for example, the volume of the following box.
    You probably remember the volume of this box as length times width times height. Let’s restate the volume in this way
    \begin{equation*} V\text{olume of box}=a\text{rea of face}\times t\text{hickness} \end{equation*}
    The advantage of thinking this way is that it applies to more general objects. For example, the volume of the following circular cylinder and prism follow the same formula
    \begin{equation*} V\text{olume of cylinder}=a\text{rea of face}\times t\text{hickness}=\pi r^2T \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} V\text{olume of prism}=a\text{rea of face}\times t\text{hickness}=\frac{1}{2}bhT \end{equation*}
    This idea even applies to ``slabs’’ that have an irregular face.
    \begin{equation*} V\text{olume of slab}=a\text{rea of face}\times t\text{hickness} \end{equation*}
    Combining this formula with our idea of dividing and integrating provides a way to compute the volumes of more general solids. For example, if you use the following cone to fill the corresponding cylinder, it seems that it takes three cones to fill the cylinder.
    Let’s verify this. We know that the volume of the cylinder is given by \(\pi R^2H\text{.}\) We need to show that the volume of the cone is \(1/3\) of that. With this in mind, we will put in a vertical axis, label it \(y\text{,}\) and divide the cone into infinitely thin slices. As before, we will only draw one generic slice, but you must imagine that this cone is comprised of infinitely many such slices stacked.
    Drawing this generic slice separately (remember our insistence on this), we can compute its volume.
    \begin{equation*} V\text{olume of slice}=a\text{rea of face}\times t\text{hickness}=\pi r^2dy \end{equation*}
    Integrating these volumes, we get
    \begin{equation*} V\text{olume of cone}=\int^H_{y=0}{\pi r^2\dx{y}} \end{equation*}
    What is left now is to put \(r\) in terms of \(y\) so the integral can be computed. This can be done by noticing that we have similar triangles.
    This gives us the proportion
    \begin{equation*} \frac{r}{y}=\frac{R}{H} \end{equation*}
    So \(r=\frac{R}{H}y\text{.}\)

    Problem 5.18. The Volume of a Cone.

    Substitute this value for \(r\) into the integral and show that the volume of the cone is, in fact, 1/3 the volume of the cylinder.

    Marcus Tullius Cicero 1  (106-43 BC) is considered to be one of the greatest orators and philosophers of the late Roman Republic 2  . When he was quaestor (a Roman official) in Syracuse (Sicily) in 75 BC, he set out to find the tomb of the great Greek mathematician Archimedes 3  who died during the Roman siege of the city 137 years earlier. Local Syracusans denied any knowledge of his grave, but when Cicero discovered a tombstone, overgrown with brambles and thorns, bearing the following symbol
    he knew that he had found the elusive grave of Archimedes.
    How did Cicero surmise that this was the grave of Archimedes? It seems that out of all of Archimedes works, he was proudest of his treatise \textit{On the Sphere and the Cylinder}. In it, Archimedes proves that the volume and surface area of a sphere is 2/3 that of its circumscribed cylinder. He was so proud if this, that he instructed that this be carved in his tombstone. This leads to the modern formulas for the volume and surface area of a sphere of radius \(r\)
    \begin{equation*} \text{Volume}=\frac{2}{3}\left(\pi r^2\cdot 2r\right)=\frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \text{Surface area}=\frac{2}{3}\left(2\pi r\cdot 2r+2\cdot \pi r^2\right)=4\pi r^2 \end{equation*}
    The way in which Archimedes discovered these formulas has led some to call him the inventor of integral calculus. This claim is arguable, but at the very least, his techniques certainly were a precursor to integral calculus. While we won’t go into Archimedes’ exact method, we will use integral calculus to derive these formulas for volume and surface area. We will focus of the volume now and come back to the surface area later. To compute this volume, we will consider that a sphere of radius \(r\) can be generated by revolving the curve \(y=\sqrt{r^2-x^2}\) about the \(x\)-axis.
    We will draw and label a generic rectangle of width \(\dx{x}\) and height \(y\) and compute the volume of the disk generated by revolving this rectangle about the \(x\)-axis. As before, we will draw and label the disk on the side.
    \begin{equation*} \text{Volume of disk}=\pi y^2\dx{x} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation} \text{Volume of sphere}=\int^r_{x=-r}{\pi y^2\ \dx{x}} \tag{5.2} \end{equation}
    Again, we insist that you go through this process (including drawing all of the pictures) instead of trying to memorize some formula. Admit it, with the disk drawn and labeled, wasn’t determining the volume of disk relatively easy. The integral only came in when we wanted to “add up” the volumes of all of the disks together.
