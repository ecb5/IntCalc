    Subsection 1.13.1 How Euler Did It

    Comment.

    This heading was borrowed from the series of articles 19  of the same name, written by the late Prof. Ed Sandifer, of Western Connecticut State University

    Problem 1.13.1.0.1.

    (a)

    Show that:
    \begin{equation*} y(t)=e^{-\frac{t}{2}} \left[ A\cos\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}t\right)+B\sin\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}t\right) \right] \end{equation*}
    satisfies equation (1.13.34) for any constants \(A\) and \(B\text{.}\)

    (b)

    Let \(A=1, B=0\) and plot this solution for \(0\le t\le 15\) and \(-0.5\le y \le 0.5.\) Does this seem to model a damped oscillation?

    Historical Background.

    Euler 21  was one of the all-time great mathematicians. In any list of stellar mathematicians, he would stand out among the best. Indeed, Pierre-Simon Laplace \((1749-1827)\text{,}\) an outstanding mathematician in his own right, was once asked by a novice how best to learn Calculus. Laplace told him “Read Euler, read Euler, he is the master of us all.”
    Leonhard Euler (1707—1783) solved the differential equation (1.13.34) by guessing that for some carefully chosen value of \(c\)
    \begin{equation*} y(t)=e^{ct} \end{equation*}
    would be a solution. In view of the results of Problem 1.13.1.0.1 this guess looks completely crazy. Where is the sine? Where is the cosine? Nothing seems to be oscillating!
    It is difficult to imagine what might have led him to make such a guess. But Euler knew that if his guess didn’t pan out he didn’t have to tell anyone about it. He could just toss all of his notes into his fireplace and pretend that he’d never made his crazy-seeming guess.
    The really crazy thing though, is that Euler’s guess worked. Here’s how.

    Pronunciation.

    His name is pronounced “oiler,” not “you-ler.”
    The key is the constant \(c\text{.}\) By leaving it unspecified Euler gave himself a little “wiggle room.” All he had to do now was find a value of \(c\) that would work.

    Problem 1.13.1.0.3.

    Show that if \(y=e^{ct}\) satisfies the differential equation
    \begin{equation*} \dfdxn{y}{t}{2}+\dfdx{y}{t}+y=0 \end{equation*}
    then \(c\) must satisfy the quadratic equation
    \begin{equation} c^2+c+1=0.\tag{1.13.35} \end{equation}
    Hint.
    Put the first two derivatives of \(y\) into the differential equation.
    Solving equation (1.13.35) we see that \(c=\frac{-1}{2}\pm\frac{\sqrt{-3}}{2},\) so we conclude that if \(c=-\frac12+\frac{\sqrt{-3}}{2}\) or \(c=-\frac12-\frac{\sqrt{-3}}{2}\) then \(y=e^{ct}\) satisfies equation (1.13.34).
    The appearance of \(\sqrt{-3}\) is troubling since there is no real number which is the square root of a negative number. But Euler knew that such “imaginary” numbers had been effectively used in the previous century, although no one could yet explain what they really were. Knowing that if nothing useful came of his efforts he could discard them and try something else, Euler decided to treat the square roots of negative numbers, like differentials, as “convenient fictions”

    Historical Background.

    Descartes had derided them as “imaginary” in his book La Geometrie in 1637 “. . . whereas we can always imagine as many roots for each equation as I have predicted, there is still not always a quantity which corresponds to each root so imagined.”
    Since he had assumed that \(c\) was a constant Euler wanted to see where that assumption would lead. In particular he decided to treat \(\sqrt{-3}\) as the constant it is. To make things “easier on the eyes,” Euler wrote \(\sqrt{-3} = \sqrt{3}\cdot\sqrt{-1}\) and set \(\sqrt{-1}=i\text{.}\)
    Euler now had two solutions of equation (1.13.34), one for each possible value of \(c\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} y_1(t) =e^{\left(-\frac12+\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}i\right)t} = e^{-\frac{t}{2}}\cdot e^{\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}it} \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} y_2(t) =e^{\left(-\frac12-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}i\right)t} =e^{-\frac{t}{2}}\cdot e^{-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}it}, \end{equation*}
    but the presence of \(i=\sqrt{-1}\) makes it hard to understand what these solutions could mean.

    Problem 1.13.1.0.4.

    Assume that \(i=\sqrt{-1}\) is a constant.

    (a)

    Show that \(y_1(t)\) is a solution of equation (1.13.34).

    (b)

    Show that \(y_2(t)\) is also a solution of equation (1.13.34).

    (c)

    Show that if \(a\) and \(b\) are constants then \(w(t)=ay_1(t)+by_2(t)\) is also a solution of equation (1.13.34).
    To find meaning in his solutions he returned to the simpler equation (1.13.31),
    \begin{equation*} m\dfdxn{y}{t}{2}= -ky, \end{equation*}
    which he already knew how to solve. To make things simpler still he assumed that \(m=k=1\) and tried his crazy guess, \(y=e^{ct}\) again on
    \begin{equation} \dfdxn{y}{t}{2}= -y.\tag{1.13.36} \end{equation}

    Drill 1.13.1.0.5.

    Show that \(y=e^{ct}\) is a solution of the differential equation (1.13.36) when \(c=i\) or \(c=-i\text{.}\)
    Drill 1.13.1.0.5 shows that \(y=e^{it}\) and \(y=e^{-it}\) are both solutions of equation (1.13.36), but Euler knew (as we do, see [cross-reference to target(s) "SECTIONmore-high-deriv" missing or not unique].) that the solution of equation (1.13.36) is \(y(t)=a\cos(t)+b\sin(t)\) where \(a\) and \(b\) are constants.
    He now had very different looking solutions of equation (1.13.36). Euler guessed that somehow these must be the same, so he set
    \begin{equation*} e^{it}=a\cos(t)+b\sin(t) \end{equation*}
    and tried to find \(a\) and \(b\text{.}\)

    Problem 1.13.1.0.6.

    (a)

    Show that if \(e^{it}=a\cos(t)+b\sin(t),\) then \(a=1\) and \(b=i.\)
    Hint.
    To get \(a,\) substitute \(t=0.\) To get \(b,\) differentiate first.

    (b)

    Show that if \(e^{-it}=a\cos(t)+b\sin(t),\) then \(a=1\) and \(b=-i.\)
    From part (a) of Problem 1.13.1.0.6 we get the formula,
    \begin{equation} e^{it}=\cos(t)+ i\sin(t).\tag{1.13.37} \end{equation}
    equation (1.13.37) is known as Euler’s Identity and it is foundational to electrical engineering, and physics (especially Quantum Mechanics). For mathematicians it is the key to understanding the geometry of the Complex Numbers (numbers of the form \(a+bi\text{,}\) where \(a\) and \(b\) are real numbers and \(i^2=-1\text{.}\)
    For Euler, it was a way to translate trigonometric functions to exponential (and hyperbolic trigonometric) functions and back. For us, it is the key to our dampened oscillation problem.
    Before we get to that, notice the following curious consequences of Euler’s Identity.

    Comment.

    We call this curious because it relates the numbers \(0, 1, i, e\) , and \(\pi\) all in one simple formula. To find out what it actually means you’ll need to become a mathematics major and take a course in Complex Analysis. It’s fun. You should do that.

    Drill 1.13.1.0.7.

    Show that \(e^{i\pi}+1=0\text{.}\)
    Another curious consequence is that the trigonometric functions and the hyperbolic trigonometric functions are related via the constant \(i=\sqrt{-1}\text{.}\)

    Problem 1.13.1.0.8.

    (a)

    Show that \(\cosh(it)=\frac{e^{it}+e^{-it}}{2}=\cos(t)\)

    (b)

    Show that \(\frac{1}{i}\sinh(it)=\frac{e^{it}-e^{-it}}{2i}=\sin(t)\text{.}\)
    Finally, we return to equation (1.13.34). In Problem 1.13.1.0.1 we saw that
    \begin{equation*} z(t)=e^{-\frac{t}{2}} \left[ A\cos\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}t\right)+B\sin\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}t\right) \right] \end{equation*}
    and in part (c) of Problem 1.13.1.0.4 we saw that
    \begin{equation*} y= e^{-\frac{t}{2}}\left[ae^{\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}it} + be^{-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}it}\right] \end{equation*}
    is another. If these are the same solution we can find a relation between the constants \(a\text{,}\) \(b\text{,}\) \(A\text{,}\) and \(B\text{.}\)

    Problem 1.13.1.0.9.

    (a)

    Assuming that
    \begin{equation*} z(t) =e^{-\frac{t}{2}} \left[ A\cos\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}t\right)+B\sin\left(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}t\right) \right], \end{equation*}
    that
    \begin{equation*} w(t) =e^{-\frac{t}{2}}\left[ae^{\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}it} + be^{-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}it}\right], \end{equation*}
    and that
    \begin{equation*} z(t)=w(t)\text{,} \end{equation*}
    use Euler’s Identity to show that \(A=a+b\) and that \(B=i(a-b)\text{.}\)

    (b)

    Show that if \(w(t)\) satisfies the initial conditions
    \begin{align*} w(0)\amp =0\\ w^\prime(0)\amp =1. \end{align*}
    then \(a=-\frac{i}{\sqrt{3}}\) and \(b=\frac{i}{\sqrt{3}}\text{.}\) what is \(\frac{-1}{i}\) equal to?
    Hint.
    If \(i^2=-1\)

    (c)

    Use \(a\) and \(b\) from part (b) to show that \(A\) and \(B\) are real numbers.
