    Section 19.2 The Partial Fraction Decomposition

    Subsection 19.2.1 Simple Decompositions: The Logistic Equation, Redux

    Mathematics is the art of avoiding computation.
    🔗
    ―Brockway McMillan
    🔗
    When we analyzed the Logistic Equation in Section 11.1 we were unable to find a formula for \(P(t)\text{.}\) But, by analyzing the IVP directly we were able to generate a “qualitative” graph of \(P(t)\text{.}\) That is, we were able to get a general sense of the shape of the graph of \(P(t)\) even if the coordinates of particular points were unavailable.
    🔗
    While a qualitative graph is better than nothing, it would be more useful to have an explicit formula for \(P(t)\text{,}\) if we can find it. With integration we now have the tool we need to find such a formula.
    🔗
    Starting with the Logistic Equation,
    \begin{align} \dfdx{P}{t} \amp{}= 0.1P \left( 1-\frac{P}{100} \right),\amp{}P(0)\amp{}=10,\tag{19.31} \end{align}
    the first step is to separate the variables:
    \begin{equation} \frac{\dx{P}}{P(100-P)}=0.001\dx{t}\text{.}\tag{19.32} \end{equation}
    If we integrate both sides of this equation the result will be an equation in which \(P(t)\) appears. If we can solve that for \(P(t)\) we will have our formula for \(P(t)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Integrating the right sides presents no difficulties:
    \begin{equation} \int 0.001\dx{t} = 0.001t+C\text{.}\tag{19.33} \end{equation}
    🔗
    But at our current level of skill with integration the only option that presents itself for integrating the left side is to to complete the square in the denominator and apply the appropriate trigonometric substitution.
    🔗

    Problem 19.2.1.1.

    By completing the square in the denominator and making the substitution \(P-50=50\sin (\theta )\) integrate the left side of equation (19.32) and show that
    \begin{equation} \frac{1}{50}\ln \left[ \sqrt{\frac{100-P}{P}} \right] = 0.001t+C\text{.}\tag{19.34} \end{equation}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 19.2.2 Heaviside’s Cover–up Method (HCUM)

    As you have just seen, integrating the left side of equation (19.32) is entirely within the range of our current skill set but it is rather daunting. And we’re not even finished yet. We must still solve equation (19.34) for \(P(t)\text{.}\) Before we do that we’d really like to find a simpler way to compute the integral.
    🔗
    To be consistent with what follows we observe that we can rewrite equation (19.32) as
    \begin{equation} \frac{\dx{P}}{P\left(P-100\right)}=-0.001 \dx{t}\tag{19.35} \end{equation}
    since \(\frac{\dx{P}}{P\left(P-100\right)} = -\frac{\dx{P}}{P\left(100-P\right)}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Integrating the right–hand side poses no problem as we would get
    \begin{equation} \int{\frac{\dx{P}}{P\left(P-100\right)}}=-0.001\ t+C\tag{19.36} \end{equation}
    To compute the left–hand side, notice that
    \begin{equation} \frac{1}{P\left(P-100\right)}=\frac{-\frac{1}{100}}{P}+\frac{\frac{1}{100}}{P-100}\tag{19.37} \end{equation}
    🔗

    Problem 19.2.2.1.

    (a)
    Confirm equation (19.37).
    🔗
    🔗
    (b)
    Use equation (19.37) to show that
    \begin{equation*} \int{\frac{\dx{P}}{P\left(P-100\right)}}=\frac{1}{100}{\mathrm{ln} \left(\frac{P-100}{P}\right)\ }+C_2 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    (c)
    Use equation (19.36) and the result in part (b) to show that
    \begin{equation*} P\left(t\right)=\frac{100}{1+Ke^{-0.1t}} \end{equation*}
    where \(K\) is an arbitrary constant.
    🔗
    🔗
    (d)
    Solve equation (19.34) for \(P\) and show you obtain the same result.
    🔗
    🔗
    (e)
    The original IVP (19.31) had the initial condition \(P\left(0\right)=10\text{.}\) Use this to show that \(K=9\) and plot this graph to confirm that it has the same general shape that we discovered in Subsection 11.1.1
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    This is certainly easier to deal with than a trigonometric substitution, but it begs the question, “Where did equation (19.37) \(\frac{1}{P\left(P-100\right)}=-\frac{\frac{1}{100}}{P}+\frac{\frac{1}{100}}{P-100}\) come from?” It is one thing to verify such an identity, but it is quite another to actually formulate one.
    🔗
    We found equation (19.37) by an algebraic technique called the Partial Fraction Decomposition or PFD. There are several places in mathematics where it is useful to break a single rational expresstion into the sum of simpler rational expressions. We will be using it to simplify the integration when the integrand is a rational function.
    🔗
    A fully general statement of the PFD is given in Theorem 19.2.3.7 but as you can see the general statement is quite comprehensive. Moreover the statement of the theorem doesn’t help us use it as a practical tool. So we will work through a number of increasingly complex examples to build things up slowly.
    🔗
    To begin, if we have an expression of the form \(\frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}\) where \(a\text{,}\) and \(b\) are known constants, and \(a\neq b\text{,}\) then the PFD says that there are constants \(A\) and \(B\) such that as
    \begin{equation} \frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}=\frac{A}{x-a}+\frac{B}{x-b}\text{.}\tag{19.38} \end{equation}
    Our task is to find values for \(A\) and \(B\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Take note of the following fact: The right side of equation (19.38) consists of rational functions where the degree of the numerator is one less than the degree of the denominator. Since the numerators are all constants (polynomials of degree zero) and the denominators are all linear (polynomials of degree one) this is, at the moment, a rather bombastic way to state this fact. However in Subsection 19.2.3 we will find it very useful.
    🔗
    There are a couple of ways we can find \(A\) and \(B\text{.}\) What we’d like to do is find the simplest, least difficult to implement method we can. After all, we’re not here to do fraction decompositions. This is just one step toward computing an integral and we’d like for the decomposition step to be as simple and painless as possible.
    🔗
    The most direct way to find \(A\) and \(B\) is to add the fractions on the right-hand side of equation (19.38).
    \begin{equation*} \frac{0x+1}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)}=\frac{A\left(x-b\right)+B\left(x-a\right)}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)}=\frac{\left(A+B\right)x+\left(-Ab-Ba\right)}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Comparing coefficients leads to the system of two equations in the two unknowns \(A,\ B\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} A+B\amp{}=0\\ -Ab-Ba\amp{}=1 \end{align*}
    🔗

    Problem 19.2.2.2.

    Solve these two equations for \(A\) and \(B\) to show that \(A=\frac{1}{a-b}\) and \(B=\frac{1}{b-a}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    This was very straightforward and not too tedious, but what if we tried to do the same with three distinct factors
    \begin{equation} \frac{1}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)\left(x-c\right)}=\frac{A}{x-a}+\frac{B}{x-b}+\frac{C}{x-c}\text{.}\tag{19.39} \end{equation}
    For now we are simply assuming that a PFD works for three or more linear factors. But this true and will follow from Theorem 19.2.3.7, below.
    🔗
    Adding the terms on the right we see that
    🔗
    \begin{align*} \amp{}\frac{0x^2+0x+1}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)\left(x-c\right)}\\ \amp{}=\frac{A\left(x-b\right)\left(x-c\right)+B\left(x-a\right)\left(x-c\right)+C\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)\left(x-c\right)}\\ \amp{}=\frac{\left(A+B+C\right)x^2-\left(A\left(b+c\right)+B\left(a+c\right)+C\left(a+b\right)\right)x+\left(Abc+Bac+Cab\right)}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)\left(x-c\right)} \end{align*}
    and equating corresponding coefficients from either side leads to the following system of three linear equations in the three unknowns \(A\text{,}\) \(B\text{,}\) and \(C\text{.}\)
    \begin{align*} A+B+C\amp{}=0\\ \left(b+c\right)A+\left(a+c\right)B+C\left(a+b\right)\amp{}=0\\ (bc)A+(ac)B+(ab)C\amp{}=1 \end{align*}
    🔗
    This is certainly doable, but it is also very tedious. Could you imagine if we had four factors? Or five? This method is simple to understand and it will always work, but the amount of computation escalates quickly as the number of linear factors increases. For this reason we (the authors) think of this as the brute force method. We’d really prefer something a little easier to implement.
    🔗
    To find a way to simplify things a bit we’ll look again at equation (19.38). We can clear the fractions by multipling both sides by \((x-a)(x-b)\) which gives
    \begin{equation*} 1=A\left(x-b\right)+B\left(x-a\right)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Instead of expanding as we did before, notice that this equation is an identity which means that it is true regardless of the value of \(x\text{.}\) Thus if we let \(x=a\) we get \(1=A\left(a-b\right)+B(a-a)\) or \(A=\frac{1}{a-b}\text{.}\)

    We’ve already simplified the PFD process quite a bit, but in fact it can be (and will need to be) streamlined even further.
    🔗
    When he was computing some PFDs related to his work with differential equations Oliver Heaviside (1850—1925) made the following observation. Look closely at the numerators of the fractions the right side of
    \begin{equation} \frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}= \frac{\frac{1}{a-b}}{x-a}+\frac{\frac{1}{b-a}}{x-b}\tag{19.42} \end{equation}
    Do you see that the numerator above \(x-a\) is what we would get if we simply replaced \(x\) with \(a\) on the left and “covered up,” (or ignored, or set equal to one) the factor \(x-a\text{?}\)
    🔗
    That is, if \(\frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}= \frac{A}{x-a}+\frac{B}{x-b}\) then
    \begin{equation*} A=\eval{\frac{1}{(\text{covered})(x-b)}}{x}{a}=\frac{1}{a-b}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Similarly
    \begin{equation*} B=\eval{\frac{1}{(x-a)(\text{covered})}}{x}{b}=\frac{1}{b-a}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This is known as Heaviside’s Cover–up Method (HCUM) and it is the simplest way we know to compute the coefficients of the PFD of the rational functions we’ve seen so far.
    🔗
    The HCUM is a very convenient shortcut. But it is necessary to understand why any shortcut works. Otherwise all we have is the shortcut. If we encounter a problem where the shortcut won’t work but the underlying principle will, and all we know is the shortcut we can’t make any progress.
    🔗
    To see why the HCUM works take another look at the decomposition
    \begin{equation} \frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}=\frac{A}{x-a}+\frac{B}{x-b}\text{.}\tag{19.43} \end{equation}
    If we multiply both sides by \(x-a\text{,}\) we get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{x-b}=A+\frac{B(x-a)}{x-b} \end{equation*}
    which holds as long as \(x\ne b\text{.}\) Notice that \(\frac{1}{x-b} = \frac{1}{(\text{covered})(x-b)}\text{.}\) Next, if we take \(x=a\) we get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{a-b}=\frac{1}{(\text{covered})(a-b)} =A+\frac{B(a-a)}{a-b}=A\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Example 19.2.3.1.
    Heaviside’s method, as we have used it so far, will not work on the rational function \(\frac{x^2}{(x-2)(x-3)}\text{.}\) If you try to apply it you’ll get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x^2}{(x-2)(x-3)} = \frac{-4}{x-2}+\frac{9}{x-3} \end{equation*}
    which is wrong. Try it and see.
    🔗
    The difficulty here is that, as a practical matter, the PFD requires that the degree of the numerator of our rational function to be strictly less than the degree of the denominator. (This is somewhat analogous to putting a fraction in “lowest terms.”) In this example the degree of the numerator is
    \begin{equation*} \deg\left((x-2)(x-3)\right)=\deg\left(x^2-5x+6\right)=2 \end{equation*}
    which is the same as the degree of the denominator: \(\deg\left(x^2\right)=2\text{.}\) So the PFD, as we’ve used it so far, doesn’t apply.
    🔗
    At first this looks like it will be a major obstacle but it really isn’t too bad. There are two ways we can proceed from here.
    🔗
    Dividing First: The first is simply to do the polynomial division indicated. In practice that means multiplying out the denominator first and then dividing:
    \begin{align} \frac{x^2}{(x-2)(x-3)} = \frac{x^2}{x^2-5x+6} \amp{}= 1+ \frac{5x-6}{x^2-5x+6}\notag\\ \amp{}=1+\frac{5x-6}{(x-2)(x-3)}\tag{19.45} \end{align}
    🔗
    From there we can compute the PFD of \(\frac{5x-6}{(x-2)(x-3)}\) as before, finally obtaining
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x^2}{x^2-5x+6} = 1+\frac{-4}{x-2}+\frac{9}{x-3}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Dividing Last: The second is to factor out the numerator:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x^2}{(x-2)(x-3)} = x^2 \left[ \frac{1}{(x-2)(x-3)} \right] \text{,} \end{equation*}
    compute the PFD of \(\frac{1}{(x-2)(x-3)}\) as before. This gives
    \begin{align} \frac{x^2}{(x-2)(x-3)} \amp{}= x^2 \left[ \frac{-1}{x-2}+\frac{1}{x-3} \right]\notag\\ \amp{}=\frac{-x^2}{x-2}+\frac{x^2}{x-3}\tag{19.46}\\ \amp{}= -x-2-\frac{4}{x-2} +x+3+\frac{9}{x-3}\notag\\ \amp{}=1-\frac{4}{x-2}+\frac{9}{x-3}.\notag \end{align}
    🔗
    If you are uncomfortable with long division of polynomials, we can accomplish the same thing using a substitution. At the point where we have
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x^2}{\left(x-2\right)\left(x-3\right)}=-\frac{x^2}{x-2}+\frac{x^2}{x-3} \end{equation*}
    if we let \(y=x-2\) in the first fraction and \(z=x-3\) in the second fraction, then we have
    🔗
    \begin{align*} \frac{x^2}{\left(x-2\right)\left(x-3\right)}\amp{}=-\frac{{\left(y+2\right)}^2}{y}+\frac{{\left(z+3\right)}^2}{z}\\ \amp{}=-\frac{y^2+4y+4}{y}+\frac{z^2+6z+9}{z}\\ \amp{}=-y-4-\frac{4}{y}+z+6+\frac{9}{z}\\ \amp{}=-x+2-4-\frac{4}{x-2}+x-3+6+\frac{9}{x-3}\\ \amp{}=1-\frac{4}{x-2}+\frac{9}{x-3} \end{align*}
    🔗
    Whether you divide first or divide last is entirely your choice, so choose whichever you are most comfortable with and use it. But be flexible. Both methods work. Use the one that feels more natural to you.

    As you have seen there are many ways to attack the PFD of a rational function. The good news is that you can use any algebraic technique to determine the coefficients. Unfortunately that is also the bad news.
    🔗
    In this section we (the authors) are building up a procedure for the PFD which is simple, both to understand and to implement, but it is not the only known procedure. And the PFD is not Calculus, it is Algebra. So for us it is simply a way to manipulate an integral into a form we can compute. So if you know, or can find, a better method for computing the PFD go ahead and use it.
    🔗
    Having so many options can make your head spin, especially if you are using techniques without fully understanding why they work. So think before you act and proceed deliberately. With experience you will begin to see patterns and there will be fewer false starts.
    🔗
    The theorem underlying all of this is Theorem 19.2.3.7 which we now state formally.
    🔗
    Theorem 19.2.3.7. The Partial Fraction Decomposition (PFD).
    If \(P(x)\text{,}\) \(Q(x)\text{,}\) and \(R(x)\) are polynomials having no non–trivial common factors with \(\deg(P)\lt\deg(Q)+ \deg(R)\) then there exist polynomials \(A(x)\) and \(B(x)\text{,}\) with \(\deg(A)\lt\deg(Q)\) and \(\deg(B)\lt\deg(R)\text{,}\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)\cdot{}R(x)} = \frac{A(x)}{Q(x)}+\frac{B(x)}{R(x)}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    For example, suppose we had the rational function
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x^2-x+2}{x{\left(x+1\right)}^2} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    By theorem 19.2.2.11, we know we can write this as
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x^2-x+2}{x{\left(x+1\right)}^2}=\frac{A}{x}+\frac{Bx+C}{{\left(x+1\right)}^2} \end{equation*}
    but it is more convenient to write it as
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x^2-x+2}{x{\left(x+1\right)}^2}=\frac{A}{x}+\frac{B(x+1)+C}{{\left(x+1\right)}^2}=\frac{A}{x}+\frac{B}{x+1}+\frac{C}{{\left(x+1\right)}^2} \end{equation*}

    Example 19.2.3.10. The PFD With Repeated Linear Factors.
    Let’s finish up the decomposition of \(\frac{x}{(x-1)^2(x+2)^2}\) from part (b) of Problem 19.2.3.9. From the HCUM You should have found that \(B=\frac19\) and \(D=\frac{-2}{9}\) so that
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{x}{(x-1)^2(x+2)^2}= \frac{A}{x-1} + \frac{\frac19}{(x-1)^2} +\frac{C}{x+2} -\frac{\frac29}{(x+2)^2} \end{equation*}
    When we reached this point in Example 19.2.3.3 (see part (b) of Problem 19.2.3.4) our next step was to simply choose a value for \(x\text{.}\) We could do that here as well but because we now have two unknowns we’d have to choose two distinct values for \(x\) and generate a system of two coupled equations to solve. We’ve already seen that there are more efficient methods for computing the PFD than solving a system of equations.
    🔗
    Instead we’ll isolate all of the terms with our unknowns on one side of the equation, thus:
    \begin{equation} \displaystyle \frac{x}{(x-1)^2(x+2)^2} - \frac{\frac19}{(x-1)^2} +\frac{\frac29}{(x+2)^2} = \frac{A}{x-1} +\frac{C}{x+2}\tag{19.48} \end{equation}
    🔗
    It is clear that the right side of equation (19.48) is the PFD of some rational function whose denominator is \((x-1)(x+2)\text{.}\) That is, if we were asked to begin the process of computing the PFD of
    \begin{equation*} \frac{P(x)}{(x-1)(x+2)} \end{equation*}
    for some polynomial \(P(x)\) then our first step would be to write down the right side of equation (19.48). Therefore it must be that
    \begin{equation} \displaystyle \frac{x}{(x-1)^2(x+2)^2} - \frac{\frac19}{(x-1)^2} +\frac{\frac29}{(x+2)^2} = \frac{P(x)}{(x-1)(x+2)}\tag{19.49} \end{equation}
    for some polynomial \(P(x)\text{,}\) and if we can find that polynomial we can compute \(A\) and \(C\) by the HCUM.
    🔗
    To find \(P(x)\text{,}\) we first clear the fraction on the right leaving,
    \begin{equation} \frac{x}{(x-1)(x+2)} - \frac19 \left( \frac{x+2}{x-1} \right) +\frac29 \left( \frac{x-1}{x+2} \right) = P(x)\tag{19.50} \end{equation}
    It is tempting to find a common denominator and add the three fractions on the left together. Since we have to end up with the polynomial \(P(x)\) it must be that once the addition is done there will be a factor in the top that will cancel with the common denominator.
    🔗
    That will work. Give it a try if you want. Good luck.
    🔗
    But we know that \(P(x)\) is a polynomial and we can make very effective use of that knowledge as follows.
    🔗
    If we perform this division in the second term on the left we get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x+2}{x-1} = 1 +\frac{3}{x-1}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Similarly if we perform the division in the third term we get \(\frac{x-1}{x+2}= 1-\frac{3}{x+2}\text{.}\) Putting these back into equation (19.50) we have
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x}{(x-1)(x+2)} - \frac19 \left( 1 +\frac{3}{x-1} \right) +\frac29 \left( 1-\frac{3}{x+2} \right) = P(x) \end{equation*}
    or
    \begin{equation} \underbrace{\left( \frac{x}{(x-1)(x+2)} -\frac{\frac13}{x-1} -\frac{\frac23}{x+2}\right)}_{=0} + \frac19 = P(x) \text{.}\tag{19.51} \end{equation}
    Since \(P(x)\) must be a polynomial it follows that the expression in the parentheses — everything that is not a polynomial — must be equal to zero, as we’ve indicated above.
    🔗
    Thus \(P(x)=\frac19{}\text{.}\) To finish the decomposition we place this in to equation (19.49) and observe that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\frac19 }{(x-1)(x+2)} = \frac{A}{x-1}+\frac{C}{x+2} \end{equation*}
    and from the HCUM we see that \(A=\frac{1}{27} \) and \(C=-\frac{1}{27}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Having found all of the constants we conclude from equation (19.48) that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x}{(x-1)^2(x+2)^2}= \frac{\frac{1}{27}}{x-1} + \frac{\frac19 }{(x-1)^2} -\frac{\frac{1}{27}}{x+2} -\frac{\frac29 }{(x+2)^2} \text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Naturally, it isn’t always that simple. If the degree of any (or all) of the numerators in equation (19.51) was not less than it’s denominator then we’d have had to compute the division in order to recover the polynomial part of each. But then the sum of the remainders would necessarily be zero so they can be ignored just as we ignored \(\frac{x}{(x-1)(x+2)} -\frac{\frac13}{x-1} -\frac{\frac23}{x+2}\) above.

    🔗
    Aside: Comment.
    But it can be factored over the complex numbers. We will address this at the end of this section.
    🔗
    From our previous work we know that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{(x-2)(x^2+1)}= \frac{A}{x-2}+\frac{Bx+C}{x^2+1} \end{equation*}
    so by the HCUM \(A=\frac15 \text{.}\) Therefore
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{(x-2)(x^2+1)}= \frac{\frac15}{x-2}+ \frac{Bx+C}{x^2+1}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗

    🔗

    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{x-2}=\frac15 \left(\frac{x^2+1}{x-2}\right)+(Bx+C) \end{equation*}

    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{x-2}-\frac15\left( \frac{x^2+1}{x-2}\right)=Bx+C\text{,} \end{equation*}

    \begin{equation*} Bx+C=-\frac15x -\frac25 \text{,} \end{equation*}

    \begin{equation} \frac{1}{(x-2)(x^2+1)}= \frac{\frac15}{x-2}- \frac{\frac15x}{x^2+1} -\frac{\frac25}{x^2+1}\text{.}\tag{19.52} \end{equation}

    Subsection 19.2.4 Avoiding the Irreducible Case by Allowing Complex Numbers

    “Imaginary numbers are a fine and wonderful resource of the divine intellect, almost an amphibian between being and non–being.”
    🔗
    ―Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (1646—1716)
    🔗
    Traditionally a course in Calculus studiously avoids using complex and imaginary numbers for reasons that have always eluded the authors. As we saw in Section 8.13 there are times when using the complex numbers can considerably simplify the problem. This is another such situation.
    🔗
    The theorem that every polynomial can be completely factored into (possibly repeated) linear factors is known by the rather pompous name The Fundamental Theorem of Algebra.
    🔗
    Since this is in fact a theorem (meaning that it has been proved) an obvious question presents itself: “Why did we bother with the Irreducible Quadratic case when discussing the Partial Fraction Decomposition?” That is, why did we (the authors) tell you that \(x^2+1\) can’t be factored, when the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra says that it can?
    🔗
    Recall that in the first paragraph of the subsection Irreducible Quadratic Factors we were careful to state that “\(x^2+1\)” cannot be factored over the real numbers. This is true but it can be factored over the complex numbers (real numbers plus imaginary numbers). C. F. Gauss (1777–1855) attempted to prove the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra four times. His first attempt was in his PhD dissertation, but that proof wasn’t complete.
    🔗
    Dissatisfied with his first effort he tried three more times over the course of his life, with better results. His last proof was published fully fifty years after his first and relied heavily on the complex numbers
    🔗
    The Fundamental Theorem of Algebra is one of the results that forced mathematicians to undertake the study of the complex numbers and eventually accept them as an extension of the real numbers.
    🔗

    DIGRESSION: How Did We Get a PFD.

    Throughout this section we’ve been citing Theorem 19.2.3.7 to justify the existence of a PFD for a rational function and we’ve used a number of shortcuts to determine the coefficients involved. It turns out that Theorem 19.2.3.7 can be derived from long division of polynomials, but instead of doing that, we will take the opportunity to build a PFD from the ground up though some illustrative examples and exercises. This could actually be used to compute the PFD of a given rational function in a systematic way but is not nearly as efficient as the methods employed. We are really only using it here to justify that a PFD does in fact exist for a given rational function. Some of what we have here will look familiar, but it will be included to give an entire picture of how a PFD is built starting with basics.
    🔗
    To begin, notice that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{x-a}-\frac{1}{x-b}=\frac{\left(x-b\right)-\left(x-a\right)}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)}=\frac{a-b}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)} \end{equation*}
    This says that if \(a\neq b\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)} =\frac{1}{a-b}\left(\frac{1}{x-a}-\frac{1}{x-b}\right) \end{equation*}
    or
    \begin{equation} \frac{1}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)}=\frac{\frac{1}{a-b}}{x-a}+\frac{\frac{1}{b-a}}{x-b} \tag{19.57} \end{equation}
    which agrees with what we obtained in Problem 19.2.2.2.
    🔗
    Earlier, we also made the claim that we can find a PFD for three or more distinct linear factors, and that this follows from Theorem 19.2.3.7. We can utilize formula (19.57) obtain this as well and we can use what we already did. Suppose \(a\text{,}\) \(b\text{,}\) and \(c\) are distinct real numbers. Then
    \begin{align*} \frac{1}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)\left(x-c\right)}\amp{}=\frac{1}{a-b}\left(\frac{1}{x-a}-\frac{1}{x-b}\right)\left(\frac{1}{x-c}\right)\\ \amp{}=\frac{1}{a-b}\left(\frac{1}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-c\right)}-\frac{1}{\left(x-b\right)\left(x-c\right)}\right) \end{align*}
    🔗
    Problem 19.2.4.3.
    (a)
    Use equation (19.57) on the two fractions in the parentheses above to obtain the PFD
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)\left(x-c\right)}=\frac{\frac{1}{\left(a-b\right)\left(a-c\right)}}{x-a}+\frac{\frac{1}{\left(b-a\right)\left(b-c\right)}}{x-b}+\frac{\frac{1}{\left(c-b\right)\left(c-a\right)}}{x-c} \end{equation*}
    which is what we obtained in Problem 19.2.2.6.
    🔗
    🔗
    (b)
    Suppose we have four distinct real numbers \(a,b,c,d\text{.}\) Using the result obtained in part a, we obtain
    \begin{align*} \amp{}\frac{1}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)\left(x-c\right)\left(x-d\right)}\\ \amp{}=\left(\frac{\frac{1}{\left(a-b\right)\left(a-c\right)}}{x-a}+\frac{\frac{1}{\left(b-a\right)\left(b-c\right)}}{x-b}+\frac{\frac{1}{\left(c-b\right)\left(c-a\right)}}{x-c}\right)\left(\frac{1}{x-d}\right) \end{align*}
    Use equation (19.57) to show that we have the PFD
    \begin{align*} \frac{1}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)\left(x-c\right)\left(x-d\right)}=\amp{}\\ \amp{}\frac{\frac{1}{\left(a-b\right)\left(a-c\right)(a-d)}}{x-a}\\ \amp{}+\frac{\frac{1}{\left(b-a\right)\left(b-c\right)\left(b-d\right)}}{x-b}\\ \amp{}+\frac{\frac{1}{\left(c-a\right)\left(c-b\right)\left(c-d\right)}}{x-c}\\ \amp{}+\frac{\frac{1}{\left(a-b\right)\left(a-c\right)\left(d-a\right)}+\frac{1}{\left(b-a\right)\left(b-c\right)\left(d-b\right)}+\frac{1}{\left(c-a\right)\left(c-b\right)\left(d-c\right)}}{x-d} \end{align*}
    Compare this result to what you get using the HCUM. [You can see why we are using equation (19.57) to show the existence of a PFD, instead of using it to determine the coefficients.]

    Example 19.2.4.4.
    Find the PFD of \(\frac{2x^2+x+1}{\left(x-4\right)\left(x-2\right)}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Notice that we can write this as
    \begin{align*} \frac{2x^2+x+1}{\left(x-4\right)\left(x-2\right)}\amp{}=\left(2x^2+x+1\right)\left(\frac{1}{\left(x-4\right)\left(x-2\right)}\right)\\ \amp{} =\left(2x^2+x+1\right)\left(\frac{\frac{1}{2}}{x-4}-\frac{\frac{1}{2}}{x-2}\right)\\ \amp{} =\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{2x^2+x+1}{x-4}-\frac{2x^2+x+1}{x-2}\right) \end{align*}
    If we let \(y=x-4\) and \(z=x-2\text{,}\) we get
    \begin{align*} \frac{2x^2+x+1}{\left(x-4\right)\left(x-2\right)}\amp{}=\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{2{\left(y+4\right)}^2+\left(y+4\right)+1}{y}-\frac{2{\left(z+2\right)}^2+\left(z+2\right)+1}{z}\right)\\ \amp{} =\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{2y^2+17y+37}{y}-\frac{2z^2+9z+11}{z}\right)\\ \amp{} =\frac{1}{2}\left(2y+17+\frac{37}{y}-2z-9-\frac{11}{z}\right)\\ \amp{}=\frac{1}{2}\left(2y-2z+8+\frac{37}{y}-\frac{11}{z}\right)\\ \amp{} =y-z+4+\frac{\frac{37}{2}}{y}-\frac{\frac{11}{2}}{z}\\ \amp{} =x-4-x+2+4+\frac{\frac{37}{2}}{x-4}-\frac{\frac{11}{2}}{x-2}\\ \amp{}=2+\frac{\frac{37}{2}}{x-4}-\frac{\frac{11}{2}}{x-2} \end{align*}

To see this, we need to look a little into complex number arithmetic. Perhaps even more surprising is that all this hinges on the notion of a complex conjugate. Given a complex number \(z=a+bi\text{,}\) its complex conjugate is given by \(\bar{z}=a-bi\text{.}\) The following are facts about complex conjugates.
    🔗

    Let \(z=a+bi\) and \(w=c+di\text{,}\) then
    1. \(\displaystyle \overline{z+w}=\bar{z}+\bar{w}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle \overline{z\cdot w}=\bar{z}\cdot \bar{w}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(z+\bar{z}=2a\) which is a real number
      🔗
      🔗
    4. \(\displaystyle z-\bar{z}=2bi\)
      🔗
      🔗
    5. \(z\cdot \bar{z}=a^2+b^2\) which is a nonnegative real number.
      🔗
      🔗

    Problem 19.2.4.9.

    🔗
    As was said previously, the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra says that every nonconstant polynomial with real coefficients has at least one root which could be a complex number. A consequence of this is that any nonconstant polynomial and be factored entirely into linear factors where some of the factors may involve complex roots.
    🔗
    Specifically, if
    \begin{equation*} p\left(x\right)=a_0+a_1x+a_2x^2+\dots +a_nx^n \end{equation*}
    is a real polynomial with roots \(r_1, r_2, \dots, r_n\text{,}\) (some roots possibly repeated, some possibly complex), then
    \begin{equation*} p\left(x\right)=a_n\left(x-r_1\right)\left(x-r_2\right)\dots (x-r_n)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The interesting thing about \(p\left(x\right)\) is that if it has a root which is a complex number \(z\) then \(\bar{z}\)is also a root.
    🔗
    Problem 19.2.4.10.
    Prove that if \(z\) is a root of
    \begin{equation*} p\left(x\right)=a_0+a_1x+a_2x^2+\dots +a_nx^n\text{,} \end{equation*}
    then \(\bar{z}\) is also a root.
    🔗
    Hint.
    If z is a root, then \(a_0+a_1z+a_2z^2+\dots +a_nz^n=0\text{.}\) Take the conjugate of this and use properties in Theorem 19.2.4.8. Also note that any real number is its own conjugate.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    The first consequence of this is that any real polynomial which is irreducible must be of degree 1 or 2. To see this, suppose we have a real polynomial \(p(x)\) of degree greater than 2. If one of the roots of this polynomial is real, say \(r\text{,}\) then we can write \(p\left(x\right)=\left(x-r\right)q(x)\) for some polynomial \(q(x)\) whose degree is at least two. This says \(p\left(x\right)\ \)is not irreducible. If \(p(x)\) has a complex root \(z\text{,}\) then \(\bar{z}\ \)is also a root, and we can write \(p\left(x\right)=\left(x-z\right)\left(x-\bar{z}\right)q(x)\) where \(q\left(x\right)\ \)has degree at least one.
    🔗
    Problem 19.2.4.11.
    Multiply \(\left(x-z\right)\left(x-\bar{z}\right)\) and conclude that it is a polynomial with real coefficients. This says that \(p\left(x\right)\) is not irreducible.
    🔗
    🔗
    With these preliminaries established, suppose we have the rational function
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{\left(x^2+ax+b\right)\left(x-c\right)} \end{equation*}
    where \(x^2+ax+b\) is an irreducible real polynomial and \(c\) is a real number. If we factor \(x^2+ax+b\) over the complex numbers, we get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{\left(x^2+ax+b\right)\left(x-c\right)}=\frac{1}{\left(x-z\right)\left(x-\bar{z}\right)\left(x-c\right)} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Note that everything we’ve done so far works over the complex numbers, so utilizing
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{\left(x-a\right)\left(x-b\right)\left(x-c\right)}=\frac{\frac{1}{\left(a-b\right)\left(a-c\right)}}{x-a}+\frac{\frac{1}{\left(b-a\right)\left(b-c\right)}}{x-b}+\frac{\frac{1}{\left(c-b\right)\left(c-a\right)}}{x-c} \end{equation*}
    we have
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{\left(x-z\right)\left(x-\bar{z}\right)\left(x-c\right)}=\frac{\frac{1}{\left(z-\bar{z}\right)\left(z-c\right)}}{x-z}+\frac{\frac{1}{\left(\bar{z}-z\right)\left(\bar{z}-c\right)}}{x-\bar{z}}+\frac{\frac{1}{\left(c-\bar{z}\right)\left(c-z\right)}}{x-c} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Problem 19.2.4.12.
    Show that \(\frac{1}{\left(c-\bar{z}\right)\left(c-z\right)}\) is a real number.
    🔗
    Hint.
    Note that \(c-\bar{z}=\overline{c-z}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Problem 19.2.4.13.
    🔗
    (a)
    Show that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\frac{1}{\left(z-\bar{z}\right)\left(z-c\right)}}{x-z}+\frac{\frac{1}{\left(\bar{z}-z\right)\left(\bar{z}-c\right)}}{x-\bar{z}}=\frac{-\frac{1}{\left(z-c\right)\left(\bar{z}-c\right)}x+\left(\frac{z}{\left(z-\bar{z}\right)\left(\bar{z}-c\right)}+\frac{\bar{z}}{\left(\bar{z}-z\right)\left(z-c\right)}\right)}{\left(x^2+ax+b\right)} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    (b)
    We already know that \(-\frac{1}{\left(z-c\right)\left(\bar{z}-c\right)}\) is a real number. Show that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{z}{\left(z-\bar{z}\right)\left(\bar{z}-c\right)}+\frac{\bar{z}}{\left(\bar{z}-z\right)\left(z-c\right)} \end{equation*}
    is a real number also.
    🔗
    Hint.
    To avoid a lot of messy algebra, notice that these are conjugates.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Putting this all together, this says that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{\left(x^2+ax+b\right)\left(x-c\right)}=\frac{Ax+B}{x^2+ax+b}+\frac{C}{x-c} \end{equation*}
    where \(A,B,C\) are real numbers. This is the PFD we were seeking. This same idea will work for any denominator which is factored into linear or quadratic irreducible factors.
    🔗
    END OF DIGRESSION
