    Section 2.2 The Partial Fraction Decomposition

    When we analyzed the [cross-reference to target(s) "EQUATIONLogEq" missing or not unique] in [cross-reference to target(s) "SECTIONpopulation-dynamics" missing or not unique] we were unable to find a formula for \(P(t)\text{.}\) But, by analyzing the IVP directly we were able to generate a “qualitative” graph of \(P(t)\text{.}\) That is, we were able to get a general sense of the shape of the graph of \(P(t)\) even if the coordinates of particular points were unavailable.
    While a qualitative graph is better than nothing, it would be more useful to have an explicit formula for \(P(t)\text{,}\) if we can find it. With integration we now have the tool we need to find such a formula.

    Subsection 2.2.1 Simple Decompositions

    The Logistic Equation, Redux.

    Starting with the Logistic Equation,
    \begin{align} \dfdx{P}{t} \amp{}= 0.1P \left( 1-\frac{P}{100} \right),\amp{}P(0)\amp{}=10,\tag{2.31} \end{align}
    the first step is to separate the variables:
    \begin{equation} \frac{\dx{P}}{P(100-P)}=0.0001\dx{t}\text{.}\tag{2.32} \end{equation}
    If we integrate both sides of this equation the result will be an equation in which \(P(t)\) appears. If we can solve that for \(P(t)\) we will have our formula for \(P(t)\text{.}\)
    Integrating the right sides presents no difficulties:
    \begin{equation} \int 0.0001\dx{t} = 0.0001t+C\text{.}\tag{2.33} \end{equation}
    But at our current level of skill with integration the only option that presents itself for integrating the left side is to to complete the square in the denominator and apply the appropriate trigonometric substitution.

    Subsubsection Heaviside’s Cover–up Method (HCUM)

    Problem 2.2.1.1.
    By completing the square in the denominator and making the substitution \(P-50=50\sin (\theta )\) integrate the left side of equation (2.32) and show that
    \begin{equation} \frac{1}{50}\ln \left[ \frac{P}{\sqrt{P(100-P)}} \right] = 0.0001t+C\text{.}\tag{2.34} \end{equation}
    As you have just seen, this integration is entirely within the range of our current skill set but it is rather daunting. And we’re not even finished yet. We must still solve equation (2.34) for \(P(t)\text{.}\) Before we do that we’d really like to find a simpler way to compute the integral.
    Here’s a simpler way. Observe that the fraction \(\frac{1}{P(100-P)}\) can be decomposed as
    \begin{equation} \frac{1}{P(100-P)}=\frac{\frac{-1}{100}}{P}+\frac{\frac{1}{100}}{100-P}\text{.}\tag{2.35} \end{equation}
    Drill 2.2.1.2.
    Confirm equation (2.35).
    With the identity (2.35) in hand we can integrate the right side of equation (2.32) as follows.
    \begin{align*} \int \frac{1}{P(100-P)}\dx{x} \amp{}= \int\frac{\frac{1}{100}}{P}+\frac{\frac{1}{100}}{100-P}\dx{x} \\ \amp{}= \frac{1}{100} \left[ -\ln\left(\frac{1}{P}\right)+\ln\left(\frac{1}{100-P}\right) \right]\\ \amp{}= \frac{1}{100} \ln \left( \frac{P}{100-P} \right) \end{align*}
    Combining this with equation (2.33) we see that
    \begin{equation} -\frac{1}{100} \ln \left( \frac{100-P}{P} \right) = \int 0.0001\dx{t} = 0.0001t+C\tag{2.36} \end{equation}
    which is much easier to solve for \(P(t)\) than and equation (2.34).
    Problem 2.2.1.3.
    (a)
    Solve equation (2.36) and show that \(P(t) = \frac{100}{Ke^{-0.01t}+1}\) where \(K\) is an arbitrary constant.
    (b)
    Use the initial condition in IVP (2.31) to show that in part (a) \(K=9\text{,}\) so that
    \begin{equation*} P(t) = \frac{100}{9e^{-0.01t}+1} \end{equation*}
    and then confirm that you get the same solution when you solve equation (2.34) for \(P(t)\text{.}\)
    (c)
    Graph \(P(t)\) from part (b) and confirm that it has the same general shape that we discovered in [cross-reference to target(s) "SUBSECTIONModelingTrout" missing or not unique].
    This is much simpler but the roadblock is this: where did equation (2.35) come from? How did we find this decomposition of the fraction \(\frac{1}{P(100-P)}\text{?}\)
    We found equation (2.35) by an algebraic technique called the Partial Fraction Decomposition or PFD. There are several places in mathematics where it is useful to break a single rational expresstion into the sum of simpler rational expressions. We will be using it to simplify the integration when the integrand is a rational function.
    A fully general statement of the PFD is given in Theorem 2.2.2.9 but as you can see the general statement is quite comprehensive. Moreover the statement of the theorem doesn’t help us use it as a practical tool. So we will work through a number of increasingly complex examples to build things up slowly.
    To begin, if we have an expression of the form \(\frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}\) where \(a\text{,}\) and \(b\) are known constants then the PFD says that there are constants \(A\) and \(B\) such that as
    \begin{equation} \frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}=\frac{A}{x-a}+\frac{B}{x-b}\text{.}\tag{2.37} \end{equation}
    Our task is to find values for \(A\) and \(B\text{.}\)
    Take note of the following fact: The right side of (2.37) consists of rational functions where the degree of the numerator is one less than the degree of the denominator. Since the numerators are all constants (polynomials of degree zero) and the denominators are all linear (polynomials of degree one) this is, at the moment, a rather bombastic way to state this fact. However we use return to and finally use this fact in Subsubsection  we will find it very useful.
    There are a couple of ways we can find \(A\) and \(B\text{.}\) What we’d like to do is find the simplest, least difficult to implement method we can. After all, we’re not here to do fraction decompositions. This is just one step toward computing an integral and we’d like for the decomposition step to be as simple and painless as possible.
    Conceptually the simplest method available is to simply to choose values for \(x\text{.}\) This is a valid approach because the equation
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}=\frac{A}{x-a}+\frac{B}{x-b} \end{equation*}
    is actually an identity — it will be true no matter what the value of \(x\) is.
    If we choose \(x=0\) we get
    \begin{equation*} Ab+Ba =-1\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and if we choose \(x=1\) we get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{(1-a)(1-b)}=\frac{A}{1-a} + \frac{B}{1-b} \end{equation*}
    and we have a system of two equations in the unknowns \(A\) and \(B\) which we can solve.
    Drill 2.2.1.4.
    In this form the equations
    \begin{equation*} Ab+Ba =-1 \end{equation*}
    and,
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{(1-a)(1-b)}=\frac{A}{1-a} + \frac{B}{1-b} \end{equation*}
    are a little intimidating. But it’s not really as bad as it looks. Clear the fractions and then solve for \(A\) and \(B\text{.}\)
    The method we’ve just outlined will work, and it will always work. But this is only our first example of the PFD and you can surely see that that if we have more than two constants the difficulty of solving for the coefficients will increase rapidly. But again, this method works and it will always work, so it is something we can always fall back on if we can’t find something simpler.
    Since the method just outlined will always work one possible way to lighten the load is to look for values of \(x\) that will make the resulting equations simpler to solve. Sadly, no obvious choice presents itself, but hold that thought for a bit. We’ll return to it.
    Another possible method is to simply clear the fractions and compare like terms. (In fact, our previous method can be made a little more palatable by clearing the fractions first.) Thus
    \begin{equation*} (x-a)(x-b)\cdot\left[\frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}\right]=\left[ \frac{A}{x-a}+\frac{B}{x-b}\right]\cdot(x-a)(x-b)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Multiplying through and cancelling, we have
    \begin{equation} 1=A(x-b)+B(x-a)\tag{2.38} \end{equation}
    so that
    \begin{equation*} \textcolor{red}{0}x+ \textcolor{blue}{1}=(\textcolor{red}{A+B})x + \left(\textcolor{blue}{-Ab-aB}\right) \text{.} \end{equation*}
    Comparing the coefficient of \(x\) and the constant terms we see that we need to solve the following system of two equations in the two unknowns \(A\) and \(B\)
    \begin{align} A+B\amp{}=0\tag{2.39}\\ Ab+Ba \amp{}=-1.\notag \end{align}
    Drill 2.2.1.5.
    Solve the system of equations (2.39) and show that you get the same solution that you got in Drill 2.2.1.4.
    This is a little better. We’ve still got a system of two equations to solve, but it seems to be a little more manageable. Still, as the problems get more complex the number of equations in our system will grow. We’d still like to find something simpler than this if we can.
    But wait! Take another look at equation (2.38). With a judicious use of that formula you can actually compute this PFD almost completely in your head. It’s that simple. Really.
    Because equation (2.38) is an identity, — it is true regardless of which value of \(x\) we choose. So of we choose \(x=a\) we get
    \begin{equation*} A=\frac{1}{a-b} \end{equation*}
    immediately. Similarly choosing \(x=b\) gives \(B=\frac{1}{b-a}\text{,}\) also immediately.Thus the PFD of \(\frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}\) is
    \begin{equation} \frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}= \frac{\frac{1}{a-b}}{x-a}+\frac{\frac{1}{b-a}}{x-b}\text{.}\tag{2.40} \end{equation}
    Drill 2.2.1.6.
    Verify the identity (2.40) by adding the fractions on the right side together.

    Example 2.2.1.9.
    As we’ve mentioned before, all mathematical models make simplifying assumptions. It is important to know what are the simplifications are so we know the conditions under which the model is unrealistic. For example, [cross-reference to target(s) "EQUATIONFishery1" missing or not unique] works fine in the short term but in the long term it predicts explosive growth which is not sustainable. We addressed this when we generated the [cross-reference to target(s) "EQUATIONLogEq" missing or not unique] by including as a factor, \(\left( 1-\frac{P}{100}\right)\text{.}\) That modification put an upper bound on the population size as we have seen.
    However the Logistic Equation doesn’t put any lower bound on the population size. This is also unrealistic since we know that if a population of critters is too small they tend to die out. The following tweak to the logistic model:
    \begin{equation} \dfdx{P}{t}=0.01P\left(1-\frac{P}{100}\right)\left(\frac{P}{10}-1\right),\tag{2.41} \end{equation}
    forces a population which is too small to die out. In this case, \(10\) is called the minimum viability level of the population.

    As you can see from Problem 2.2.1.11 even in its simplest form the PFD can become a very large problem. For example suppose \(n=100\text{.}\) In that case we’d need to find \(100\) coefficients. We ’d really like to make the method as efficient as possible. To that end there is one more refinement we’ll need before moving on to more general problems.
    When he was computing some PFDs related to his work solving differential equations Oliver Heaviside
     6 
    https://mathshistory.st-andrews.ac.uk/Biographies/Heaviside/
    (1850—1925) made the following observation. Look closely at the numerators of the fractions the right side of
    \begin{equation} \frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}= \frac{\frac{1}{a-b}}{x-a}+\frac{\frac{1}{b-a}}{x-b}\tag{2.42} \end{equation}
    (this is equation (2.40)). Do you see that the numerator above \(x-a\) is what we would get if we simply replaced \(x\) with \(a\) on the left and “covered up” \(x-a\text{?}\)
    That is, if \(\frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}= \frac{A}{x-a}+\frac{B}{x-b}\) then
    \begin{equation*} A=\eval{\frac{1}{(\text{covered})(x-b)}}{x}{a}=\frac{1}{a-b}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Similarly
    \begin{equation*} B=\eval{\frac{1}{(x-a)(\text{covered})}}{x}{b}=\frac{1}{b-a}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This is known as Heaviside’s Cover–up Method (hcum) and it is the simplest way we know to compute the coefficients of the PFD of the rational functions we’ve seen so far.

    Subsection 2.2.2 Advanced Decompositions

    Subsubsection Linear Factors

    Example 2.2.2.1.
    Heaviside’s method will not work on the rational functions \(\frac{x^2}{(x-2)(x-3)}\text{.}\) If you try to apply it you’ll get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x^2}{(x-2)(x-3)} = \frac{-4}{x-2}+\frac{9}{x-3} \end{equation*}
    which is wrong. Try it and see.
    The difficulty here is that the PFD requires that the rational function to be decomposed must be in “lowest terms.” In this context that means that the degree (the value of the largest exponent) of the numerator must be strictly less than the degree of the denominator. In this example the degree of the numerator is \(\deg\left((x-2)(x-3)\right)=\deg\left(x^2-5x+6\right)=2\) which is the same as the degree of the denominator: \(\deg\left(x^2\right)=2\text{.}\) So the PFD doesn’t apply.
    At first this looks like it will be a major obstacle but it really isn’t too bad. We simply need to do the polynomial division indicated. In this case that means computing
    \begin{equation} \frac{x^2}{x^2-5x+6} = 1+ \frac{5x-6}{(x-2)(x-3)}\text{.}\tag{2.43} \end{equation}
    From there we can compute the PFD of \(\frac{5x-6}{(x-2)(x-3)}\) as before.

    Example 2.2.2.3.
    Heaviside’s method will also fail on this function:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x^2+x-3}{x^2(x-1)}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    If you apply it you’ll get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x^2+x-3}{x^2(x-1)} = \frac{3}{x^2} + \frac{-1}{x-1} \end{equation*}
    which is incorrect. Check it and see.
    The problem here is the factor \(x^2\) in the denominator. If you look back at the problems and examples we’ve looked at so far you’ll see that in every case every possible factor which appeared in the denominator of the fraction on the left side also appeared in one of the denominators on the right side. In this case clearly both \(x^2\) and \(x-1\) are factors of \(x^2(x-1)\text{,}\) but \(x\) is also a factor so the correct decomposition
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x}{x^2(x-1)} = \frac{A}{x} + \frac{B}{x^2}+ \frac{C}{x-1}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and we need to find the constants \(A\text{,}\) \(B\text{,}\) and \(C\text{.}\)
    The values \(B=3\text{,}\) and \(C=-1\) can still be found using the HCUM, so we have
    \begin{equation} \frac{x}{x^2(x-1)} = \frac{A}{x} + \frac{3}{x^2}- \frac{1}{x-1}\text{.}\tag{2.44} \end{equation}
    To find \(A\) recall that equation (2.44) is an identity so it is true regardless of the value of \(x\text{.}\) Since \(A\) is the only parameter left we can find it by setting \(x=2\) (or anything else). This gives \(\frac{2}{2^2(2-1)} = \frac{A}{2} + \frac{3}{2^2}- \frac{1}{2-1} \text{,}\) or \(A=2\text{.}\) So the full decomposition is
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x}{x(x-1)} - \frac{3}{x}+ \frac{x}{x-1} = A\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Example 2.2.2.6. The PFD With Distinct Linear Factors.
    Let’s finish up the decomposition of \(\frac{x}{(x-1)^2(x+2)^2}\) from part (b) of Problem 2.2.2.5. From the HCUM You should have found that \(B=\frac19\) and \(D=\frac{-2}{9}\) so that
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{x}{(x-1)^2(x+2)^2}= \frac{A}{x-1} + \frac{\frac19}{(x-1)^2} +\frac{C}{x+2} -\frac{\frac29}{(x+2)^2} \end{equation*}
    When we reached this point in Example 2.2.2.3 our next step was to simply choose a value for \(x\text{.}\) We could do that here as well but we’d have to choose two values for \(x\) thereby generating a pair of equations to solve and we’ve already seen that there are more efficient methods for computing the PFD than solving a system of equations.
    Instead we’ll isolate all of the terms with our unknowns on one side of the equation, thus:
    \begin{equation} \displaystyle \frac{x}{(x-1)^2(x+2)^2} - \frac{\frac19}{(x-1)^2} +\frac{\frac29}{(x+2)^2} = \frac{A}{x-1} +\frac{C}{x+2}\tag{2.45} \end{equation}
    It is clear that the right side of equation (2.45) is the PFD of some rational function whose denominator is \((x-1)(x+2)\text{.}\) That is, if we were asked to begin the process of computing the PFD of
    \begin{equation*} \frac{P(x)}{(x-1)(x+2)} \end{equation*}
    for some polynomial \(P(x)\) then our first step would be to write down the right side of equation (2.45). Therefore it must be that
    \begin{equation} \displaystyle \frac{x}{(x-1)^2(x+2)^2} - \frac{\frac19}{(x-1)^2} +\frac{\frac29}{(x+2)^2} = \frac{P(x)}{(x-1)(x+2)}\tag{2.46} \end{equation}
    for some polynomial \(P(x)\text{,}\) and if we can find that polynomial we can compute \(A\) and \(C\) by the HCUM.
    We can find \(P(x)\) by essentially the same method we used to find the constant \(A\) in Example 2.2.2.3. First we clear the fraction on the right
    \begin{equation} \frac{x}{(x-1)(x+2)} - \frac19 \left( \frac{x+2}{x-1} \right) +\frac29 \left( \frac{x-1}{x+2} \right) = P(x)\tag{2.47} \end{equation}
    Since the left side is guaranteed to reduce to the polynomial it is tempting to find a common denominator and add the three fractions on the left together. Since we have to end up with the polynomial \(P(x)\) it must be that once the addition is done there will be a factor in the top that will cancel with the common denominator.
    That will work. Give it a try if you want. Good luck.
    But we’re trying to find the most efficient method we can so instead we’re going to make judicious use of the knowledge that we must end up with a polynomial.
    First think about the expression in the second term on the left: \(\frac{x+2}{x-1}\text{.}\) If we perform this division we get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x+2}{x-1} = 1 +\frac{3}{x-1}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Similarly if we computing the division in the third term we get \(\frac{x-1}{x+2}= 1-\frac{3}{x+2}\text{.}\) Putting these back into equation (2.47) we have
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x}{(x-1)(x+2)} - \frac19 \left( 1 +\frac{3}{x-1} \right) +\frac29 \left( 1-\frac{3}{x+2} \right) = P(x) \end{equation*}
    or
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x}{(x-1)(x+2)} - \frac19 -\frac{\frac13}{x-1} +\frac29 -\frac{\frac23}{x+2} = P(x) \text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since we must get a polynomial it must be that the fractions in this last equation will sum to zero. So we can ignore them and conclude that our polynomial is given by the other terms. So
    \begin{equation*} P(x)=-\frac19 + \frac29 =\frac19\text{.} \end{equation*}
    To finish the decomposition we observe that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\frac19 }{(x-1)(x+2)} = \frac{A}{x-1}+\frac{C}{x+2} \end{equation*}
    and from the HCUM we see that \(A=\frac{1}{27} \) and \(C=-\frac19\text{.}\)
    Having found all of the constants we conclude that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x}{(x-1)^2(x+2)^2}= \frac{\frac{1}{27}}{x-1} + \frac{\frac19 }{(x-1)^2} -\frac{\frac19}{x+2} -\frac{\frac29 }{(x+2)^2} \text{.} \end{equation*}

    Subsubsection Irreducible Quadratic Factors

    In the problems and examples we’ve encountered so far whenever there was a quadratic factor in the denominator we were able to either factor it into two distinct linear factors (e.g. \((x^2-x+6) = (x+2)(x-3)\) or into one such factor, squared (e.g. \((x^2+6x+9)=(x+3)^2\)). Unfortunately we cannot yet compute the integral
    \begin{equation*} \int \frac{1}{(x-2)(x^2+1)}\dx{x}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    because the factor \(x^2+1\) cannot be factored (over the real numbers). So we’ll need to extend our procedure to account for this.
    Comment.
    But it can be factored over the complex numbers. We will address this at the end of this section.
    The PFD still guarantees that \(\frac{A}{x+2}\) will appear as a term in the decomposition and the HCUM will still allow us to find
    \begin{equation*} A= \frac{\eval{\frac{1}{(\text{covered})(x^2+1)}}{x}{2}}{x-2}= \frac{\frac15}{x-2}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Thus we have
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{(x-2)(x^2+1)}= \frac{\frac15}{x+2} + \ParamEqTwo {\text{ other stuff we}} {\text{ don't know yet}}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The computation of the “other stuff” is the next topic.
    Fortunately the setup for the “other stuff” is not substantially different from what we have done heretofore. First, since the factor \(x+2\) and \(x^2+1\) cannot be factored there will be a term in the decompostion which corresponds to each: To identify the “unknown” recall that in the paragraph following equation (2.37) we observed that in the PFD the degrees of the numerators were always one less than the degrees of the denominators. Following that pattern it is reasonable to speculate that the decomposition is
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{(x-2)(x^2+1)}= \frac{A}{x+2}+ \frac{Bx+C}{x^2+1} \end{equation*}
    where \(A\text{,}\) \(B\text{,}\) and \(C\) are constants to be determined. And our speculation is correct. If we can find \(A\text{,}\) \(B\text{,}\) and \(C\) the decomposition will be complete.
    Reasoning by analogy it seems reasonable to speculate that the PFD of \(\frac{1}{x^2(x^2+1)^3(x^2+9)(x-5)} \) will be
    \begin{align} \frac{1}{x^2(x^2+1)^3(x^2+9(x-5))} =\amp{} \frac{A}{x}+\frac{B}{x^2}+\frac{Cx+D}{x^2+1}+\frac{Ex+F}{(x^2+1)^2}\tag{2.48}\\ \amp{}+\frac{Gx+H}{(x^2+1)^3} + \frac{Ix+J}{x^2+9}+\frac{K}{x-5},\notag \end{align}
    and that is correct.
    The good news is that we are done. Equation (2.48) is an example of the most general form that a Partial Fraction Decomposition can have. Beyond that they just get bigger.
    The HCUM can still be used to compute \(B\) and \(K\text{.}\) It can even be extended to compute \(I\) and \(J\text{,}\) but for the kinds of problems we will be encountering extending the HCUM is not worth the effort.
    Let’s finish the decomposition of \(\frac{1}{(x-2)(x^2+1}\) which we had interupted. We had reached this point:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{(x-2)(x^2+1)}= \frac{\frac15}{x+2}+ \frac{Bx+C}{x^2+1} \end{equation*}
    which, as we keep repeating, is an identity. So if we isolate the expression \(Bx+C\) on the right side of the equation then the left side will reduce to a linear expression in \(x\text{.}\) We can then simply compare the coefficients.
    Multiplying both sides by \(x^2+1\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{x-2}=\frac15 \left(\frac{x^2+1}{x-2}\right)+(Ax+B) \end{equation*}
    and isolating \(Ax+B\) gives
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{x-2}-\frac15\left( \frac{x^2+1}{x-2}\right)=Ax+B\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and if we simplify the expression on the left we will see that
    \begin{equation*} Ax+B=\frac15x +\frac25 \text{,} \end{equation*}
    so
    \begin{equation} \frac{1}{(x-2)(x^2+1)}= \frac15\left(\frac{1}{x+2}\right)+ \frac15\left( \frac{x}{x^2+1}\right) +\frac25\left(\frac{1}{x^2+1}\right)\text{.}\tag{2.49} \end{equation}
    Drill 2.2.2.8.
    Confirm that equation (2.49) is correct by getting a common denominator and adding the terms on the right together.
    Theorem 2.2.2.9. The Partial Fraction Decomposition (PFD).
    If \(P(x)\text{,}\) \(Q(x)\text{,}\) and \(R(x)\) are polynomials having no non–trivial common factors with \(\deg(P)\lt\deg(Q)+ \deg(R)\) then there exist polynomials \(A(x)\) and \(B(x)\text{,}\) \(\deg{A}\lt\deg{Q}\) and \(\deg(B)\lt\deg(R)\text{,}\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)\cdot{}R(x)} = \frac{A(x)}{Q(x)}+\frac{B(x)}{R(x)}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    In his PhD dissertation Gauss
     7 
    https://mathshistory.st-andrews.ac.uk/Biographies/Gauss/
    proved that every polynomial can be completely factored into (possibly repeated) linear factors. These days this is known as the rather bombastic name The Fundamental Theorem of Algebra.
    Since we have covered all cases where the denominator of a rational function can be factored into (possibly repeated) linear factors, it appears that we need say no more about integrating such functions via the PFD.
    But that is, unfortunately, not true. For the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra to hold we need to allow the polynomial to have complex as apposed to real roots. That is we need to expand our number system to include the complex numbers \(\CC \) (a complex number is a real number plus an imaginary number). We first encountered the complex numbers in [cross-reference to target(s) "PROBLEMEulersFormula" missing or not unique] in connection with Euler’s Identity.
    Example 2.2.2.10.
    Suppose we want to decompose the rational function \(\frac{x-2}{x(x^2+1)}\text{.}\) There are no real numbers, \(a\) and \(b\text{,}\) with the property that \(x^2+1=(x-a)(x-b)\) but \(a=i\) and \(b=-i\) (where as before \(i^2=-1\)) will work just fine since
    \begin{equation*} (x-i)(x+i)=x^2-i^2=x^2-(-1)=x^2+1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Thus we can use the HCUM to compute
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x-2}{x(x^2+1)}=\frac{x-2}{x(x-1)(x+1)} =\frac{-2}{x}+\frac{\frac{i-2}{i(2i)}}{x-i}+\frac{\frac{i+2}{i(-2i)}}{x+i}= \frac{-2}{x}+\frac{\frac{i-2}{-2}}{x-i}+\frac{\frac{-i-2}{-2}}{x+i} =\frac{-2}{x}+\frac{1-\frac12i}{x-i}+\frac{1+\frac12 }{x+1}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    But, unfortunately, in this course we confine our attention to the real numbers and treat the complex numbers as a guilty secret, even when using them would make more sense. To get the decomposition back into just the real number we compute the sum \(\frac{1-\frac12i}{x-i}+\frac{1+\frac12 }{x+1}\) as follows
    \begin{equation} \frac{1-\frac12i}{x-i}+\frac{1+\frac12 }{x+1}=2x+1\text{.}\tag{2.50} \end{equation}
    Thus our decompostion over the real numbers (rather than the complex numbers is
    \begin{equation} \frac{x-2}{x(x^2+1)}=\frac{-2}{x}+\frac{2x+1}{x^2+1}\text{.}\tag{2.51} \end{equation}
