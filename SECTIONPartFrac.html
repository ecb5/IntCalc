    Section 19.2 The Partial Fraction Decomposition

    When we analyzed the Logistic Equation in Section 11.1 we were unable to find a formula for \(P(t)\text{.}\) But, by analyzing the IVP directly we were able to generate a “qualitative” graph of \(P(t)\text{.}\) That is, we were able to get a general sense of the shape of the graph of \(P(t)\) even if the coordinates of particular points were unavailable.
    While a qualitative graph is better than nothing, it would be more useful to have an explicit formula for \(P(t)\text{,}\) if we can find it. With integration we now have the tool we need to find such a formula.

    Subsection 19.2.1 Simple Decompositions

    The Logistic Equation, Redux.

    Starting with the Logistic Equation,
    \begin{align} \dfdx{P}{t} \amp{}= 0.1P \left( 1-\frac{P}{100} \right),\amp{}P(0)\amp{}=10,\tag{19.31} \end{align}
    the first step is to separate the variables:
    \begin{equation} \frac{\dx{P}}{P(100-P)}=0.0001\dx{t}\text{.}\tag{19.32} \end{equation}
    If we integrate both sides of this equation the result will be an equation in which \(P(t)\) appears. If we can solve that for \(P(t)\) we will have our formula for \(P(t)\text{.}\)
    Integrating the right sides presents no difficulties:
    \begin{equation} \int 0.0001\dx{t} = 0.0001t+C\text{.}\tag{19.33} \end{equation}
    But at our current level of skill with integration the only option that presents itself for integrating the left side is to to complete the square in the denominator and apply the appropriate trigonometric substitution.

    Subsubsection Heaviside’s Cover–up Method (HCUM)

    Problem 19.2.1.1.
    By completing the square in the denominator and making the substitution \(P-50=50\sin (\theta )\) integrate the left side of equation (19.32) and show that
    \begin{equation} \frac{1}{50}\ln \left[ \sqrt{\frac{100-P}{P}} \right] = 0.0001t+C\text{.}\tag{19.34} \end{equation}
    As you have just seen, integrating the left side of equation (19.32) is entirely within the range of our current skill set but it is rather daunting. And we’re not even finished yet. We must still solve equation (19.34) for \(P(t)\text{.}\) Before we do that we’d really like to find a simpler way to compute the integral.
    Here’s a simpler way. Observe that the fraction \(\frac{1}{P(100-P)}\) can be decomposed as
    \begin{equation} \frac{1}{P(100-P)}=\frac{\frac{-1}{100}}{P}+\frac{\frac{1}{100}}{100-P}\text{.}\tag{19.35} \end{equation}
    With the identity (19.35) in hand we can integrate the right side of equation (19.32) as follows.
    \begin{align*} \int \frac{1}{P(100-P)}\dx{x} \amp{}= \int\frac{\frac{1}{100}}{P}+\frac{\frac{1}{100}}{100-P}\dx{x} \\ \amp{}= \frac{1}{100} \left[ -\ln\left(\frac{1}{P}\right)+\ln\left(\frac{1}{100-P}\right) \right]\\ \amp{}= \frac{1}{100} \ln \left( \frac{P}{100-P} \right) \end{align*}
    Combining this with equation (19.33) we see that
    \begin{equation} -\frac{1}{100} \ln \left( \frac{100-P}{P} \right) = \int 0.0001\dx{t} = 0.0001t+C\tag{19.36} \end{equation}
    which is much easier to solve for \(P(t)\) than equation (19.34).
    Problem 19.2.1.2.
    (a)
    Confirm equation (19.35).
    (b)
    Use the result of part (a) to show that
    \begin{equation*} \int \frac{\dx{P}}{P(100-P)}=-\frac{1}{100}\ln \left( \frac{100-P}{P} \right)+C_2 \end{equation*}
    (c)
    Use the result of part (b) to show that equation (19.32) yields
    \begin{equation*} P(t)=\frac{100}{1+Ke^{-0.001t}} \end{equation*}
    where \(K\) is an arbitrary constant.
    (d)
    Use the initial condition in IVP (19.31) to show that in part (c) \(K=9\text{,}\) and then confirm that you get the same solution when you solve equation (19.34) for \(P(t)\text{.}\)
    (e)
    Graph \(P(t)\) from part (c) and confirm that it has the same general shape that we discovered in Subsection 11.1.1.
    This is much simpler but the roadblock is this: where did equation (19.35) come from? How did we find this decomposition of the fraction \(\frac{1}{P(100-P)}\text{?}\)
    We found equation (19.35) by an algebraic technique called the Partial Fraction Decomposition or PFD. There are several places in mathematics where it is useful to break a single rational expresstion into the sum of simpler rational expressions. We will be using it to simplify the integration when the integrand is a rational function.
    A fully general statement of the PFD is given in Theorem 19.2.2.11 but as you can see the general statement is quite comprehensive. Moreover the statement of the theorem doesn’t help us use it as a practical tool. So we will work through a number of increasingly complex examples to build things up slowly.
    To begin, if we have an expression of the form \(\frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}\) where \(a\text{,}\) and \(b\) are known constants, and \(a\neq b\text{,}\) then the PFD says that there are constants \(A\) and \(B\) such that as
    \begin{equation} \frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}=\frac{A}{x-a}+\frac{B}{x-b}\text{.}\tag{19.37} \end{equation}
    Our task is to find values for \(A\) and \(B\text{.}\)
    Take note of the following fact: The right side of equation (19.37) consists of rational functions where the degree of the numerator is one less than the degree of the denominator. Since the numerators are all constants (polynomials of degree zero) and the denominators are all linear (polynomials of degree one) this is, at the moment, a rather bombastic way to state this fact. However in Subsection 19.2.2 we will find it very useful.
    There are a couple of ways we can find \(A\) and \(B\text{.}\) What we’d like to do is find the simplest, least difficult to implement method we can. After all, we’re not here to do fraction decompositions. This is just one step toward computing an integral and we’d like for the decomposition step to be as simple and painless as possible.
    Conceptually the simplest method available is to simply to choose values for \(x\text{.}\) This is a valid approach because the equation
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}=\frac{A}{x-a}+\frac{B}{x-b} \end{equation*}
    is actually an identity — it will be true no matter what the value of \(x\) is.
    If we choose \(x=0\) we get
    \begin{equation*} Ab+Ba =-1\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and if we choose \(x=1\) we get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{(1-a)(1-b)}=\frac{A}{1-a} + \frac{B}{1-b} \end{equation*}
    and we have a system of two equations in the unknowns \(A\) and \(B\) which we can solve.
    Drill 19.2.1.3.
    In this form the equations
    \begin{equation*} Ab+Ba =-1 \end{equation*}
    and,
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{(1-a)(1-b)}=\frac{A}{1-a} + \frac{B}{1-b} \end{equation*}
    are a little intimidating. But it’s not really as bad as it looks. Clear the fractions and then solve for \(A\) and \(B\text{.}\)
    The method we’ve just outlined will work, and it will always work. But this is only our first example of the PFD and you can surely see that that if we have more than two constants the difficulty of solving for the coefficients will increase rapidly. But again, this method works and it will always work, so it is something we can always fall back on if we can’t find something simpler.
    Since the method just outlined will always work one possible way to lighten the load is to look for values of \(x\) that will make the resulting equations simpler to solve. Sadly, no obvious choice presents itself, but hold that thought for a bit. We’ll return to it.
    Another possible method is to simply clear the fractions and compare like terms. (In fact, our previous method can be made a little more palatable by clearing the fractions first.) Thus
    \begin{equation*} (x-a)(x-b)\cdot\left[\frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}\right]=\left[ \frac{A}{x-a}+\frac{B}{x-b}\right]\cdot(x-a)(x-b)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Multiplying through and cancelling, we have
    \begin{equation} 1=A(x-b)+B(x-a)\tag{19.38} \end{equation}
    so that
    \begin{equation*} \textcolor{red}{0}x+ \textcolor{blue}{1}=(\textcolor{red}{A+B})x + \left(\textcolor{blue}{-Ab-aB}\right) \text{.} \end{equation*}
    Comparing the coefficient of \(x\) and the constant terms we see that we need to solve the following system of two equations in the two unknowns \(A\) and \(B\)
    \begin{align} A+B\amp{}=0\tag{19.39}\\ Ab+Ba \amp{}=-1.\notag \end{align}
    Drill 19.2.1.4.
    Solve the system of equations (19.39) and show that you get the same solution that you got in Drill 19.2.1.3.
    This is a little better. We’ve still got a system of two equations to solve, but it seems to be a little more manageable. Still, as the problems get more complex the number of equations in our system will grow. We’d still like to find something simpler than this if we can.
    But wait! Take another look at equation (19.38). With a judicious use of that formula you can actually compute this PFD almost completely in your head. It’s that simple. Really.
    Because equation (19.38) is an identity, — it is true regardless of which value of \(x\) we choose. So of we choose \(x=a\) we get
    \begin{equation*} A=\frac{1}{a-b} \end{equation*}
    immediately. Similarly choosing \(x=b\) gives \(B=\frac{1}{b-a}\text{,}\) also immediately.Thus the PFD of \(\frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}\) is
    \begin{equation} \frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}= \frac{\frac{1}{a-b}}{x-a}+\frac{\frac{1}{b-a}}{x-b}\text{.}\tag{19.40} \end{equation}
    Drill 19.2.1.5.
    Verify the identity (19.40) by adding the fractions on the right side together.

    When he was computing some PFDs related to his work with differential equations Oliver Heaviside
     6 
    https://mathshistory.st-andrews.ac.uk/Biographies/Heaviside/
    (1850—1925) made the following observation. Look closely at the numerators of the fractions the right side of
    \begin{equation} \frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}= \frac{\frac{1}{a-b}}{x-a}+\frac{\frac{1}{b-a}}{x-b}\tag{19.42} \end{equation}
    (this is equation (19.40)). Do you see that the numerator above \(x-a\) is what we would get if we simply replaced \(x\) with \(a\) on the left and “covered up,” (or ignored) the factor \(x-a\text{?}\)
    That is, if \(\frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}= \frac{A}{x-a}+\frac{B}{x-b}\) then
    \begin{equation*} A=\eval{\frac{1}{(\text{covered})(x-b)}}{x}{a}=\frac{1}{a-b}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Similarly
    \begin{equation*} B=\eval{\frac{1}{(x-a)(\text{covered})}}{x}{b}=\frac{1}{b-a}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This is known as Heaviside’s Cover–up Method (HCUM) and it is the simplest way we know to compute the coefficients of the PFD of the rational functions we’ve seen so far.
    The HCUM is a very convenient shortcut. But it is necessary to understand why any shortcut works. Otherwise all we have is the shortcut. If we encounter a problem where the shortcut wont’t work but the underlying principle will, and all we know is the shortcut we can’t make any progress.
    To see why the HCUM works take another look at the decomposition
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}=\frac{A}{x-a}+\frac{B}{x-b}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    If we multiply both sides by \(x-a\text{,}\) we get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{x-b}=A+\frac{B(x-a)}{x-b} \end{equation*}
    which holds as long as \(x\ne b\text{.}\) Notice that \(\frac{1}{x-b} = \frac{1}{(\text{covered})(x-b)}\text{.}\) Next, if we take \(x=a\) we get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{(\text{covered})(a-b)} =\frac{1}{a-b}=A+\frac{B(a-a)}{a-b}=A\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Problem 19.2.1.10.

    It is straightforward to generalize this result to an arbitrary number of linear factors.

    Subsubsection Linear Factors

    Example 19.2.2.1.
    Heaviside’s method, as we have used it so far, will not work on the rational function \(\frac{x^2}{(x-2)(x-3)}\text{.}\) If you try to apply it you’ll get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x^2}{(x-2)(x-3)} = \frac{-4}{x-2}+\frac{9}{x-3} \end{equation*}
    which is wrong. Try it and see.
    The difficulty here is that, as a practical matter, the PFD requires that we want the degree of the numerator of our rational function to be strictly less than the degree of the denominator. (This is somewhat analogous to putting a fraction in “lowest terms.”) In this example the degree of the numerator is
    \begin{equation*} \deg\left((x-2)(x-3)\right)=\deg\left(x^2-5x+6\right)=2 \end{equation*}
    which is the same as the degree of the numerator: \(\deg\left(x^2\right)=2\text{.}\) So the PFD, as we’ve used it so far, doesn’t apply.
    At first this looks like it will be a major obstacle but it really isn’t too bad. There are two ways we can proceed from here.
    Dividing First: The first is simply to do the polynomial division indicated. In practice that means multiplying out the denominator first and then dividing:
    \begin{align} \frac{x^2}{(x-2)(x-3)} = \frac{x^2}{x^2-5x+6} \amp{}= 1+ \frac{5x-6}{x^2-5x+6}\notag\\ \amp{}=1+\frac{5x-6}{(x-2)(x-3)}\tag{19.43} \end{align}
    From there we can compute the PFD of \(\frac{5x-6}{(x-2)(x-3)}\) as before, finally obtaining
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x^2}{x^2-5x+6} = 1+\frac{-4}{x-2}+\frac{9}{x-3}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Dividing Last: The second is to factor out the numerator:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x^2}{(x-2)(x-3)} = x^2 \left[ \frac{1}{(x-2)(x-3)} \right] \text{,} \end{equation*}
    compute the PFD of \(\frac{1}{(x-2)(x-3)}\) as before. This gives
    \begin{align} \frac{x^2}{(x-2)(x-3)} \amp{}= x^2 \left[ \frac{-1}{x-2}+\frac{1}{x-3} \right]\notag\\ \amp{}=\frac{-x^2}{x-2}+\frac{x^2}{x-3}\tag{19.44}\\ \amp{}= -x-2-\frac{4}{x-2} +x+3+\frac{9}{x-3}\notag\\ \amp{}=1-\frac{4}{x-2}+\frac{9}{x-3}.\notag \end{align}
    Whether you divide first or last is entirely up to you. We (the authors) prefer dividing last because it doesn’t involve factoring and then un–factoring the denominator, and because we only ever have to divide by a linear expression, which we find less burdensome.
    However, we do advise that you pick one method that you like and use it consistently, at least until you have fully mastered the Partial Fraction Decomposition.

    Example 19.2.2.4.
    Heaviside’s Cover–up Method will also fail on this function:
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x^2+x-3}{x^2(x-1)}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    If you apply it you’ll get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x^2+x-3}{x^2(x-1)} = \frac{3}{x^2} + \frac{-1}{x-1} \end{equation*}
    which is incorrect. Check it and see.
    The problem here is the factor \(x^2\) in the denominator. If you look back at the problems and examples we’ve looked at so far you’ll see that each distinct factor which appeared in the denominator of the fraction on the left side also appeared in one of the denominators on the right side. In this case the distinct factors are \(x^2\) and \(x-1\) so the decomposition will be
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x^2+x-3}{x^2(x-1)} = \frac{\text{something}}{x^2} + \frac{C}{x-1} \text{.} \end{equation*}
    But now the question is, “What is the something?” Up until now none of the distinct factors in the denominator appeared with a power higher than one. So a factor of \(x^2\) is altogether new and different.
    Looking back at our earlier, and simpler, examples we see that every case the terms on the left were rational functions where the degree of the numerator (which was always \(0\) in those examples) was one less than the degree of the denominator (which was always \(1\) in those examples). If we keep that pattern here then it appears that the decomposition must be
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x^2+x-3}{x^2(x-1)} = \frac{Ax+B}{x^2} + \frac{C}{x-1} \end{equation*}
    because
    \begin{equation*} \deg (Ax+B)=1 \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \deg(x^2) =2\text{.} \end{equation*}
    We can break this up a bit more by observing that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{Ax+B}{x^2} =\frac{A}{x}+\frac{B}{x^2} \end{equation*}
    so we need to find \(A\text{,}\) \(B\text{,}\) and \(C\) where
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x^2+x-3}{x^2(x-1)} = \frac{A}{x}+\frac{B}{x^2} + \frac{C}{x-1} \text{.} \end{equation*}
    The values \(B=3\text{,}\) and \(C=-1\) can still be found using the HCUM, so we have
    \begin{equation} \frac{x^2+x-3}{x^2(x-1)} = \frac{A}{x} + \frac{3}{x^2}- \frac{1}{x-1}\text{.}\tag{19.45} \end{equation}
    To find \(A\) recall that equation (19.45) is an identity so it is true regardless of the value of \(x\text{.}\) Since \(A\) is the only parameter left we can find it by setting \(x=2\) (or anything else). This gives \(\frac{2}{2^2(2-1)} = \frac{A}{2} + \frac{3}{2^2}- \frac{1}{2-1} \text{,}\) or \(A=2\text{.}\) So the full decomposition is
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x^2+x-3}{x^2(x-1)} = \frac{2}{x}+ \frac{3}{x^2}-\frac{1}{x-1}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Example 19.2.2.7. The PFD With Repeated Linear Factors.
    Let’s finish up the decomposition of \(\frac{x}{(x-1)^2(x+2)^2}\) from part (b) of Problem 19.2.2.6. From the HCUM You should have found that \(B=\frac19\) and \(D=\frac{-2}{9}\) so that
    \begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{x}{(x-1)^2(x+2)^2}= \frac{A}{x-1} + \frac{\frac19}{(x-1)^2} +\frac{C}{x+2} -\frac{\frac29}{(x+2)^2} \end{equation*}
    When we reached this point in Example 19.2.2.4 our next step was to simply choose a value for \(x\text{.}\) We could do that here as well but we’d have to choose two values for \(x\) thereby generating a pair of equations to solve and we’ve already seen that there are more efficient methods for computing the PFD than solving a system of equations.
    Instead we’ll isolate all of the terms with our unknowns on one side of the equation, thus:
    \begin{equation} \displaystyle \frac{x}{(x-1)^2(x+2)^2} - \frac{\frac19}{(x-1)^2} +\frac{\frac29}{(x+2)^2} = \frac{A}{x-1} +\frac{C}{x+2}\tag{19.46} \end{equation}
    It is clear that the right side of equation (19.46) is the PFD of some rational function whose denominator is \((x-1)(x+2)\text{.}\) That is, if we were asked to begin the process of computing the PFD of
    \begin{equation*} \frac{P(x)}{(x-1)(x+2)} \end{equation*}
    for some polynomial \(P(x)\) then our first step would be to write down the right side of equation (19.46). Therefore it must be that
    \begin{equation} \displaystyle \frac{x}{(x-1)^2(x+2)^2} - \frac{\frac19}{(x-1)^2} +\frac{\frac29}{(x+2)^2} = \frac{P(x)}{(x-1)(x+2)}\tag{19.47} \end{equation}
    for some polynomial \(P(x)\text{,}\) and if we can find that polynomial we can compute \(A\) and \(C\) by the HCUM.
    We can find \(P(x)\) by essentially the same method we used to find the constant \(A\) in Example 19.2.2.4. First we clear the fraction on the right
    \begin{equation} \frac{x}{(x-1)(x+2)} - \frac19 \left( \frac{x+2}{x-1} \right) +\frac29 \left( \frac{x-1}{x+2} \right) = P(x)\tag{19.48} \end{equation}
    Since the left side is guaranteed to reduce to the polynomial it is tempting to find a common denominator and add the three fractions on the left together. Since we have to end up with the polynomial \(P(x)\) it must be that once the addition is done there will be a factor in the top that will cancel with the common denominator.
    That will work. Give it a try if you want. Good luck.
    But we’re trying to find the most efficient method we can so instead we’re going to make judicious use of the knowledge that we must end up with a polynomial.
    First think about the expression in the second term on the left: \(\frac{x+2}{x-1}\text{.}\) If we perform this division we get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x+2}{x-1} = 1 +\frac{3}{x-1}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Similarly if we perform the division in the third term we get \(\frac{x-1}{x+2}= 1-\frac{3}{x+2}\text{.}\) Putting these back into equation (19.48) we have
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x}{(x-1)(x+2)} - \frac19 \left( 1 +\frac{3}{x-1} \right) +\frac29 \left( 1-\frac{3}{x+2} \right) = P(x) \end{equation*}
    or
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x}{(x-1)(x+2)} - \frac19 -\frac{\frac13}{x-1} +\frac29 -\frac{\frac23}{x+2} = P(x) \text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since \(P(x)\) must be a polynomial it follows that \(\frac{x}{(x-1)(x-2)}-\frac{\frac13 }{x-1}-\frac{\frac23 }{x+2}\) (everything that isn’t a polynomial) must sum to zero. So we can ignore them and conclude that our polynomial is given by the other terms. So
    \begin{equation*} P(x)=-\frac19 + \frac29 =\frac19\text{.} \end{equation*}
    To finish the decomposition we observe that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{\frac19 }{(x-1)(x+2)} = \frac{A}{x-1}+\frac{C}{x+2} \end{equation*}
    and from the HCUM we see that \(A=\frac{1}{27} \) and \(C=-\frac19\text{.}\)
    Having found all of the constants we conclude that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x}{(x-1)^2(x+2)^2}= \frac{\frac{1}{27}}{x-1} + \frac{\frac19 }{(x-1)^2} -\frac{\frac19}{x+2} -\frac{\frac29 }{(x+2)^2} \text{.} \end{equation*}

    Subsubsection Irreducible Quadratic Factors

    In the problems and examples we’ve encountered so far whenever there was a quadratic factor in the denominator we were able to either factor it into two distinct linear factors (e.g. \((x^2-x+6) = (x+2)(x-3)\) or into one such factor, squared, e.g. \((x^2+6x+9)=(x+3)^2\text{.}\) Unfortunately we cannot yet compute the integral
    \begin{equation*} \int \frac{1}{(x-2)(x^2+1)}\dx{x}\text{,} \end{equation*}
    because the factor \(x^2+1\) cannot be factored (over the real numbers). So we’ll need to extend our procedure to account for this.
    Comment.
    But it can be factored over the complex numbers. We will address this at the end of this section.
    From our previous work we know that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{(x-2)(x^2+1)}= \frac{A}{x-2}+\frac{Bx+C}{x^2+1} \end{equation*}
    so by the HCUM \(A=\frac15 \text{.}\) Therefore
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{(x-2)(x^2+1)}= \frac{\frac15}{x-2}+ \frac{Bx+C}{x^2+1} \end{equation*}
    which, as we keep repeating, is an identity. If we isolate the expression \(Bx+C\) on the right side of the equation then the left side will reduce to a linear expression in \(x\text{.}\) We can then simply compare the coefficients.
    Multiplying both sides by \(x^2+1\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{x-2}=\frac15 \left(\frac{x^2+1}{x-2}\right)+(Bx+C) \end{equation*}
    and isolating \(Bx+C\) gives
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{x-2}-\frac15\left( \frac{x^2+1}{x-2}\right)=Ax+B\text{,} \end{equation*}
    and if we simplify the expression on the left we will see that
    \begin{equation*} Bx+C=-\frac15x +\frac25 \text{,} \end{equation*}
    so
    \begin{equation} \frac{1}{(x-2)(x^2+1)}= \frac15\left(\frac{1}{x-2}\right)- \frac15\left( \frac{x}{x^2+1}\right) -\frac25\left(\frac{1}{x^2+1}\right)\text{.}\tag{19.49} \end{equation}
    Problem 19.2.2.10.
    (a)
    Confirm that equation (19.49) is correct by getting a common denominator and adding the terms on the right together.
    (b)
    Compute the integral \(\displaystyle \int\frac{1}{(x-2)(x^2+1)}\dx{x}\text{.}\)
    Reasoning by analogy it seems reasonable to speculate that the PFD of \(\frac{1}{x^2(x^2+1)^3(x^2+9)(x-5)} \) will be
    \begin{align} \frac{1}{x^2(x^2+1)^3(x^2+9(x-5))} = \frac{A}{x}+\frac{B}{x^2}+\amp{}\frac{Cx+D}{x^2+1}+\frac{Ex+F}{(x^2+1)^2}\tag{19.50}\\ +\frac{Gx+H}{(x^2+1)^3} \amp{}+ \frac{Ix+J}{x^2+9}+\frac{K}{x-5},\notag \end{align}
    and that is correct.
    The good news is that we are done. Equation (19.50) is an example of the most general form that a Partial Fraction Decomposition can have. Beyond that they just get bigger.
    The HCUM can still be used to compute \(B\) and \(K\text{.}\) It can even be extended to compute \(I\) and \(J\text{,}\) but for the kinds of problems we will be encountering that extension is not worth the effort.
    So, at long last here is the full statement of the Partial Fraction Decomposition.
    Theorem 19.2.2.11. The Partial Fraction Decomposition (PFD).
    If \(P(x)\text{,}\) \(Q(x)\text{,}\) and \(R(x)\) are polynomials having no non–trivial common factors with \(\deg(P)\lt\deg(Q)+ \deg(R)\) then there exist polynomials \(A(x)\) and \(B(x)\text{,}\) with \(\deg(A)\lt\deg(Q)\) and \(\deg(B)\lt\deg(R)\text{,}\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)\cdot{}R(x)} = \frac{A(x)}{Q(x)}+\frac{B(x)}{R(x)}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    You can see why we did not begin this section by stating this theorem. The language is so terse that it is actually hard to recognize this theorem as describing everything we have been through in this section. Nevertheless, Theorem 19.2.2.11 covers every kind of decomposition we have looked at in this section.
    DIGRESSION: Avoiding the Irreducible Case by Allowing Complex Numbers.
    In his PhD dissertation C. F. Gauss
     7 
    https://mathshistory.st-andrews.ac.uk/Biographies/Gauss/
    (1777–1855) proved that every polynomial can be completely factored into (possibly repeated) linear factors. These days this is known by the rather pompous name The Fundamental Theorem of Algebra.
    In view of this theorem an obvious question presents itself: “Why did we bother with the Irreducible Quadratic case when discussing the Partial Fraction Decomposition?” That is, why did we (the authors) tell you that \(x^2+1\) can’t be factored, when Gauss’s theorem clearly says that it can.
    Recall that in the first paragraph of the subsection Irreducible Quadratic Factors we were careful to state that “\(x^2+1\)” cannot be factored over the real numbers. This is true but it can be factored over the complex numbers (real numbers plus imaginary numbers).
    While Gauss’s proof didn’t make explicit use of complex numbers they were implicit in his approach and his proof was valid. This was quite a puzzle at the time because while his proof said that \(x^2+1\) could be factored, the nature of the factors was not clear. When Gauss proved the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra the complex numbers were not well understood at all and had not been accepted. The Fundamental Theorem of Algebra is one of the results that forced mathematicians to undertake the study of the complex numbers and eventually accept them as an extension of the real numbers.
    Problem 19.2.2.12.
    (a)
    Let \(i=\sqrt{-1}\) as in Section 8.13 and show that
    \begin{equation*} x^2+1 = (x-i)(x+i)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    (b)
    Use the result of part (a) to show that
    \begin{equation*} \int \frac{1}{x^2+1}\dx{x} = \frac{i}{2}\ln \left( \frac{x+i}{x-i} \right) +C \end{equation*}
    (c)
    Recall that we’ve already seen that \(\int \frac{1}{x^2+1}\dx{x} = \inverse \tan(x) +C\text{.}\) In view of the result in part (b), do you believe that
    \begin{equation} \frac{i}{2}\ln \left( \frac{x+i}{x-i} \right) +C = \inverse \tan(x) +C\text{?}\tag{19.51} \end{equation}
    Notice that we have not asked if this is true, only if you believe it. Hence there is no right or wrong answer to the following question: Explain why you do or don’t believe that equation (19.51). Give mathematical reasons to support your opinion either way.
    Example 19.2.2.13.
    By allowing the use of complex numbers we can use the HCUM to compute
    \begin{align*} \frac{x-2}{x(x^2+1)}=\frac{x-2}{x(x-i)(x+i)} \amp{}=\frac{-2}{x}+\frac{\frac{i-2}{i(2i)}}{x-i}+\frac{\frac{i+2}{i(-2i)}}{x+i}\\ \amp{}= \frac{-2}{x}+\frac{\frac{i-2}{-2}}{x-i}+\frac{\frac{-i-2}{-2}}{x+i}\\ \amp{}=\frac{-2}{x}+\frac{1-\frac12i}{x-i}+\frac{1+\frac12i }{x+i}. \end{align*}
    But unfortunately, in this course we traditionally confine our attention to the real numbers and treat the complex numbers as if they are somehow unsavory, even when using them would make more sense. To remove the complex numbers from our decomposition we compute the sum \(\frac{1-\frac12i}{x-i}+\frac{1+\frac12i }{x+i}\) as follows
    \begin{equation} \frac{1-\frac12i}{x-i}+\frac{1+\frac12i }{x+i}=\frac{2x+1}{x^2+1}\text{.}\tag{19.52} \end{equation}
    Thus our decomposition over the real numbers (rather than the complex numbers is
    \begin{equation} \frac{x-2}{x(x^2+1)}=\frac{-2}{x}+\frac{2x+1}{x^2+1}\text{.}\tag{19.53} \end{equation}
    Problem 19.2.2.14.
    (a)
    Confirm that equation (19.52) is correct.
    (b)
    Confirm that equation (19.53) is correct.
