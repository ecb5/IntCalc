    Section 19.2 The Partial Fraction Decomposition

    When we analyzed the Logistic Equation in Section 11.1 we were unable to find a formula for \(P(t)\text{.}\) But, by analyzing the IVP directly we were able to generate a “qualitative” graph of \(P9t)\text{.}\) That is, we were able to get a general sense of the shape of the graph of \(P(t)\) even if the coordinates of particular points were unavailable.
    While a qualitative graph is better than nothing, it would be more useful to have an explicit formula for \(P(t)\text{,}\) if we can find it. With integration we now have the tool we need to find such a formula.

    Subsection 19.2.1 Simple Decompositions

    The Logistic Equation, Redux.

    Starting with the Logistic Equation,
    \begin{align} \dfdx{P}{t} \amp{}= 0.1P \left( 1-\frac{P}{100} \right),\amp{}P(0)\amp{}=10,\tag{19.31} \end{align}
    the first step is to separate the variables:
    \begin{equation} \frac{\dx{P}}{P(100-P)}=0.0001\dx{t}\text{.}\tag{19.32} \end{equation}
    If we integrate both sides of this equation the result will be an equation in which \(P(t)\) appears. If we can solve that for \(P(t)\) we will have our formula for \(P(t)\text{.}\)
    Integrating the right sides presents no difficulties:
    \begin{equation} \int 0.0001\dx{t} = 0.0001t+C\text{.}\tag{19.33} \end{equation}
    But at our current level of skill with integration the only option that presents itself is to to complete the square in the denominator and apply the appropriate trigonometric substitution.

    Subsubsection Heaviside’s Cover–up Method (HCUM)

    Drill 19.2.1.1.
    By completing the square in the denominator and making the substitution \(\frac{P-50}{50}=\sin (\theta )\) integrate the left side of equation (19.32) and show that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{\sqrt{1- \left( \frac{P-50}{50} \right)^2}} + \frac{\frac{P-50}{50}}{\sqrt{1- \left( \frac{P-50}{50} \right)^2}} = 0.0001t+C\text{.} \end{equation*}
    As you can see, while it is entirely within the range of our current skill set, finding \(P(t)\) that way will be rather daunting. We’d really like to find a simpler way.
    Here’s a simpler way. Observe that the fraction \(\frac{1}{P(100-P)}\) can be decomposed as
    \begin{equation} \frac{1}{P(100-P)}=\frac{\frac{-1}{100}}{P}+\frac{\frac{1}{100}}{100-P}\text{.}\tag{19.34} \end{equation}
    Drill 19.2.1.2.
    Confirm (19.34).
    With the identity (19.34) in hand we can integrate the right side of equation (19.32) as follows.
    \begin{align*} \int \frac{1}{P(100-P)}\dx{x} \amp{}= \int\frac{\frac{-1}{100}}{P}+\frac{\frac{1}{100}}{100-P}\dx{x} \\ \amp{}= -\frac{1}{100} \left[ -\ln\left(\frac{1}{P}\right)+\ln\left(\frac{1}{100-P}\right) \right]\\ \amp{}= -\frac{1}{100} \ln \left( \frac{100-P}{P} \right) \end{align*}
    Combining this with equation (19.33) we see that
    \begin{equation} -\frac{1}{100} \ln \left( \frac{100-P}{P} \right) = \int 0.0001\dx{t} = 0.0001t+C\tag{19.35} \end{equation}
    which is much easier to solve for \(P(t)\text{.}\)
    Problem 19.2.1.3.
    (a)
    Solve equation (19.35) and show that \(P(t) = \frac{100}{Ke^{-0.01t}+1}\) where \(K\) is an arbitrary constant.
    (b)
    Use the initial condition in IVP (19.31) to show that in part (a) \(K=9\text{,}\) so that
    \begin{equation*} P(t) = \frac{100}{9e^{-0.01t}+1} \end{equation*}
    (c)
    Graph \(P(t)\) from part (b) and confirm that it has the same general shape that we discovered in Subsection 11.1.1.
    This is great but the roadblock is, where did equation (19.34) come from? How did we find this decomposition of the fraction \(\frac{1}{P(100-P)}\text{?}\)
    We found equation (19.34) by an algebraic technique called the Partial Fraction Decomposition or PFD. There are several places in mathematics where it is useful to break a single rational expresstion into the sum of simpler rational expression. We will use it to simplify integration in certain cases.
    A fully general statement of the PFD is given in Theorem 19.2.3.3 but as you can see the general statement is quite comprehensive. Moreover the statement of the theorem doesn’t indicate how it can be used as a practical tool. So we will work through a number of increasingly complex examples to build things up slowly.
    To begin, if we have an expression of the form \(\frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}\) where \(a\text{,}\) and \(b\) are known constants then the PFD says that there are constants \(A\) and \(B\) such that as
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}=\frac{A}{x-a}+\frac{B}{x-b}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Our task is to find values for \(A\) and \(B\text{.}\)
    There are a couple of ways we can find \(A\) and \(B\text{.}\) What we’d like to do is find the simplest, least difficult to implement method we can. After all, we’re not here to do fraction decompositions. This is just one step toward computing an integral and we’d like for the decomposition step to be as simple and painless as possible.
    Conceptually the simplest method available is to simply to choose values for \(x\text{.}\) This is a valid approach because the equation
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}=\frac{A}{x-a}+\frac{B}{x-b} \end{equation*}
    is actually an identity — it will be true no matter what the value of \(x\) is. But the constants \(A\) and \(B\) are fixed.
    If we choose \(x=0\) we get
    \begin{equation*} Ab+Ba =-1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    If we choose \(x=1\) we get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{(1-a)(1-b)}=\frac{A}{1-a} + \frac{B}{1-b} \end{equation*}
    and we have a system of two equations in the unknowns \(A\) and \(B\) which we can solve.
    Drill 19.2.1.4.
    In the form we have them thus far the equations
    \begin{equation*} Ab+Ba =-1 \end{equation*}
    and, especially
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{(1-a)(1-b)}=\frac{A}{1-a} + \frac{B}{1-b} \end{equation*}
    are a little scary. But it’s not really as bad as it looks. Clear the fractions and then solve for \(A\) and \(B\text{.}\)
    The method we’ve just outlined will work, and it will always work. But this is only our first example of the PFD and you can surely see that that for a more complex problem the difficulty of solving for the coefficients can become extreme. But again, this method works and it will always work, so it is something we can always fall back on if we can’t find something simpler.
    Since the method just oulined will work no matter what values we choose for \(x\) one possible way to lighten the load is to look for values of \(x\) that will make the resulting equations simpler to solve. Do you see a choice that will do that?
    Sadly, nothing obvious presents itself. But hold that thought for a bit. We’ll return to it.
    Another possible method available is to simply clear the fractions and compare like terms. (In fact, our previous method can be made a little more palatable by clearing the fractions first.) Thus
    \begin{align} (x-a)(x-b)\cdot\left[\frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}\right]\amp{}=\left[ \frac{A}{x-a}+\frac{B}{x-b}\right]\cdot(x-a)(x-b)\notag\\ 1\amp{}=A(x-b)+B(x-a)\tag{19.36}\\ \textcolor{red}{0}x+ \textcolor{blue}{1}\amp{}=(\textcolor{red}{A+B})x + \left(\textcolor{blue}{-Ab-aB}\right) \notag \end{align}
    Comparing the coefficients of \(x\) and the constant terms we see that we need to solve the following system of two equations in the two unknowns \(A\) and \(B\)
    \begin{align} A+B\amp{}=0\tag{19.37}\\ Ab+Ba \amp{}=-1.\notag \end{align}
    Drill 19.2.1.5.
    Solve the system of equations (19.37) and show that you get the same solution that you got in Drill 19.2.1.4.
    This is a little better. We’ve still got a system of two equations to solve, but at least it seems to be a little more manageable. Still, as the problems get more complex the number of equations in our system will grow. We’d still like to find something simpler than this if we can.
    But wait! Take another look at equation (19.36). With a judicious use of that formula you can actually compute this PFD almost completely in your head. It’s that simple. Really.
    In the paragraph following Drill 19.2.1.4 we emphasized that equation (19.36) is an identity, that it is true regardless of value of \(x\text{.}\) That is still true and we have equation (19.36) in three equivalent forms. The second one is
    \begin{equation*} 1=A(x-b)+B(x-a) \end{equation*}
    and, as we keep saying, this is an identity — it is true regardless of which values of \(x\) we choose. Choosing \(x=a\) and \(x=b\) gives us
    \begin{align*} A=\frac{1}{a-b} \amp{}\amp{}\text{ and }\amp{}\amp{} B=\frac{1}{b-a} \end{align*}
    respectively. So the PFD of \(\frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}\) is
    \begin{equation} \frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}= \frac{\frac{1}{a-b}}{x-a}+\frac{\frac{1}{b-a}}{x-b}\text{.}\tag{19.38} \end{equation}
    Drill 19.2.1.6.
    Verify the identity (19.38) by adding the fractions on the right side together.
    Drill 19.2.1.7.
    Compute each integral by decomposing the integrand using any of the methods you’ve seen so far.
    1. \(\displaystyle \int\frac{1}{(x-2)(x-1)}\dx{x}\)
    2. \(\displaystyle \int \frac{1} {x(x-100 )}\dx{x}\)
    3. \(\displaystyle \int \frac{4}{(x+2 )(x-2 )}\dx{x}\)
    4. \(\displaystyle \int \frac{6}{(x-3 )(x+10 )}\dx{x}\)
    5. \(\displaystyle \int \frac{\frac{2}{10}}{(x-10 )(x-100 )}\dx{x}\)
    6. \(\displaystyle \int \frac{1}{(2x- 5)(3x- 1)}\dx{x}\)
    Problem 19.2.1.8.
    Compute each integral by decomposing the integrand using any of the methods you’ve seen so far.
    (a)

    When he was computing some PFDs related to his work solving differential equations Oliver Heaviside
     6 
    https://mathshistory.st-andrews.ac.uk/Biographies/Heaviside/
    (1850—1925) made the following observation. Look closely at the numerators of the fractions the right side of
    \begin{equation} \frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)}= \frac{\frac{1}{a-b}}{x-a}+\frac{\frac{1}{b-a}}{x-b}\tag{19.40} \end{equation}
    (this is equation (19.38)). Do you see that the numerator above \(x-a\) is what we would get if we simply replaced \(x\) with \(a\) on the left and “covered up” \(x-a\text{?}\)

    \begin{equation*} A=\eval{\frac{1}{(\text{covered})(x-b)}}{x}{a}=\frac{1}{a-b}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Similarly
    \begin{equation*} B=\eval{\frac{1}{(x-a)(\text{covered})}}{x}{b}=\frac{1}{b-a}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This is known as Heaviside’s Cover–up Method and it is the simplest way we know to compute the coefficients of the PFD of the rational functions we’ve seen so far.

    As we’ve mentioned before, all mathematical models are simplifications of reality, and typically are modified to take into account more complexities. For example, consider the following modification of the logistic model above
    \begin{equation*} \dfdx{y}{t}=.01y\left(1-\frac{y}{100}\right)\left(\frac{y}{10}-1\right)=0.00001y(100-y)(y-10) \end{equation*}
    In this case, \(10\) is called the minimum viability level of the population. (Why?) Solving this would require that we compute the integral
    \begin{equation*} \int \frac{\dx{y}}{y(100-y)(y-10)}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    This form of this integral does not suggest any obvious trigonometric substitution so we will need a new idea.
    The next technique is wholly algebraic in nature. The trick to try and separate the integrand into partial fractions each of which will be easier to integrate than the original. For example, consider the expression \(\frac{1}{y(100-y)}\text{.}\) With begin by making a reasonable, educated guess that such a fraction can be written as \(\frac{1}{y(100-y)}=\frac{A}{y}+\frac{B}{100-y}\text{,}\) where \(A\) and \(B\) are constant to be determined. We can see if this guess pans out by combining the partial fractions to obtain the original.
    \begin{equation*} \frac{A}{y}+\frac{B}{100-y}=\frac{A(100-y)+By}{y(100-y)}=\frac{100A+(B-A)y}{y(100-y)} \end{equation*}
    Comparing this with \(\frac{1}{y(100-y)}\) we see that if they are to be equal we must have \(100A=1\) and \(B-A=0\text{.}\) Therefore \(A=B=\frac{1}{100}\text{.}\) Thus
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{y(100-y)}=\frac{1/100}{y}+\frac{1/100}{100-y}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Substituting into the original integral we see that
    \begin{align*} \int\frac{\dx{y}}{y(100-y)}\amp =\int\frac{1/100}{y}\dx{y}+\int\frac{1/100}{100-y}\dx{y}\\ \amp =\frac{1}{100}\left(\int\frac{\dx{y}}{y}+\int\frac{\dx{y}}{100-y}\right) \end{align*}
    and so
    \begin{gather} \int\frac{\dx{y}}{y(100-y)}=\frac{1}{100}\left(\ln(y)-\ln(100-y)\right)+C\tag{19.41} \end{gather}

    Comment.

    This isn’t really a guess. There is a theorem from algebra that says that there will always be constants \(A\) and \(B\) such that \(\frac{1}{y(100-y)}=\frac{A}{y}+\frac{B}{100-y}\) but since we haven’t discussed this yet we’ll just roll with it for now.

    The Partial Fraction Decomposition Now that we’ve seen that decomposing a ratio of polynomials (these are called rational functions) into a sum of partial fractions is possible, we’ll look for a systematic way of doing this for any rational function. Our method will hinge on the following theoretical, algebraic fact.
    Suppose we have two polynomials \(p(x)\) and \(q(x)\) which have no common factors (other than constants). Then there are polynomials \(r(x)\) and \(s(x)\) such that
    \begin{equation*} r(x)\cdot{}p(x)+s(x)\cdot{}q(x)=1\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Example 19.2.3.2.

    For example notice that if \(a\) and \(b\) are constants then \(x+a\) and \(x+b\) have no common factors. Since \((x+a)-(x+b)=a-b\) it follows that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{a-b}(x+a)+\frac{-1}{a-b}(x+b)=1\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Comment.
    Notice that since \(\frac{1}{a-b}\) and \(\frac{-1}{a-b}\) are constants they are polynomials of degree zero.
    Our approach to the problem in example 19.2.3.2 was somewhat ad–hoc. We will eventualy need a more systematic approach but it will be useful to look at some more examples first. So for now we will use an (educated) guess and check method. Notice that in the example 19.2.3.2 we had
    \begin{align*} \frac{1}{(x+a)(x+b)}\amp =\frac{\frac{1}{a-b}(x+a)+\frac{-1}{a-b}(x+b)}{(x+a)(x+b)}\\ \amp = \frac{\frac{1}{a-b}}{x+b}+\frac{\frac{-1}{a-b}}{x+a}\\ \amp =\frac{\frac{1}{a-b}}{x+b}-\frac{\frac{1}{a-b}}{x+a} \end{align*}
    It is generally true that if \(r(x)\cdot p(x+s(x)\cdot q(x)=1\) then
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{p(x)\cdot{}q(x)}=\frac{r(x)\cdot{}p(x)+s(x)\cdot{}q(x)}{p(x)\cdot{}q(x)}= \frac{s(x)}{p(x)}+\frac{r(x)}{q(x)}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Multiplying by \(t(x)\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \frac{t(x)}{p(x)\cdot{}q(x)}= \frac{t(x)\cdot s(x)}{p(x)}+\frac{t(x)\cdot{}r(x)}{q(x)}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Setting \(A(x)=t(x)\cdot{}s(x)\) and \(B(x)=t(x)\cdot r(x)\) gives
    \begin{gather*} \frac{t(x)}{p(x)\cdot{}q(x)}= \frac{A(x)}{p(x)}+\frac{B(x)}{q(x)}\text{.} \end{gather*}
    Everything we’ve just shown assumes that if \(p(x)\) and \(q(x)\) are known then \(r(x)\) and \(s(x)\) can be found so that \(r(x)\cdot p(x)+ s(x)\cdot q(x)=1\text{.}\) For arbitrary functions this is a very restrictive condition. However as we observed above, if \(p(x)\) and \(q(x)\) are polynomials having only trivial common factors then the existence of \(r(x)\) and \(s(x)\) is guaranteed. Thus for polynomials the Partial Fraction Decomposition is also guaranteed.

    Mathematical Terminology.

    The word “trivial” is very context dependent in mathematics. In this context a “trivial common factor” would be a constant. For example, \(2\) is a trivial common factor of \((2x-4)\) and \(2x+6\text{.}\)
    We formalize this in the following theorem.

    Theorem 19.2.3.3. The Partial Fraction Decomposition (PFD).

    If \(P(x)\text{,}\) \(Q(x)\text{,}\) and \(R(x)\) are polynomials having no non–trivial common factors with \(\deg(P)\lt\deg(Q)+ \deg(R)\) then there exist polynomials \(A(x)\) and \(B(x)\text{,}\) \(\deg{A}\lt\deg{Q}\) and \(\deg(B)\lt\deg(R)\text{,}\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)\cdot{}R(x)} = \frac{A(x)}{Q(x)}+\frac{B(x)}{R(x)}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    As stated in [cross-reference to target(s) "PartialFractionDecomp" missing or not unique] the PFD is very comprehensive. We will
    To extend this idea further, if \(p\left(x\right),\ q\left(x\right),\ r(x)\) have no nontrivial factors, then there are polynomials \(A\left(x\right),\ B\left(x\right),\ C(x)\) with
    \begin{equation*} \frac{t(x)}{p\left(x\right)\cdot q\left(x\right)\cdot r(x)}=\frac{A\left(x\right)}{p\left(x\right)}+\frac{B\left(x\right)}{q\left(x\right)}+\frac{C\left(x\right)}{r\left(x\right)} \end{equation*}
    and this can be extended to any number of factors in the denominator. Again, this can be proven theoretically, and this partial fractions decomposition can be obtained systematically, but we will adopt a guess and check method to find \(A,\ B,\ C\text{.}\) To make our guess a little more educated, the following fact can be employed. You can make peace with this in your own closet.
    If the degree of \(t(x)\) is less than the degree of \(p(x)\cdot q(x)\cdot r(x)\text{,}\) then \(A,B,C\) can be chosen with \(\deg( A)\lt \deg(p), \deg(B)\lt \deg(q), \deg(C)\lt \deg(r)\text{.}\)

    Example 19.2.3.4.

    Recall we had
    \begin{equation*} \int{\frac{dy}{y\left(100-y\right)(y-10)}} \end{equation*}
    To compute this we will find the partial fractions decomposition of \(\frac{1}{y\left(100-y\right)\left(y-10\right)}\text{.}\) We make the educated guess
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{y\left(100-y\right)\left(y-10\right)}=\frac{A}{y}+\frac{B}{100-y}+\frac{C}{y-10} \end{equation*}
    and determine what \(A,B,C\) are. This can be done in a number of ways, but the most direct (and labor intensive) way to do this is to combine the terms in the right-hand side and compare coefficients.
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{y\left(100-y\right)\left(y-10\right)}=\frac{A\left(100-y\right)\left(y-10\right)+By\left(y-10\right)+Cy\left(100-y\right)}{y\left(100-y\right)\left(y-10\right)} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\frac{A\left(-y^2+110y-100\right)+B\left(y^2-10y\right)+C\left(100y-y^2\right)}{y\left(100-y\right)\left(y-10\right)} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\frac{\left(-A+B-C\right)y^2+\left(110A-10B+100C\right)y+\left(-1000A\right)}{y\left(100-y\right)\left(y-10\right)} \end{equation*}
    Since the fractions must be equal and the denominators are the same, the numerators must also be equal. Thus
    \begin{align*} -A+B-C& =0\\ 110A-10B+100C& =0\\ -1000A& =1 \end{align*}
    Solve these equations anyway you wish, but you should get \(A=-\frac{1}{1000}\text{,}\) \(B=\frac{1}{9000}\text{,}\) and \(C=\frac{1}{900}\text{.}\) Thus
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{y\left(100-y\right)\left(y-10\right)}=\frac{-1/1000}{y}+\frac{1/9000}{100-y}+\frac{1/900}{y-10} \end{equation*}
    so
    \begin{equation*} \int{\frac{dy}{y\left(100-y\right)\left(y-10\right)}}=-\frac{1}{1000}\int{\frac{dy}{y}}+\frac{1}{9000}\int{\frac{dy}{100-y}}+\frac{1}{900}\int{\frac{dy}{y-10}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =-\frac{1}{1000}{\mathrm{ln} y\ }-\frac{1}{9000}{\mathrm{ln} \left(100-y\right)\ }+\frac{1}{900}{\mathrm{ln} \left(y-10\right)\ }+c \end{equation*}
    The PFD is Algebra, not Calculus, but as the problems and examples above show it can be very tedious algebra. There are shortcuts to the process which can eliminate some of the tedium, but the problem with shorcuts is that if you do not thoroughly understand the underlying goal it is all too easy to mess up the computation. As a rule, you should not start using computational shortcuts until you have thoroughly mastered the full calculation.

    Example 19.2.3.5. Heaviside’s Cover-up Method.

    Suppose we wish to find the partial fractions decomposition of \(\frac{3x+1}{(x-1)(x-2)(x-5)}\text{.}\) As before, we have
    \begin{equation} \frac{3x+1}{(x-1)(x-2)(x-5)}=\ \frac{A}{x-1}+\frac{B}{x-2}+\frac{C}{x-5} \tag{19.42} \end{equation}
    This leads to
    \begin{equation*} 3x+1=A\left(x-2\right)\left(x-5\right)+B\left(x-1\right)\left(x-5\right)+C(x-1)(x-2) \end{equation*}
    Observe that (19.42) will be true for every real (or complex) value of \(x\text{.}\) The factor \(x-1\) appears in two of the terms on the right suggests that we let \(x=1.\) In that case we have
    \begin{equation*} 3\left(1\right)+1=A\left(1-2\right)\left(1-5\right) \end{equation*}
    or
    \begin{equation*} \frac{3\left(1\right)+1}{\left(1-2\right)\left(1-5\right)}=A. \end{equation*}
    Comment.
    Actually we are replacing \(x-1\) with 1.
    We did not simplify this last formula in order to highlight the following\(.\) Observe that we get the same result if we simply “cover up” the factor \((x-1)\) on the left side of (19.42) above and set \(x=1.\)
    \begin{equation*} A=\frac{3\left(1\right)+1}{(\text{covered})\left(1-2\right)\left(1-5\right)}=1. \end{equation*}
    In precisely the same fashion (setting \(x=2\) and \(x=3\)) we get:
    \begin{equation*} B=\frac{\left(3\right)2+1}{\left(2-1\right)\left(\text{covered}\right)\left(2-5\right)}=\ \frac{-7}{3}, \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} C=\frac{\left(3\right)5+1}{(5-1)(5-2)(\text{covered})}=\frac{4}{3}, \end{equation*}
    so that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{3x+1}{(x-1)(x-2)(x-5)}=\frac{1}{x-1}+\frac{-7/3}{x-2}+\frac{4/3}{x-5}.\ \end{equation*}
    Warning!
    In order to use Heaviside’s Cover–Up Method you must have distinct linear factors in the denominator.
    In this example they were \(x-1\text{,}\) \(x-2\text{,}\) and \(x-5\text{.}\) If our rational function had been
    \begin{equation*} \frac{3x+1}{(x-1)^2(x-2)(x-5)} \end{equation*}
    Heaviside’s Method would not work because \((x-1)^2\) is not linear.
    Try it and see what goes wrong.
    This trick is known as Heaviside’s Cover-Up Method (HCUM). It is well known, and can be used to find the PFD of a rational function as long as the denominator can be factored into distinct linear factors.
    In the previous partial fractions decompositions, we started with a rational function where the degree of the numerator was less than the degree of the denominator and where the factors in the denominator were linear. In this section, we will expand our techniques to encompass other situations.
    For example, suppose the degree of the numerator is not less than the degree of the denominator. If this is the case, then we can perform long division first and then find the partial fractions decomposition of the remainder. For example, suppose we have
    \begin{equation*} \frac{x^3-2x+1}{x^2+x-6}=\frac{x^3-2x+1}{\left(x-2\right)\left(x+3\right)} \end{equation*}
    We first perform long division of the original rational function.
    \begin{align} \frac{x^3-2x+1}{x^2+x-6}& =x-1+\frac{5x-5}{x^2+x-6}\notag\\ &=x-1+5\left(\frac{x-1}{\left(x-2\right)\left(x+3\right)}\right)\tag{19.43} \end{align}
    We can now perform a partial fractions decomposition on the remaining fraction.

    Example 19.2.3.7. The PFD With Nonlinear Factors in the Denominator.

    What about something like
    \begin{equation*} \frac{3x+2}{x^2\left(x-1\right)} \end{equation*}
    Since this function satisfies all of the conditions of [cross-reference to target(s) "PartialFractionDecomp" missing or not unique] we can still decompose it into partial fractions. However since \(x^2\) is not linear we can’t use Heaviside’s Method.
    Recall that in the decomposition, the numerators in the individual terms can be chosen so that the degrees are less than the degrees in the denominators. This being said, we make the following educated guess as to the form of the partial fractions decomposition.
    \begin{equation*} \frac{3x+2}{x^2\left(x-1\right)}=\frac{Ax+B}{x^2}+\frac{C}{x-1} \end{equation*}
    We can now recombine this to determine \(A,B,\) and \(C\text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} \frac{3x+2}{x^2\left(x-1\right)}=\frac{Ax+B}{x^2}+\frac{C}{x-1}=\frac{\left(Ax+B\right)\left(x-1\right)+Cx^2}{x^2\left(x-1\right)} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\frac{Ax^2+Bx-Ax-B+Cx^2}{x^2\left(x-1\right)}=\frac{\left(A+C\right)x^2+\left(-A+B\right)x-B}{x^2\left(x-1\right)} \end{equation*}
    This leads to
    \begin{equation*} A+C=0 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} -A+B=3 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} -B=2 \end{equation*}
    Solving this (any way you can) we get
    \begin{equation*} A=-5,\ B=-2,\ C=5 \end{equation*}
    so that
    \begin{equation*} \frac{3x+2}{x^2\left(x-1\right)}=\frac{-5x-2}{x^2}+\frac{5}{x-1} \end{equation*}
    If we wanted to integrate this, we would obtain
    \begin{equation*} \int{\frac{3x+2}{x^2\left(x-1\right)}}\ \dx{x}=\int{\left(\frac{-5x-2}{x^2}+\frac{5}{x-1}\right)\dx{x}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\int{\left(-\frac{5}{x}-\frac{2}{x^2}+\frac{5}{x-1}\right)\dx{x}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =-5{\mathrm{ln} x\ }+2x^{-1}+5{\mathrm{ln} \left(x-1\right)\ }+c \end{equation*}
    Notice in this example that we really needed to look at the most general possible polynomial of degree one less than the denominators in the partial fractions decomposition; we would not have obtained the correct answer otherwise.

    Example 19.2.3.9.

    Suppose we need to compute
    \begin{equation*} \int{\frac{25}{{\left(x-2\right)}^2(x^2+1)}}\ \dx{x} \end{equation*}
    Applying our partial fractions decomposition, we get
    \begin{equation*} \frac{25}{{\left(x-2\right)}^2\left(x^2+1\right)}=\frac{Ax+B}{{\left(x-2\right)}^2}+\frac{Cx+D}{x^2+1} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\frac{\left(Ax+B\right)\left(x^2+1\right)+\left(Cx+D\right){\left(x-2\right)}^2}{{\left(x-2\right)}^2\left(x^2+1\right)} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\frac{\left(A+C\right)x^3+\left(B-4C-D\right)x^2+\left(A+4C+4D\right)x+\left(B-4D\right)}{{\left(x-2\right)}^2\left(x^2+1\right)} \end{equation*}
    This leads to the equations
    \begin{align*} A+C\amp{}=0\\ B-4C-D\amp{}=0\\ A+4C+4D\amp{}=0\\ B-4D\amp{}=25 \end{align*}
    Solving this (any way you can), we get \(A=-4\text{,}\) \(B=13\text{,}\) \(C=4\text{,}\) and \(D=-3\text{.}\)
    Getting back to the integral, we have
    \begin{equation*} \int{\frac{25}{{\left(x-2\right)}^2\left(x^2+1\right)}}\ \dx{x}=\int{\frac{-4x+13}{{\left(x-2\right)}^2}}\dx{x}+\int{\frac{4x-3}{x^2+1}\dx{x}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\int{\frac{-4x+13}{{\left(x-2\right)}^2}}\dx{x}+4\int{\frac{x}{x^2+1}\dx{x}}-3\int{\frac{1}{x^2+1}\dx{x}} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\int{\frac{-4x+13}{{\left(x-2\right)}^2}}\dx{x}+2{\mathrm{ln} \left(x^2+1\right)\ }-3{\mathrm{arctan} x\ }+c \end{equation*}
    Notice that the last two integrals were pretty straightforward. This first integral is more problematic. There is a little algebraic trick which will allow us to break up that integral even further.
    \begin{equation*} \int{\frac{-4x+13}{{\left(x-2\right)}^2}}\dx{x}=\int{\frac{-4\left(\left(x-2\right)+2\right)+13}{{\left(x-2\right)}^2}}\dx{x} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\int{\frac{-4\left(x-2\right)-8+13}{{\left(x-2\right)}^2}}\dx{x} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =\int{\frac{-4\left(x-2\right)+5}{{\left(x-2\right)}^2}}\dx{x} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =-4\int{\frac{1}{x-2}\ \dx{x}}+5\int{\frac{1}{{\left(x-2\right)}^2}}\dx{x} \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} =-4{\mathrm{ln} \left(x-2\right)\ }-5{\left(x-2\right)}^{-1} \end{equation*}
    Putting this all together, we finally have
    \begin{equation*} \int{\frac{25}{{\left(x-2\right)}^2\left(x^2+1\right)}}\ \dx{x}=-4{\mathrm{ln} \left(x-2\right)\ }-5{\left(x-2\right)}^{-1}+2{\mathrm{ln} \left(x^2+1\right)\ }-3{\mathrm{arctan} x\ }+c \end{equation*}
    The trick that we employed above is something you might want to remember. It allows us to take our partial fractions decomposition a bit further. For example, if we start with
    \begin{equation*} \int{\frac{x^3}{{\left(2x^2+1\right)}^2}}\ \dx{x} \end{equation*}
    we could rewrite this as
    \begin{align*} \int{\frac{x^3}{{\left(2x^2+1\right)}^2} \dx{x}}&=\int{\frac{\frac{1}{2}x\left(2x^2\right)}{{\left(2x^2+1\right)}^2} \dx{x}}\\ &=\int{\frac{\frac{1}{2}x\left(2x^2+1-1\right)}{{\left(2x^2+1\right)}^2} \dx{x}}\\ &=\frac{1}{2}\int{\frac{x}{2x^2+1}\ \dx{x}}-\frac{1}{2}\int{\frac{x}{{\left(2x^2+1\right)}^2}}\ \dx{x} \end{align*}
