Example 19.2.3.10. The PFD With Repeated Linear Factors.
Let’s finish up the decomposition of \(\frac{x}{(x-1)^2(x+2)^2}\) from part (b) of Problem 19.2.3.9. From the HCUM You should have found that \(B=\frac19\) and \(D=\frac{-2}{9}\) so that
\begin{equation*} \displaystyle \frac{x}{(x-1)^2(x+2)^2}= \frac{A}{x-1} + \frac{\frac19}{(x-1)^2} +\frac{C}{x+2} -\frac{\frac29}{(x+2)^2} \end{equation*}
When we reached this point in [cross-reference to target(s) "EXAMPLEHeaviNonLinear1" missing or not unique] our next step was to simply choose a value for \(x\text{.}\) We could do that here as well but we’d have to choose two values for \(x\) thereby generating a pair of equations to solve and we’ve already seen that there are more efficient methods for computing the PFD than solving a system of equations.
Instead we’ll isolate all of the terms with our unknowns on one side of the equation, thus:
\begin{equation} \displaystyle \frac{x}{(x-1)^2(x+2)^2} - \frac{\frac19}{(x-1)^2} +\frac{\frac29}{(x+2)^2} = \frac{A}{x-1} +\frac{C}{x+2}\tag{19.45} \end{equation}
It is clear that the right side of equation (19.45) is the PFD of some rational function whose denominator is \((x-1)(x+2)\text{.}\) That is, if we were asked to begin the process of computing the PFD of
\begin{equation*} \frac{P(x)}{(x-1)(x+2)} \end{equation*}
for some polynomial \(P(x)\) then our first step would be to write down the right side of equation (19.45). Therefore it must be that
\begin{equation} \displaystyle \frac{x}{(x-1)^2(x+2)^2} - \frac{\frac19}{(x-1)^2} +\frac{\frac29}{(x+2)^2} = \frac{P(x)}{(x-1)(x+2)}\tag{19.46} \end{equation}
for some polynomial \(P(x)\text{,}\) and if we can find that polynomial we can compute \(A\) and \(C\) by the HCUM.
We can find \(P(x)\) by essentially the same method we used to find the constant \(A\) in [cross-reference to target(s) "EXAMPLEHeaviNonLinear1" missing or not unique]. First we clear the fraction on the right
\begin{equation} \frac{x}{(x-1)(x+2)} - \frac19 \left( \frac{x+2}{x-1} \right) +\frac29 \left( \frac{x-1}{x+2} \right) = P(x)\tag{19.47} \end{equation}
Since the left side is guaranteed to reduce to the polynomial it is tempting to find a common denominator and add the three fractions on the left together. Since we have to end up with the polynomial \(P(x)\) it must be that once the addition is done there will be a factor in the top that will cancel with the common denominator.
That will work. Give it a try if you want. Good luck.
But we’re trying to find the most efficient method we can so instead we’re going to make judicious use of the knowledge that we must end up with a polynomial.
First think about the expression in the second term on the left: \(\frac{x+2}{x-1}\text{.}\) If we perform this division we get
\begin{equation*} \frac{x+2}{x-1} = 1 +\frac{3}{x-1}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Similarly if we perform the division in the third term we get \(\frac{x-1}{x+2}= 1-\frac{3}{x+2}\text{.}\) Putting these back into equation (19.47) we have
\begin{equation*} \frac{x}{(x-1)(x+2)} - \frac19 \left( 1 +\frac{3}{x-1} \right) +\frac29 \left( 1-\frac{3}{x+2} \right) = P(x) \end{equation*}
or
\begin{equation*} \frac{x}{(x-1)(x+2)} - \frac19 -\frac{\frac13}{x-1} +\frac29 -\frac{\frac23}{x+2} = P(x) \text{.} \end{equation*}
Since \(P(x)\) must be a polynomial it follows that the left side,
\begin{equation} \frac{x}{(x-1)(x+2)} - \frac19 -\frac{\frac13}{x-1} +\frac29 -\frac{\frac23}{x+2}\tag{19.48} \end{equation}
is also a polynomial. The constants \(-\frac19{}\) and \(\frac{2}{9}\) are polynomials of degree zero. Are there any others? When we perform the divisions \(\frac{x}{(x-1)(x+2)}\text{,}\) \(\frac{\frac13}{x-1}\text{,}\) or \(\frac{\frac23}{x+2}\) is it possible that a polynomial will result? Think about this for a moment before reading on.
Clearly we will not. The result of polynomial division is always get a polynomial with a remainder, where in the remainder the degree of the numerator is always strictly less than the degree of the denominator. (This is analogous to integer division, where the remainder is always less than the denominator. For example \(\frac{26}{3}=8\) with remainder \(2\lt 3\text{.}\))
The numerators of \(\frac{x}{(x-1)(x+2)}\text{,}\) \(\frac{\frac13}{x-1}\text{,}\) and \(\frac{\frac23}{x+2}\) already have a lower degree than their denominators. Thus if we perform the divisions indicated we will, in each case, get zero as the polynomial and the original fraction as the remainder. Try it and see.
But what about when we add the remainders up? Is it possible that we could get a polynomial that way?
No it isn’t. It is straightforward to show that if \(A(x)\text{,}\) \(B(x)\text{,}\) \(C(x)\text{,}\) and \(D(x)\) are polynomials with \(\deg(A(x))\lt \deg (B(x))\) and \(\deg (C(x))\lt \deg (D(x))\) then \(\deg (A(x)\cdot D(x) + C(x)\cdot B(x)) \lt \deg(B(x)\cdot D(x)) \text{.}\) Thus performing the division
\begin{equation*} \frac{A(x)}{B(x)}+\frac{C(x)}{D(x)}= \frac{A(x)\cdot D(x)+C(x)\cdot B(x)}{B(x)\cdot D(x)} \end{equation*}
also yields a zero polynomial with \(\frac{A(x)\cdot D(x)+C(x)\cdot B(x)}{B(x)\cdot D(x)}\) as the remainder.
The upshot, in this instance, is that \(\frac{x}{(x-1)(x-2)}-\frac{\frac13 }{x-1}-\frac{\frac23 }{x+2}\) must sum to zero. So we can ignore them and conclude that our polynomial is given by the other terms. So
\begin{equation*} P(x)=-\frac19 + \frac29 =\frac19 \text{.} \end{equation*}
To finish the decomposition we observe that
\begin{equation*} \frac{\frac19 }{(x-1)(x+2)} = \frac{A}{x-1}+\frac{C}{x+2} \end{equation*}
and from the HCUM we see that \(A=\frac{1}{27} \) and \(C=-\frac19\text{.}\)
Having found all of the constants we conclude that
\begin{equation*} \frac{x}{(x-1)^2(x+2)^2}= \frac{\frac{1}{27}}{x-1} + \frac{\frac19 }{(x-1)^2} -\frac{\frac19}{x+2} -\frac{\frac29 }{(x+2)^2} \text{.} \end{equation*}
Naturally, it isn’t always that simple. If any (or all) of the numerators in formula (19.48) did not have a lower degree than it’s denominator then we’d have had to divide them in order to recover the polynomials. But then the sum of the remainders would necessarily be zero so they can be ignored just as we ignored them here.
