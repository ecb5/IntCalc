Problem 19.1.4.8.

From entry #11 and #13 of Table 18.1.0.4 we know that
\begin{equation*} \int \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}\dx{x}=\inverse\sin (x)+C \end{equation*}
and
\begin{equation*} \int \frac{1}{\abs{x}\sqrt{x^2-1}}\dx{x}=\inverse\sec (x)+C\text{.} \end{equation*}
Emulate our discussion in Example 19.1.4.5 to compute these integrals directly.
\begin{align*} \int\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}\dx{x}\amp{}\amp{}\text{ and }\amp{} \amp{} \int \frac{1}{\abs{x}\sqrt{x^2-1}}\dx{x}. \end{align*}
To avoid complications caused by the absolute value assume that \(0\le \theta \le \frac{\pi}{2}\text{,}\) so that \(\sec( \theta )\ge 0\) when you make your substitution.
