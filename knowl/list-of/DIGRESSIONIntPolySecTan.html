Comment.
There is an old joke that a technique is a trick that you use more than once.
We have called the substitution \(u=\tan(x)+\sec(x) \) a trick but what seems to be a trick is often a special case of more general technique. Th is the situation here. We would like to find the underlying structure behind Example 19.1.2.11.
The technique follows from definitions BAD XREFS COMMENTED OUT HERE.
\begin{align} u\amp{}=\sec(x)+\tan(x)\tag{19.8}\\ v\amp{}=\sec(x)-\tan(x)\tag{19.9} \end{align}
from which we see that
\begin{align} \sec(x) \amp{}=\frac12 (u+v)\tag{19.10}\\ \tan(x) \amp{}=\frac12 (u-v).\tag{19.11} \end{align}

Example 19.1.2.13.
(a)
For our first example we will compute
\begin{align*} \int\sec^3(x)\dx{x} \amp{}= \int \sec^2(x)\sec{x}\dx{x} \end{align*}
From equation (19.10) we have
\begin{align*} \int\sec^3(x)\dx{x}\amp{}=\frac14\int(u+v)^2\sec(x)\dx{x}\\ \amp{}=\frac14\int(u^2+2uv+v^2)\sec(x)\dx{x} \end{align*}
and from equation (19.12) we have
\begin{align*} \int\sec^3(x)\dx{x}\amp{}=\frac14\int(u^2+2+v^2))\sec(x)\dx{x}\\ \amp{}=\frac14\int u^2\left(\frac{1}{u}\dx{u}\right)+\frac{1}{2}\int\sec(x)\dx{x} -\frac14 \int v^2\left(\frac{1}{v}\dx{v}\right)\\ \amp{}=\frac18\left(u^2-v^2\right)+\frac12\int\sec(x)\dx{x}\\ =\amp{}\frac18\left[\left(\sec(x)+\tan(x)\right)^2-\left( \sec(x)-\tan(x)\right)^2 \right] +\frac12\abs{\sec(x)+\tan(x)}+C \end{align*}
So finally,
\begin{equation*} \int\sec^3(x)\dx{x}=\frac12\left(\sec(x)\tan(x)+\abs{\sec(x)+\tan(x)}\right)+C. \end{equation*}
In fact, in the paper cited above, McCammond shows that if \(P(s,t)\) is any polynomial in two variables then
\begin{equation*} \int P\left(\sec(x), \tan(x)\right)\dx{x} \end{equation*}
can be evaluated using the substitutions in equations (19.8), (19.9), (19.10), (19.11), (19.12), (19.13), and the judicious use of the Pythagorean trigonometric identity
\begin{equation} \tan^2(x)+1=\sec^2(x).\tag{19.14} \end{equation}
