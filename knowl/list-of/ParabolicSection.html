Example 21.5.1.1.

Find the area of the following region bounded by the curves \(y=x^2\) and \(y=2x\text{.}\) A graph of this region is below.

Utilizing our strategy, we will draw a single generic rectangle of width \(\dx{x}\) whose endpoints lie on the bounding curves. We will label the endpoints of this infinitely thin rectangle on the diagram and draw this rectangle on the side with its length and width labeled. You will see that this will make the computation of areas easier (which is the point). We insist that you follow this routine on every application, no matter how easy it may seem. This will serve you well as the applications get more complicated.

This is the set up. Notice that by labeling properly, we could read the area of the rectangle right off the picture. This was the point â€” to divide the problem into easily computable parts. The integral just added them together. To compute this integral, we need to put everything in terms of one variable. This can done by noticing that \(y_2=2x\) and \(y_1=x^2\text{.}\) Thus, we have
\begin{align*} \text{ Total area } =\int_{x=0}^2 (y_2-y_1)\dx{x}\amp =\int_{x=0}^2 (2x-x^2)\dx{x}\\ \amp =\eval{\left( x^2-\frac{x^3}{3}\right)}{x}{0}^2\\ \amp =\left( 2^2-\frac{2^3}{3}\right) - \left( 0^2-\frac{0^3}{3}\right) \\ \amp =\frac43. \end{align*}

Of course, there is nothing special about getting everything in terms of \(x\text{.}\) We could divide the region into horizontal rectangles and put everything in terms of \(y\text{.}\) We should obtain the same area. Notice that in the previous set up out two points had the same \(x\) coordinate. In this one, they will have the same \(y\) coordinate.

Using the fact that \((x_2,y)\) lies on the curve \(y=x^2\) and that \((x_1,y)\) lies on the curve \(y=2x\text{,}\) we get
\begin{align*} \text{ Total Area } \amp = \int_{y=0}^4 (x_2-x_1)\dx{y}\\ \amp = \int_{y=0}^4 (y^\frac12 -\frac{y}{2}\dx{y}\\ \amp =\eval{\left( \frac{2y^\frac32}{3}-\frac{y^2}{4}\right)}{y}{0}^4 \\ \amp =\left( \frac{2(4)^\frac32}{3}-\frac{4^2}{4}\right) -(0-0)\\ \amp =\frac{16}{3}-4=\frac43{} \end{align*}
which is the same answer as before. You can choose whichever way you want; it depends on whether you ultimately want things in terms of \(x\) or in terms of \(y\text{.}\) In this example, one was not much harder than the other. Sometimes the difference in difficulty will help you make the decision. In the next example, we willset up the problem in both ways and then determine which will be the easiest route to follow.
