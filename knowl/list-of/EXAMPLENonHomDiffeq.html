Example 19.3.0.1. Population Dynamics: A Non–Separable Differential Equation.

Recall that in Example 18.1.0.9 we solved the differential equation \(\dfdx{y}{t}=Ky \) by separating the variables and integrating. This is the equation we used to model the exponential growth of an algal bloom which we first saw in Section 8.6.
For reasons that will become clear shortly we re–write will this equation as
\begin{equation} \dfdx{y}{t}-Ky =0\text{.}\tag{19.54} \end{equation}
The exponential growth model does not allow for the possibility that there is an outside source of new population. It assumes, implicitly, that the population grows only by it’s own reproductive activity.
But suppose there is an external source of population. Suppose for example, that we have a population of algae growing in an alpine pond, and that there are new algae entering the pond via the stream that feeds the pond. The rate at which the new population is added to the old will rise and fall with the rate at which the stream is flowing. For simplicity of demonstration we’ll suppose that the “rising and falling” takes the form of a cosine wave, \(\cos(t) \text{.}\)
This means that the rate of change of our algae population will be \(\dfdx{y}{t}=Ky+\cos (t)\) because the rate of growth of our population is still proportional to the size of the population. To be consistent with equation (19.56) we’ll re–write this as
\begin{equation} \dfdx{y}{t}-Ky=\cos (t)\text{.}\tag{19.55} \end{equation}
To complete our model we’ll assume that initally there are \(10^4\) algae in the pond. Thus to find the population as a function of time we need to solve the IVP
\begin{align*} \dfdx{y}{t} - Ky \amp{}=\cos(t),\amp{} y(0)=10^4. \end{align*}
This is not a separable equation but it is very similar to
\begin{equation} \dfdx{y}{t}-Ky=0 \text{,}\tag{19.56} \end{equation}
whose solution \(y(t)=Ae^{Kt}\) is known. Since the equations are the similar it seems reasonable to suppose that their solutions will also be similar.
The difficulty of course, is that it is not at all obvious how we might tweak \(y(t)=Ae^{Kt}\) so to obtain a solution of equation (19.55)
This is why we re–wrote our differential equations the way we did. On the left side of both we have
\begin{equation*} \dfdx{y}{t}-Ky\text{.} \end{equation*}
If you look closely at that expression you can almost see the result of a Product Rule. In fact if we multiply by \(e^{-Kt}\) we will have exactly the Product Rule. Try it and see.
Of course we can–t just multiply one side of an equation by something just because we like the outcome, so we’ll multiply both sides of equation (19.56) instead, giving
\begin{equation*} \underbrace{e^{-Kt}\dfdx{y}{t}-e^{-Kt}Ky}_{=\dfdx{\left(e^{-Kt}\cdot y\right)}{t}}=0\cdot e^{-Kt} \end{equation*}
or
\begin{equation*} \dfdx{\left(e^{-Kt}\cdot y\right)}{t}=0\text{.} \end{equation*}
From there we can recover the solution of (19.56) by integrating
\begin{align*} \int\dfdx{\left(e^{-Kt}\cdot y\right)} {t}\dx{t}\amp{}=\int0\dx{t}\\ e^{-Kt}\cdot y \amp{}= A\\ y(t)\amp{}=Ae^{Kt}. \end{align*}
