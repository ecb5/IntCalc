Problem 21.5.1.13. Archimedes Quadrature of the Parabola.

In his treatise, Quadrature of the Parabola, Archimedes (287–212 BC) shows that the area of a parabolic 4 segment is \(3\) the area of its inscribed triangle.
🔗
The “inscribed triangle” is the one where the distance from point \(C\) to segment \(\overline{AB} \) is a maximum. That The “inscribed triangle” is the one where the distance from point \(C\) to segment \(AB\text{.}\) Archimedes did this without calculus, equations, or coordinate geometry is a tribute to his genius and talent as a mathematician. He didn’t even have formulas at his disposal; this is why he stated the area as he did. We will use calculus to confirm Archimedes’ result. With this in mind, consider the parabola \(y=x^2\text{,}\) and let \(A=(a, a^2)\text{,}\) \(B=(b, b^2)\text{,}\) and \(C=(c,c^2)\text{.}\)
🔗
(a)
Find the area of \(\triangle ABC\) in terms of \(a\text{,}\) \(b\text{,}\) \(c\text{.}\) [Hint: You can do this in many ways, but the “easiest” way might be to draw a vertical line through \(C\) and use this to divide the triangle into two triangles (only a suggestion).]
🔗
🔗
(b)
Find the coordinates of \(C\) which maximizes the distance from \(C\) to segment \(\overline{AB} \) and verify Archimedes’ \(r\) result that the area of the parabolic segment is \(3\) the area of \(\triangle ABC\text{.}\)
🔗
Hint.
Again, you can maximize this distance in many ways, but the easiest way might be to notice that the maximum distance (measured along the perpendicular to \(\overline{AB} \)) occurs when the vertical distance is maximized (again, only a suggestion).
