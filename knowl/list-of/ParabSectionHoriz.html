Example 4.5.1.5.

Compute the area of the following region bounded by \(y=0\text{,}\) \(y=x+2\text{,}\) and \(y=\sqrt{4-x}\text{.}\) If we want everything in terms of \(x\) then we need to draw a generic vertical rectangle of width \(\dx{x}\text{,}\) which we’ve included in the diagram along with all of the appropriate labeling.
Everything is just as before. The real challenge comes from trying to put everything in terms of \(x\) on the entire interval. We have \(y_1=0\) but the formula for \(y_2\) changes. Remember that the rectangle is a generic rectangle and represents only one of the infinitely many rectangles that are being integrated on the interval from \(x=-2\) and \(x=4\text{.}\) On \([-2,0]\text{,}\) \(y_2=x+2\text{,}\) while on \([0,4]\text{,}\) \(y_2=\sqrt{4-x}\text{.}\)
We actually know a way to handle this, utilizing our properties of definite integrals. Specifically, we can do the following
\begin{align*} \text{ Total area }\amp =\int_{x=-2}^4(y_2-y_1)\dx{x}\\ \amp =\int_{x=-2}^0(y_2-y_1)\dx{x}+\int_{x=0}^4(y_2-y_1)\dx{x}\\ \amp =\int_{x=-2}^0(x+2-0)\dx{x}+\int_{x=-2}^4\sqrt{4-x}-0\dx{x} \end{align*}
Each integral can be handled separately, and the total area is the sum of those two values. This is a perfectly acceptable way to do the problem, and each integral is not too bad to do. But how about if we try a horizontal rectangle and putting everything in terms of \(y\text{?}\)
