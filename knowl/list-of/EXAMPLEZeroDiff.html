Example 26.2.0.5. The Problem of Zero Differentials.

Naturally, things are not quite this simple. Consider
\begin{align*} f_1(x)\amp{}=x^2+7 \text{ and}\\ f_2(x)\amp{}=x^2+4. \end{align*}
Clearly \(\dx{f_1}=\dx{f_2}=2x\dx{x}\) so the sum of \(\dx{f_1}\) and the sum of \(\dx{f_2}\) would seem to be the same as. But clearly
\begin{equation*} \text{ the sum of the}\dx{y_1}=x^2+7 \end{equation*}
whereas
\begin{equation*} \text{ the sum of the}\dx{y_2}=x^2+5\text{.} \end{equation*}
This apparent contradiction needs to be resolved.

One way to resolve it is to form the function
\begin{equation*} f(x)=y_1(x)-y_2(x) \end{equation*}
and notice that since \(\dx{y_1}=\dx{y_2} \) it follows that
\begin{equation*} \dx(y_1-y_2)=\dx{y_1}-\dx{y_2}=0. \end{equation*}
Dividing by \(\dx{x}\) and thinking of \(\dfdx{y_1}{x} \) and \(\dfdx{y_2}{x} \) as slopes, we have
\begin{equation*} \dfdx{f}{x}= \dfdx{y_1}{x} -\dfdx{y_2}{x} =\dfdx{(y_1-y_2)}{x}=0\text{.} \end{equation*}
This says that the curve \(f(x) = y_1(x) - y_2(x)\) has a slope which is constantly equal to zero.
