Example 2.3.0.2.

To integrate \(\int x\cos (x)\dx{x}\) we set
\begin{align*} u=x\amp{}\amp{}\text{ and } \amp{}\amp{} \dx{v}=\cos(x)\dx{x}\\ \end{align*}
so that
\begin{align*} \dx{u}=\dx{x} \amp{}\amp{}\text{ and } \amp{}\amp{} v=\int\cos(x)\dx{x} = \sin(x), \end{align*}
and making these substitutions gives
\begin{align*} \int x\cos(x)\dx{x} = \int u\dx{v}\amp{}=uv-\int v\dx{u}\\ \amp{}=x\sin(x) -\int \sin (x)\dx{x}.\\ \amp{}=x\sin(x) + \cos (x). \end{align*}
and, finally
\begin{equation} \int x\cos(x)\dx{x} = x\sin(x) + \cos (x) +C\tag{2.58} \end{equation}
Integration by Parts is Not a Substitution
We did not get an integral with \(u\)’s or \(v\)’s in it that we then had to transform back into an integral in terms of \(x\text{,}\) the way we did with Integration by Substitution. These were introduced just to keep track of the integration by parts formula.
In Choosing \(u\) and \(v\) We Used Up the Entire Integrand
That is, the expression \(u\dx{v}\) is exactly equal to the the integrand, \(x\cos(x)\dx{x}\text{.}\) Nothing was left out.
Integration By Parts Does Not Compute the Integral
Rather, it replaces that integral with, hopefully, an easier integral. This means that as long as the integration by parts technique is applied correctly, it is not wrong; it just might not be helpful.
This is actually true of all of the integration techniques. They do not compute the integral, they just seek to replace it with an integral that we already know.
For example, we could have let \(u=\cos(x){}\) and \(\dx{u}=x\dx{x}\text{.}\) This would give \(v=x^2/2\text{.}\) Substituting these into [cross-reference to target(s) "IntByParts" missing or not unique] we get
\begin{equation*} \int x\cos(x)\dx{x}=\frac{x^2}{2}\cos(x)+\frac{1}{2}\int x^2\sin(x)\dx{x}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Everything we’ve done is correct, but the integral on the right is actually harder to evaluate than the one we started with.
