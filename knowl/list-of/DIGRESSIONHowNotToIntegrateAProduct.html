DIGRESSION: How Not to Integrate a Product.

In the beginning a common error is to assume that we need only integrate the two factors separately. If we do that with formula (2.62) we’ll get
\begin{equation*} \frac{x^2}{2}\sin(x) +C\text{.} \end{equation*}
This is not correct as we can easily verify by differentiation. In fact it is not generally true that
\begin{equation} \int f(x)\cdot g(x)\dx{x}=\left(\int f(x)\dx{x}\right)\cdot \left(\int g(x) \dx{x}\right) \text{.}\tag{2.63} \end{equation}
Of course, we already knew that
\begin{equation*} \left(f\cdot g\right)^\prime(x) \neq f^\prime(x)\cdot g^\prime(x) \text{,} \end{equation*}
so on second thought it may seem like it was silly to assume that equation (2.64) is valid. But it was not. It is actually quite a natural assumption to make. But assumptions can be treacherous as we’ve seen. They need to be examined closely. In this case our assumption comes up short as we have just seen.
But it is not silly, it is just wrong.
So when the integrand is the product of two functions we need a new idea.

Problem 2.3.0.2. When the Derivative of a Product is the Product of the Derivatives.

Before we leave this topic behind entirely here is a curious fact. There are certain special pairs of functions for which it is true that
\begin{equation} \left(f\cdot g\right)^\prime(x) = f^\prime(x)\cdot g^\prime(x) \text{.}\tag{2.64} \end{equation}
In this problem we will find a few examples of such pairs.
(a)
Suppose \(f^\prime(x)\neq{}f(x)\text{.}\) Show that if \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) satisfy equation (2.64) then
\begin{equation} g(x)=\exp\left(\int\frac{1}{1-\frac{f(x)}{f^\prime (x)}}\dx{x}\right)\text{.}\tag{2.65} \end{equation}
Why did we require that \(f^\prime(x)\neq{}f(x)\text{?}\) Are any other constraints on the functions needed?
(b)
Explain how you can tell that
\begin{equation} f(x)=\exp\left(\int\frac{1}{1-\frac{g(x)}{g^\prime (x)}}\dx{x}\right)\tag{2.66} \end{equation}
is also true. Is it necessary to repeat the computations you did in part (a)?
(c)
For each of these pairs of functions either use equation (2.65) to derive \(g(x)\) from \(f(x)\) or use equation (2.66) to derive \(f(x)\) from \(g(x)\text{.}\) Assume that \(\alpha{}\) is an arbitrary constant.
  1. \(f(x)=e^{\alpha x}\text{,}\) \(g(x)=e^{\frac{\alpha }{\alpha -1}x}\) \(\alpha{}\neq1\text{.}\)
  2. \(f(x)=\left(\frac{\alpha}{\alpha -x}\right)^\alpha\text{,}\) \(g(x)= x^\alpha\)
  3. \(f(x) = x+\alpha \text{,}\) \(g(x)=\frac{1}{(1-\alpha )-x}\)
  4. \(f(x)=xe^x\text{,}\) \(g(x)=e^{\frac{x^2}{2}+x} \)
  5. \(f(x)=e^{\frac{x}{2}}\sqrt{\frac{\sec(x) }{1-\tan(x) }}\text{,}\)\(g(x)=\sin(x) \)
(d)
For each pair of functions in part (c) confirm that they satisfy equation (2.64) by computing the derivatives and comparing them
