Example 25.8.0.3.

We know that the circumference of a unit circle is \(2\pi \text{.}\) Thus, the length of a quarter of that circle is \(\frac{\pi }{2}\text{.}\) Suppose we wanted to use calculus to verify this. The easiest way would be to parameterize the quarter of the unit circle lying in the first quadrant by
\begin{align*} x=\cos(\theta), \amp{}\amp{} y=\sin(\theta) , \amp{}\amp{} 0\le \theta \le \frac{\pi }{2}\\ \end{align*}
or
\begin{align*} P(\theta) \ParamEqThree{x=\cos(\theta)} {y=\sin(\theta)} {0\le \theta \le \frac{\pi }{2}} \end{align*}
Thus, the arc length would be
\begin{align*} \int^{\frac{\pi }{2}}_{\theta =0}\dx{s}= \int^{\frac{\pi}{2}}_{\theta=0}{\sqrt{{\left(\dx{x}\right)}^2+{\left(\dx{y}\right)}^2}}\amp{}=\int^{\frac{\pi}{2}}_{\theta=0} \sqrt{\left(-\sin(\theta)\right)^2+\left(\cos(\theta) \right)^2} \dx{\theta}\\ \amp{} =\int^{\frac{\pi }{2}}_{\theta =0}{1} \dx{\theta} =\frac{\pi }{2} \end{align*}
Suppose instead we use the equation \(y=\sqrt{1-x^2},\) \(0\le x\le 1\text{.}\) In this case we would get the integral
\begin{equation*} \int^1_{x=0} \sqrt{\frac{1}{1-x^2}} \dx{x} =\eval{\inverse\sin(x)}{x}{0}=\frac{\pi }{2} \end{equation*}
