Example 19.1.3.20.

\(\int\sec^4(\theta)\tan^2(\theta)\dx{\theta}\)
In this example, we can save a \(\sec^2(\theta)\) as part of the differential and use the identity \(\sec^2(\theta)=1+\tan^2(\theta)\) to change everything else into an expression in tangent. Since the secant is to an even power this again avoids square roots, which is the general idea.
\begin{align*} \int \sec^4(\theta)\tan^2(\theta)\dx{\theta}\amp =\int \sec^2(\theta)\tan^2(\theta)\sec^2(\theta)\dx{\theta}\\ \int(1+\tan^2(\theta))\tan^2(\theta)\sec^2(\theta)\dx{\theta} \end{align*}
Letting \(y=\tan(\theta)\) we have \(\dx{y}=\sec^2(\theta)\dx{\theta}\text{,}\) so we obtain
\begin{gather*} \int(1+y^2)y^2\dx{y}=\int(y^2+y^4)\dx{y}=\frac{y^3}{3}+\frac{y^5}{5}+c=\frac{\tan^3(\theta)}{3}+\frac{\tan^5(\theta)}{5}+c \end{gather*}
