Example 2.1.4.5.

For example if we make the substitution \(x=\tan (\theta )\text{,}\) \(\dx{x}=\sec^2 (\theta )\) in the integral \(\int \frac{1}{(1+x^2)^2}\dx{x}\) we get
\begin{align*} \int \frac{1}{(1+x^2)^2}\dx{x} \amp{}= \int \frac{1}{\sec^2 (\theta )}\dx{\theta}\\ \amp{}=\int \cos^2 (\theta )\dx{\theta }. \end{align*}
It may not be obvious at the moment how to compute this last integral but it is straightforward to show that if \(x=\tan (\theta )\text{,}\) then
\begin{equation} \int \frac{1}{(1+x^2)^2}\dx{x} = \int \cos^2 (\theta )\dx{\theta }= \frac12 \theta +\frac14 \sin (2\theta ) +C \text{.}\tag{2.27} \end{equation}
You will confirm equation (2.27) in Problem 2.1.4.7.
Now all that is left is to put everything back in terms of \(x\) which we will leave to you in Problem 2.1.4.6.
