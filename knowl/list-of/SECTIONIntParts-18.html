Problem 19.3.0.7.

Did you notice that in Example 19.3.0.3 we suppressed the arbitrary constant when we computed \(v\text{?}\) This is because once we have chosen \(\dx{v}\text{,}\) any antiderivative will work as this problem shows.
🔗

(a)

Evaluate the integral \(\int x\cos(x)\dx{x}\) letting \(u=x\) again, letting \(\dx{v}=\cos(x)\dx{x}\) as before. But this time take \(y=\sin(x)+a\) where \(a\) is an arbitrary constant. Show that we will obtain the same answer as before.
🔗
🔗

(b)

Show that in general, if we let \(\int\dx{v}=v+a\) in the integration by parts formula, then we still obtain the formula
\begin{equation*} \int u\dx{v}=uv-\int v\dx{u}. \end{equation*}
🔗
Hint.
You may find it helpful to use the functional form of Integration by Parts in Theorem 19.3.0.5.
🔗
🔗
🔗
Since keeping track of the arbitrary constant in subsequent computations pointlessly complicates everything it is usually ignored. But don’t forget to tack on the “\(+C\)” at the end.
