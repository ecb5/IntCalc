Example 20.0.0.1. Bridge Arches, the Catenary Curve, and Integration as Summation.

“Without stones there is no arch.”
🔗
―Marco Polo (1254—1324)
🔗
In Subsection 5.10.3 we mentioned that when Sir Christopher Wren(1632–1723) designed the dome of St Paul’s Cathedral he was advised by his friend Robert Hooke (1635–1703) that for maximum stability he should design the dome so that every cross section through the center would be a catenary curve:
\begin{equation*} y=\frac{1}{a}\cosh(ax)=\frac{1}{2a}\left(e^{ax}+e^{-ax}\right) \end{equation*}
where \(a\) is an appropriate constant. Specifically, Hooke said:
🔗
As hangs a flexible cable so, inverted, stand the touching pieces of an arch.
🔗
🔗
Through trial–and–error over the centuries stonemasons had also concluded that the best shape for a stone bridge is an inverted catenary.
🔗
To see why this is so consider one half of a stone arch bridge as represented in the schematic below. We will draw the positive \(y\)-axis downward and focus on the forces at a generic point \(P\) with coordinates \((x,y)\text{.}\) Our analysis will be very similar to our analysis of the suspension bridge cable in Subsection 5.10.1, so you might want to review that before reading further.
🔗
The problem is to find the curve \(y=y(x)\) with the property that the vertical component of the tangential force \(T\text{,}\) at \(P\text{,}\) is equal to the weight of the bridge from \(0\) to \(x\text{.}\) Let \(A(x)\) denote the cross–sectional area of the bridge from \(0\) to \(x\text{.}\)
🔗
We will use the following two facts about \(A(x)\text{:}\)
  1. The weight of bridge from \(0\) to \(x\) is proportional to \(A(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
  2. \(A(x)=\int\dx{A}\text{.}\) (Do you see what \(\dx{A}\) represents in our sketch above?)
    🔗
    🔗
🔗
Next we denote by \(H\) the (constant) magnitude of the horizontal force along the length of the bridge and by \(w\) be the weight density of the stone (per cross sectional area) so that the weight of the bridge from \(0\) to \(x\) is \(wA\text{.}\)
🔗
We want the horizontal component of the force \(T\text{,}\) at \((x,y)\text{,}\) to be \(H\) and the vertical component to be the weight of the bridge from \(0\) to \(x\text{,}\) namely, \(wA\text{.}\) This leads to the following picture, from which we conclude that the slope of the tangent line to \(y(x)\) at \((x,y)\) is equal to \(\frac{w}{H}\text{.}\)
🔗
And so we have the differential equation
\begin{equation} \dfdx{y}{x}=\frac{w}{H}A\text{.}\tag{20.1} \end{equation}
🔗
The problem here is that we don’t know what \(A\) is. However, we do know what \(dA\) is. Do you see it?
🔗
We will return to that in a moment, but first differentiating equation (20.1) we see that the arch should satisfy the differential equation
\begin{equation} \dfdxn{y}{x}{2}=\frac{w}{H}\dfdx{A}{x}\text{.}\tag{20.2} \end{equation}
🔗
Returning to \(\dx{A}\) we form the infinitesimal rectangle whose base is \(\dx{x}\) and whose height is \(y\) as in the sketch below.
🔗
Notice that the area of that rectangle is a differential since it is the product of the real number \(y\) and the differential \(\dx{x}\text{.}\) Moreover the sum of the areas of all such rectangles is clearly \(A\) so it must be that
\begin{equation*} \dx{A}=y\dx{x}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Substituting this into equation (20.2) we find that
\begin{equation*} \dfdxn{y}{x}{2}=\frac{w}{H}\dfdx{A}{x}=\frac{w}{H} y\text{.} \end{equation*}
