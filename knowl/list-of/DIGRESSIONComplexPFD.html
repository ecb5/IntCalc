DIGRESSION: Avoiding the Irreducible Case by Allowing Complex Numbers.
In his PhD dissertation C. F. Gauss
 7 
https://mathshistory.st-andrews.ac.uk/Biographies/Gauss/
(1777–1855) proved that every polynomial can be completely factored into (possibly repeated) linear factors. These days this is known by the rather pompous name The Fundamental Theorem of Algebra.
In view of this theorem an obvious question presents itself: “Why did we bother with the Irreducible Quadratic case when discussing the Partial Fraction Decomposition?” That is, why did we (the authors) tell you that \(x^2+1\) can’t be factored, when Gauss’s theorem clearly says that it can.
Recall that in the first paragraph of the subsection Irreducible Quadratic Factors we were careful to state that “\(x^2+1\)” cannot be factored over the real numbers. This is true but it can be factored over the complex numbers (real numbers plus imaginary numbers).
While Gauss’s proof didn’t make explicit use of complex numbers they were implicit in his approach and his proof was valid. This was quite a puzzle at the time because while his proof said that \(x^2+1\) could be factored, the nature of the factors was not clear. When Gauss proved the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra the complex numbers were not well understood at all and had not been accepted. The Fundamental Theorem of Algebra is one of the results that forced mathematicians to undertake the study of the complex numbers and eventually accept them as an extension of the real numbers.
Problem 19.2.2.12.
(a)
Let \(i=\sqrt{-1}\) as in Section 8.13 and show that
\begin{equation*} x^2+1 = (x-i)(x+i)\text{.} \end{equation*}
(b)
Use the result of part (a) to show that
\begin{equation*} \int \frac{1}{x^2+1}\dx{x} = \frac{i}{2}\ln \left( \frac{x+i}{x-i} \right) +C \end{equation*}
(c)
Recall that we’ve already seen that \(\int \frac{1}{x^2+1}\dx{x} = \inverse \tan(x) +C\text{.}\) In view of the result in part (b), do you believe that
\begin{equation} \frac{i}{2}\ln \left( \frac{x+i}{x-i} \right) +C = \inverse \tan(x) +C\text{?}\tag{19.51} \end{equation}
Notice that we have not asked if this is true, only if you believe it. Hence there is no right or wrong answer to the following question: Explain why you do or don’t believe that equation (19.51). Give mathematical reasons to support your opinion either way.
Example 19.2.2.13.
By allowing the use of complex numbers we can use the HCUM to compute
\begin{align*} \frac{x-2}{x(x^2+1)}=\frac{x-2}{x(x-i)(x+i)} \amp{}=\frac{-2}{x}+\frac{\frac{i-2}{i(2i)}}{x-i}+\frac{\frac{i+2}{i(-2i)}}{x+i}\\ \amp{}= \frac{-2}{x}+\frac{\frac{i-2}{-2}}{x-i}+\frac{\frac{-i-2}{-2}}{x+i}\\ \amp{}=\frac{-2}{x}+\frac{1-\frac12i}{x-i}+\frac{1+\frac12i }{x+i}. \end{align*}
But unfortunately, in this course we traditionally confine our attention to the real numbers and treat the complex numbers as if they are somehow unsavory, even when using them would make more sense. To remove the complex numbers from our decomposition we compute the sum \(\frac{1-\frac12i}{x-i}+\frac{1+\frac12i }{x+i}\) as follows
\begin{equation} \frac{1-\frac12i}{x-i}+\frac{1+\frac12i }{x+i}=\frac{2x+1}{x^2+1}\text{.}\tag{19.52} \end{equation}
Thus our decomposition over the real numbers (rather than the complex numbers is
\begin{equation} \frac{x-2}{x(x^2+1)}=\frac{-2}{x}+\frac{2x+1}{x^2+1}\text{.}\tag{19.53} \end{equation}
Problem 19.2.2.14.
(a)
Confirm that equation (19.52) is correct.
(b)
Confirm that equation (19.53) is correct.
