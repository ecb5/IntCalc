Example 2.2.1.15.
If we try to decompose the rational function \(\frac{x^2}{(x-2)(x-3)}\) using the methods we’ve seen so far we get
\begin{equation*} \frac{x^2}{(x-2)(x-3)}=\frac{-4}{x-2} +\frac{9}{x-3}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Confirm that this is not correct.
So what went wrong?
In our problems and examples so far we’ve been careful to make sure that the integrands in the problems and examples have been in “lowest terms,”. In this context that means that the degree of the numerator is strictly less than the degree of the denominator. Look back up at the problems and examples and confirm that this is true.
Conceptually, this is easy to address. We simply divide the denominator into the numerator using long division just as you learned it in gradeschool. To do that you need to the denominator in its unfactored form. In this example we have
\begin{equation*} \frac{x^2}{(x-2)(x-3)}=\frac{x^2}{x^2-5x+6}\text{,} \end{equation*}
so
\begin{equation} \frac{x^2}{(x-2)(x-3)}=\frac{x^2}{x^2-5x+6} = 1+\frac{5x-6}{(x-2)(x-3)}\text{.}\tag{2.42} \end{equation}
