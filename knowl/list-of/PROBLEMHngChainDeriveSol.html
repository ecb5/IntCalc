Problem 19.1.3.2. Deriving the Solution of the Hanging Chain.

Returning to the Example 18.1.0.11 we see that it was originally given in terms of \(y\) where \(y(x)\) represents the vertical position of the Hanging Chain. We had made the substitution \(z=\dfdx{y}{x}\) to make the original equation a little “easier on the eyes.”
So we are interested in finding \(y\) not \(z\text{.}\)
For convenience we take the lowest point to be the origin of our coordinate system so that at the lowest point \((x,y)=(0,0)\text{.}\)
Observe that at the lowest point of the curve the catenary will be horizontal so its slope will \(\eval{\dfdx{y}{x}}{x}{0}=0\text{.}\)
So our problem can be re–cast as the IVP
\begin{align} \dfdx{y}{x},\amp{}=z\amp{}z(0)=0.\tag{19.19} \end{align}
We will address this IVP once we have found \(z(x)\text{.}\)
(a)
Integrate both sides of equation (18.10) (from Example 18.1.0.11) to show that
\begin{equation} \ln\abs{z+ \sqrt{1+z^2}} = \frac{W}{H}x +C\text{.}\tag{19.20} \end{equation}
(b)
Use the initial conditions from equation (19.19) to show that \(C=0\text{.}\)
(c)
Next solve equation (19.20) for \(z(x)\) to show that
\begin{equation*} z=\dfdx{y}{x}=\frac12\left(e^{\frac{w}{H}x}-e^{-\frac{w}{H}x}\right)\text{.} \end{equation*}
This is purely a problem in Algebra. As such it is completely straightforward, but remember that “straightforward” does not mean “easy.” In fact, this is a long, complex, and rather difficult computation. Be careful. Organize your calculations and write them down so you can follow them when you come back to check your work (as you will surely need to do from time to time). We (the authors) recommend that you work with a partner (or a group) so that you can check each other’s work for errors. Above all be patient with yourself.
We have provided several hints below, but do not open them all immediately. You will not learn that way. Take your best shot at getting started. When you get stuck open the first hint and take the problem as far as you can. When you get stuck again open the next hint, and so on. It is OK if you have to open the first hint just to get started as long as you have made a real effort at the problem. But if you give up too soon you won’t learn what you need to from this problem. Don’t rob yourself of your own education.
Hint 1. If You Don’t Know Where To Begin.
The absolute value is a problem here. Try ignoring it at first. Later when you have \(z(x)\) you can come back and verify that your solution still works with the absolute value in place. Or you can adjust it if needed.
Now your problem is to get \(z\) out of the logarithm. How can you do that?
Hint 2. When You’ve Eliminated The Natural Logarithm.
Most likely you know what you have to do now. But it’s really scary and most people will try to convince themselves that there must be an easier way. There isn’t.
You need to get rid of the \(\sqrt{\ }\) symbol and the only way to do that is to square both sides of the equation. It will work if you square both sides of the equation in the form you probably have. It is slightly less work if you move \(z\) to one side by itself first. But only slightly.
Hint 3.
By now you might be very frustrated, and possibly angry with us, because when you squared both sides the \(\sqrt{\ }\) symbol didn’t go away. That’s right. You’ll have to square it again, but first cancel everything you can legitimately cancel, and isolate the term that contains the \(\sqrt{\ }\) symbol on one side of the equation.
Hint 4.
Now solve for \(z^2\text{.}\) What appears on the other side is a perfect square if you did all of the Algebra correctly.
(d)
Solve the differential equation in IVP (19.19) and show that
\begin{equation*} y=\frac{H}{w}\left(\frac{e^{\frac{wx}{H}}+e^{\frac{-wx}{H}}}{2}\right) +C\text{.} \end{equation*}
You used the initial condition given in the IVP in part (b) to show that the integration constant from equation (18.10) was equal to zero. The constant here comes from solving IVP (19.19) so it is not (necessarily) the same.
Hint. Don’t Frighten Yourself
Given how difficult the computations in part (c) were it is only natural to expect the next step to be equally complex. Is it?
(e)
Find an initial condition for the differential equation in IVP (19.19) that will allow you of find the integration constant in part (d).
Hint.
What is the slope of \(y(x)\) at \(x=0\text{?}\)
