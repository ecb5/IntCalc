Example 2.2.2.10.
Suppose we want to decompose the rational function \(\frac{x-2}{x(x^2+1)}\text{.}\) There are no real numbers, \(a\) and \(b\text{,}\) with the property that \(x^2+1=(x-a)(x-b)\) but \(a=i\) and \(b=-i\) (where as before \(i^2=-1\)) will work just fine since
\begin{equation*} (x-i)(x+i)=x^2-i^2=x^2-(-1)=x^2+1\text{.} \end{equation*}
Thus we can use the HCUM to compute
\begin{equation*} \frac{x-2}{x(x^2+1)}=\frac{x-2}{x(x-1)(x+1)} =\frac{-2}{x}+\frac{\frac{i-2}{i(2i)}}{x-i}+\frac{\frac{i+2}{i(-2i)}}{x+i}= \frac{-2}{x}+\frac{\frac{i-2}{-2}}{x-i}+\frac{\frac{-i-2}{-2}}{x+i} =\frac{-2}{x}+\frac{1-\frac12i}{x-i}+\frac{1+\frac12 }{x+1}\text{.} \end{equation*}
But, unfortunately, in this course we confine our attention to the real numbers and treat the complex numbers as a guilty secret, even when using them would make more sense. To get the decomposition back into just the real number we compute the sum \(\frac{1-\frac12i}{x-i}+\frac{1+\frac12 }{x+1}\) as follows
\begin{equation} \frac{1-\frac12i}{x-i}+\frac{1+\frac12 }{x+1}=2x+1\text{.}\tag{2.50} \end{equation}
Thus our decompostion over the real numbers (rather than the complex numbers is
\begin{equation} \frac{x-2}{x(x^2+1)}=\frac{-2}{x}+\frac{2x+1}{x^2+1}\text{.}\tag{2.51} \end{equation}
