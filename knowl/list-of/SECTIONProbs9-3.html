The simple formula for the area of a rectangle (length times width) provides the basis for determining the area of a more general shape. You will probably notice that we had been using this area model in looking at definite integrals already so this should not be too big a jump. However, do not take a short cut and utilize our general strategy. That is divide the region into infinitely thin rectangles where our simple length times width formula applies, and then â€œintegrateâ€ them into the area of the entire region. \begin{equation*} \text{Total area}=\int^2_{x=0}{\left(y_2-y_1\right)\dx{x}} \end{equation*}
This is the set up. Notice that by labeling properly, we could read the area of the rectangle right off the picture. This was the point â€” to divide the problem into easily computable parts. The integral just added them together. To compute this integral, we need to put everything in terms of one variable. This can done by noticing that \(y_2=2x\) and \(y_1=x^2\text{.}\) Thus, we have
\begin{equation*} \text{Total area}=\int^2_{x=0}{\left(y_2-y_1\right)\dx{x}}=\int^2_{x=0}{\left(2x-x^2\right)\dx{x}}={\left.\left(\int{\left(2x-x^2\right)\dx{x}}\right)\right|}^2_{x=0} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} ={\left.\left(x^2-\frac{x^3}{3}\right)\right|}^2_{x=0}=\left(2^2-\frac{2^3}{3}\right)-\left(0^2-\frac{0^3}{3}\right)=\frac{4}{3} \end{equation*}
Of course, there is nothing special about getting everything in terms of \(x\text{.}\) We could divide the region into horizontal rectangles and put everything in terms of \(y\text{.}\) We should obtain the same area. Notice that in the previous set up out two points had the same \(x\) coordinate. In this one, they will have the same \(y\) coordinate.

A cycloid is a curve traced out by a point \(P\) on a circle as the circle rolls along a straight line without slipping.
ğŸ”—
This curve has fascinated mathematicians for a long time. In class, we showed that the coordinates of the point \(P\) are given by
\begin{align*} x=a\left(\theta -\sin(\theta) \right)\amp{}\amp{} y=a\left(1-\cos(\theta)\right) \end{align*}
Galileo actually gave the curve its name in 1599 (though he was not the first to study it) and attempted to compute the area under one arch. He couldnâ€™t accomplish this mathematically, but by weighing pieces of metal, one in the shape of a cycloid and one in the shape of its generating circle, Galileo surmised that the area of the cycloid was approximately three times that of the circle. In 1634, Gilles Personne de Roberval showed that the area under one arch of the cycloid is exactly three times that of its generating circle. This predated the invention of calculus, but he utilized infinitely thin rectangles very much like we have been doing.
