Problem 19.3.0.14. When the Integral of a Product is the Product of the Integrals.

In DIGRESSION: How Not to Integrate a Product we showed that it is not generally true that \(\int f(x)\cdot g(x)\dx{x}=\left(\int f(x)\dx{x}\right)\cdot \left(\int g(x) \dx{x}\right)\text{.}\) But in certain special cases it is. In this problem we characterize all of the functions where the integral of a product is the product of the integrals

(a)

Suppose \(f^\prime(x)\neq{}f(x)\) Show that if
\begin{equation*} \left(f\cdot g\right)^\prime(x) = f^\prime(x)\cdot g^\prime(x) \end{equation*}
then
\begin{equation} g(x)=\exp\left({\int{\frac{1}{1-\frac{f(x)}{f^\prime(x)}}}\dx{x}{}}\right)\tag{19.68} \end{equation}
Hint.
Don’t let formula (19.68) frighten you. Recall that \(\exp(x)=e^x\text{.}\)

(b)

For each \(f(x)\) given, find the corresponding \(g(x)\) from part (a) and confirm that \(\left(f\cdot g\right)^\prime(x) = f^\prime(x)\cdot g^\prime(x) \text{.}\) Assume that \(\alpha{}\) is an arbitrary constant.
  1. \(f(x)=e^{\alpha x}\text{,}\) \(\alpha{}\neq1\text{.}\)
  2. \(\displaystyle f(x)=x^\alpha\)
  3. \(\displaystyle f(x) = x+\alpha{}\)
  4. \(\displaystyle f(x)=xe^x\)
