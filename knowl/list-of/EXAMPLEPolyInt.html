Example 2.1.2.1. The Integral of a Polynomial.

To demonstrate this consider the integral
\begin{equation*} \int 4x^3+\frac12 x^2 - 17 x - 12 \dx{x}\text{.} \end{equation*}
This is not difficult to compute. In fact you may already see the answer but we’ll take it very slowly in order to establish the procedure. First notice that each of the terms \(4x^3\text{,}\) \(\frac12 x^2\text{,}\) \(- 17 x\text{,}\) and \(- 12\) is a function on its own. Thus by entry #2 of Table 1.1.0.4 we have
\begin{equation*} \int 4x^3\dx{x}+\int \frac12 x^2 \dx{x}- \int 17 x \dx{x} -\int 12 \dx{x}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Of these, only the last on has an entry in out table. By entry #3 we have
\begin{equation*} \int 4x^3\dx{x}+\int \frac12 x^2 \dx{x}- \int 17 x \dx{x} -12x\text{.} \end{equation*}
Next we notice that the expression \(x^3\dx{x}\) is a differential. We don’t know what it is the differential of yet, but the presence of the differnential \(\dx{x}\) guarantees that \(x^3\dx{x}\) is the differential of something. So the first integral above has the form
\begin{equation*} \int 4x^3\dx{x} = \int 4\dx{y} \end{equation*}
where \(\dx{y} = x^3\dx{x}\text{.}\) Therefore by entry #3 in our table we see that
\begin{equation*} \int 4x^3\dx{x} = 4\int x^3\dx{x}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Each of the remaining integrals has the same general form so we see that
\begin{equation*} \int 4x^3+\frac12 x^2 - 17 x - 12 \dx{x} = 4\int x^3\dx{x} +\frac12\int x^2\dx{x} - 17 \int x\dx{x} - 12x\text{,} \end{equation*}
and each of these fits the pattern of entry #4 (from the Product Rule) so finally we have
\begin{align*} \int 4x^3+\frac12 x^2 - 17 x - 12 \dx{x} \amp{}= \frac{4}{4}x^4 +\frac{\frac12}{3}x^3 - \frac{17}{2} x^2 - 12x\\ \amp{}= x^4 +\frac16x^3 - \frac{17}{2} x^2 - 12x + C. \end{align*}
Did you forget the arbitrary constant? It’s easy to do. Be careful.
Very soon you will be able to integrate a polynomial as quickly and easily as you can differentiate a polynomial. It isn’t nearly as hard as we’ve made it look in this example.
We were careful to put in every step just so you could see that each step can justified. This is not magic. The rules are the same whether the integral is easy or hard. If you get stuck on an integral take one small, completely clear step at a time.
