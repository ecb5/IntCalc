Problem 19.1.4.9.

We have seen that
  • the presence of \(1+x^2\) suggests the substitution \(x=\tan(x) \text{,}\)
  • the presence of \(1-x^2\) suggests the substitution \(x=\sin(x) \text{,}\)
  • and the presence of \(x^2-1\) suggests the substitution \(x=\sec(x) \text{.}\)
Use those substitutions to show that the antiderivative given are correct. You may need to you may need to rearrange the integrand algebraically or make other substitutions first.
(a)
\(\displaystyle \int \frac{8}{1+4x^2}\dx{x}=4\inverse\tan (2x)+ C\)
(b)
\(\displaystyle \int \frac{6}{\sqrt{1-9t^2}}\dx{t}=2\inverse\sin(3t)+C \)
(c)
\(\displaystyle \int \frac{1}{x\sqrt{x^2-1}} \dx{x} = \inverse\sec \left( 2x \right)+C\)
(d)
\(\displaystyle \int \frac{2}{x(x^2-1)}\dx{x} = 2\ln\abs{\frac{x-1}{\sqrt{x^2-1}}}+C \)
(e)
\(\displaystyle \int \frac{1}{16+x^2}\dx{x}= \frac14\inverse\tan\left(\frac{x}{4}\right)+C\)
(f)
\(\int\frac{6}{\sqrt{16-9x^2}}\dx{x} = 2\inverse\sin \left( \frac{3x}{4} \right)+C\)
(g)
\(\displaystyle \int \frac{x}{3+x^4}\dx{x}= \frac{1}{\sqrt{12}}\inverse\tan \left( \frac{x^2}{\sqrt{12}}+C \right) \)
(h)
\(\displaystyle \int \frac{e^{2t}}{\sqrt{5-e^{4t}}}\dx{t} = \frac12 \inverse \sin \left( \frac{e^{2t}}{\sqrt{5}} \right)+C\)
(i)
\(\displaystyle \int \frac{1}{\sqrt{x}(1-x)}\dx{x}= 2\inverse\tan \left( \sqrt{x} \right)+C\)
(j)
\(\displaystyle \int \frac{1}{\sqrt{x}(x-1)}\dx{x}=2\ln\abs{\sqrt{x}-1}+C\)
