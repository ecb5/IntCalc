Example 19.2.3.3.
Suppose we try the HCUM on \(\frac{1}{x^2\left(x-1\right)}\text{.}\) If we do this without thinking, we will get
ðŸ”—
\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{x^2\left(x-1\right)}=-\frac{1}{x^2}+\frac{1}{x-1} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
But this is incorrect as you can see.
ðŸ”—
\begin{equation*} -\frac{1}{x^2}+\frac{1}{x-1}=\frac{-x+1+x^2}{x^2\left(x-1\right)} \end{equation*}
ðŸ”—
Can you see what the problem is? According to our Theorem, all we know is that the degrees of the numerators in our decomposition must be less than the degrees of the denominators. This means our decompositions form should be
\begin{equation} \frac{1}{x^2(x-1)}=\frac{\left(Ax+B\right)}{x^2}+\frac{C}{x-1}=\frac{A}{x}+\frac{B}{x^2}+\frac{C}{x-1}\tag{19.47} \end{equation}
ðŸ”—
We can still use the HCUM to compute \(B=-1,\ C=1.\)
