Problem 2.2.2.9.

Based on our experience so far if we wanted to decompose the expression \(\frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)(x-c)}\) we’d try to find \(A\text{,}\) \(B\text{,}\) and \(C\) in
\begin{equation} \frac{1}{(x-a)(x-b)(x-c)}=\frac{A}{x-a}+\frac{B}{x-b}+\frac{C}{x-c}\text{.}\tag{2.44} \end{equation}
(a)
Multiply both sides of equation (2.44) by \((x-a)(x-b)(x-c)\) and show that
\begin{equation*} A= \frac{1}{(a-b)(a-c)}\text{.} \end{equation*}
(b)
By a similar argument, show that \(B=\frac{1}{(b-a)(b-c)}\) and \(C=\frac{1}{(c-a)(c-b)}\text{.}\)
This problem shows that the HCUM also works when there are three linear factors in the denominator. It is straightforward to generalize this result to an arbitrary number of linear factors.
