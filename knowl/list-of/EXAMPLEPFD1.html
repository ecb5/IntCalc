Example 2.2.3.4.

Recall we had
\begin{equation*} \int{\frac{dy}{y\left(100-y\right)(y-10)}} \end{equation*}
To compute this we will find the partial fractions decomposition of \(\frac{1}{y\left(100-y\right)\left(y-10\right)}\text{.}\) We make the educated guess
\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{y\left(100-y\right)\left(y-10\right)}=\frac{A}{y}+\frac{B}{100-y}+\frac{C}{y-10} \end{equation*}
and determine what \(A,B,C\) are. This can be done in a number of ways, but the most direct (and labor intensive) way to do this is to combine the terms in the right-hand side and compare coefficients.
\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{y\left(100-y\right)\left(y-10\right)}=\frac{A\left(100-y\right)\left(y-10\right)+By\left(y-10\right)+Cy\left(100-y\right)}{y\left(100-y\right)\left(y-10\right)} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} =\frac{A\left(-y^2+110y-100\right)+B\left(y^2-10y\right)+C\left(100y-y^2\right)}{y\left(100-y\right)\left(y-10\right)} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} =\frac{\left(-A+B-C\right)y^2+\left(110A-10B+100C\right)y+\left(-1000A\right)}{y\left(100-y\right)\left(y-10\right)} \end{equation*}
Since the fractions must be equal and the denominators are the same, the numerators must also be equal. Thus
\begin{align*} -A+B-C& =0\\ 110A-10B+100C& =0\\ -1000A& =1 \end{align*}
Solve these equations anyway you wish, but you should get \(A=-\frac{1}{1000}\text{,}\) \(B=\frac{1}{9000}\text{,}\) and \(C=\frac{1}{900}\text{.}\) Thus
\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{y\left(100-y\right)\left(y-10\right)}=\frac{-1/1000}{y}+\frac{1/9000}{100-y}+\frac{1/900}{y-10} \end{equation*}
so
\begin{equation*} \int{\frac{dy}{y\left(100-y\right)\left(y-10\right)}}=-\frac{1}{1000}\int{\frac{dy}{y}}+\frac{1}{9000}\int{\frac{dy}{100-y}}+\frac{1}{900}\int{\frac{dy}{y-10}} \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} =-\frac{1}{1000}{\mathrm{ln} y\ }-\frac{1}{9000}{\mathrm{ln} \left(100-y\right)\ }+\frac{1}{900}{\mathrm{ln} \left(y-10\right)\ }+c \end{equation*}
