As was mentioned before, this result was known to both Newton and Leibniz and was first publish in a short paper by Leibniz in 1693. The idea is so surprisingly simple and profound, that it warrants an (apocryphal) story. When Leibniz was a young diplomat in Paris, he sought Christian Huygens who, at the time, was one of the most brilliant mathematicians in the world. Leibniz credited much of his mathematicalgrowth in Paris to Huygens. The idea for summing differences came to Leibniz from a problem given to him by Huygens in 1672, namely to compute the following sum
\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{1\cdot2}+\frac{1}{2\cdot3{}}+\frac{1}{3\cdot4}\cdots \end{equation*}
Leibniz recognized that this could be rewritten as
\begin{equation*} \left(\frac11=\frac12\right)+\left(\frac12-\frac13\right)+\left(\frac13-\frac14\right)+\cdots \end{equation*}
and noted that nearly all of the fractions cancelled out leaving \(1-\frac{1}{\infty{}}=1-0=1\text{.}\)
