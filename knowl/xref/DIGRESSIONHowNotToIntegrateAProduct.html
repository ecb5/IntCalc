DIGRESSION: How Not to Integrate a Product.

In the beginning a common error is to assume that that we need only integrate the two factors separately. If we do that with formula (2.62) we’ll get
\begin{equation*} \frac{x^2}{2}\sin(x) +C\text{.} \end{equation*}
This is not correct as we can easily verify by differentiation. Moreover, we know that the differential of a product is not the product of the differentials, so on second thought that may seem like it was a silly idea. It was not. It is the most natural assumption we can make. But assumptions can be treacherous and need to be examined closely. In this case the assumption comes up short as we have just seen.
So we need a new idea.

Problem 2.3.0.2. When the Derivative of a Product is the Product of the Derivatives.

In DIGRESSION: How Not to Integrate a Product we showed that it is not generally true that
\begin{equation} \int f(x)\cdot g(x)\dx{x}=\left(\int f(x)\dx{x}\right)\cdot \left(\int g(x) \dx{x}\right)\text{.}\tag{2.63} \end{equation}
But in certain special cases it is. In this problem we characterize all of the functions where the integral of a product is the product of the integrals
(a)
Suppose \(f^\prime(x)\neq{}f(x)\) Show that if
\begin{equation*} \left(f\cdot g\right)^\prime(x) = f^\prime(x)\cdot g^\prime(x) \end{equation*}
then
\begin{equation} g(x)=\exp\left({\int{\frac{1}{1-\frac{f(x)}{f^\prime(x)}}}\dx{x}{}}\right)\tag{2.64} \end{equation}
Hint.
Don’t let formula (2.64) frighten you. Recall that \(\exp(x)=e^x\text{.}\)

Show that each pair of functions satisfies equation (2.63). Assume that \(\alpha{}\) is an arbitrary constant.
  1. \(f(x)=e^{\alpha x}\text{,}\) \(g(x)=e^{\frac{\alpha }{\alpha -1}x}\) \(\alpha{}\neq1\text{.}\)
  2. \(f(x)=\left(\frac{\alpha}{\alpha -x}\right)^\alpha\text{,}\) \(g(x)= x^\alpha\)
  3. \(f(x) = x+\alpha \text{,}\) \(g(x)=\frac{1}{(1-\alpha )-x}\)
  4. \(f(x)=xe^x\text{,}\) \(g(x)=e^{\frac{x^2}{2}+x} \)
  5. \(f(x)=e^{\frac{x}{2}}\sqrt{\frac{\sec(x) }{1-\tan(x) }}\text{,}\)\(g(x)=\sin(x) \)
