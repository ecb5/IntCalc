Example 18.3.0.1. A Very Simple Differential Equation.

The simplest differential equations to solve are those like equation (18.5), where the derivative of a function, say velocity as a function of time, or \(v(t)\text{,}\) is given entirely in terms of of its variable, \(t\) in this case. In general such equations can be written in the form
\begin{equation} \dfdx{v}{t} = f(t)\tag{18.6} \end{equation}
where \(f(t)\) is some known function. Equation (18.5) is particularly simple since \(f(t)=g\text{,}\) is a constant function.
We solve this differential equation as follows. First multiply both sides by \(\dx{t}\text{,}\) giving
\begin{align} \dfdx{v}{t}\amp{}= g\notag\\ \dx{v}\amp{}=g\dx{t}\text{.}\tag{18.7} \end{align}
Equation (18.7) says that the differential \(\dx{v} \) is equal to the differential \(g\dx{t} \text{.}\) Adding all of the differentials on both sides of equation (18.7) we have
\begin{equation*} \int \dx{v}=\int g\dx{t} \end{equation*}
and from this we see that
\begin{equation*} y=\int g\dx{t}{}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Thus to solve equation (18.5) we need only find an antiderivative of \(g\text{.}\)
Since the only force acting on the body is gravity, we see that the total force, \(F=m\dfdxn{y}{t}{2} \) is equal to \(g\text{,}\) or
\begin{equation*} F=m\dfdxn{y}{t}{2} = g\text{,} \end{equation*}
but since we have assumed that \(m=1\) this reduces to equation (18.5).
From this model we can recover functions which tell us the velocity and the position of a falling body at any time and under any starting conditions (see Section 5.4). We will have more to say about this particular model in Section 18.5.
