Example 2.1.0.1. Falling Down: A Very Simple Differential Equation.

The simplest differential equations to solve are those like equation (2.1), where the derivative of a function, say velocity as a function of time, or \(v(t)\text{,}\) is given entirely in terms of of its variable, \(t\) in this case. In general such equations can be written in the form
\begin{equation} \dfdx{v}{t} = f(t)\tag{2.2} \end{equation}
where \(f(t)\) is some known function. Equation (2.1) is particularly simple since \(f(t)=g\text{,}\) is a constant function.
We solve this differential equation as follows. First multiply both sides by \(\dx{t}\text{,}\) giving
\begin{align} \dfdx{v}{t}\amp{}= g\notag\\ \dx{v}\amp{}=g\dx{t}\text{.}\tag{2.3} \end{align}
Equation (2.3) says that the differential \(\dx{v} \) is equal to the differential \(g\dx{t} \text{.}\) Adding all of the differentials on both sides of equation (2.3) we have
\begin{equation*} \int \dx{v}=\int g\dx{t} \end{equation*}
and from this we see that
\begin{equation*} v(t)=\int g\dx{t}{}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Thus to solve equation (2.1) we need only find an antiderivative of \(g\text{.}\)
From (2.1) we can recover functions which tell us the velocity and the position of a body with a mass of one kilogram (falling freely near the surface of the earth and disregarding air resistance) at any time and under any starting conditions (see [cross-reference to target(s) "SECTIONGalileoDrops" missing or not unique]).
