Example 19.3.0.1. Population Dynamics: A Non–Separable Differential Equation.

Recall that in Example 18.1.0.9 we solved the differential equation \(\dfdx{y}{t}=Ky \) by separating the variables and then integrating. This is the equation we used to model the exponential growth of an algal bloom which we first saw in Section 8.6. For reasons that will become clear shortly we re–write will this equation as
\begin{equation} \dfdx{y}{t}-Ky =0\text{.}\tag{19.55} \end{equation}
Equation (19.55) assumes that the relative growth rate, \(K\) in this case, is constant. But that is almost never true. All biological processes including reproduction tend to slow down when the ambient temperature is cooler and to speed up when it is warmer.
Suppose for example, that we have a population of algae growing in a pond. At night when the temperature is cooler the rate of growth will slow and during the daytime it will speed up. For simplicity of demonstration we’ll suppose that the “speeding up and slowing down” takes the form of a cosine wave, \(\cos(t) \text{.}\) This means that the rate of change of our algae population will be \(\dfdx{y}{t}=Ky+\cos (t)\) because the rate of growth of our population is still proportional to the size of the population. To be consistent with equation (19.55) we’ll re–write this as
\begin{equation*} \dfdx{y}{t}-Ky=\cos (t)\text{.} \end{equation*}
To complete our model we’ll assume that initally there are \(20\) kilograms of algae in the pond. Thus to find the population as a function of time we need to solve the IVP
\begin{align} \dfdx{y}{t} - Ky \amp{}=\cos(t),\amp{} y(0)=20.\tag{19.56} \end{align}
The differential equation part of IVP (19.56) is not separable but it is very similar to
\begin{equation} \dfdx{y}{t}-Ky=0 \tag{19.57} \end{equation}
whose solution
\begin{equation} y(t)=Ae^{Kt}\tag{19.58} \end{equation}
is known. Since the equations are similar it seems reasonable to suppose that their solutions will also be similar so the question is, “How do we tweak solution (19.58)) so as to obtain a solution of IVP (19.56)?”
Observe that \(y(t)=Ae^{Kt}\) is the product of the constant \(A\) and the function \(e^{Kt}\text{,}\) One possible tweak is to suppose that the solution of IVP (19.56) is also a product. But since this is a slightly more complex problem than equation (21.16) we need a slightly more complex product. So let’s guess that
\begin{equation*} \bar{y}(t)=A(t)\cdot e^{Kt} \end{equation*}
where \(A(t)\) is a differentiable function rather than a constant.

Comment.

We have called this a guess because ultimately, that’s all it really is. As such it is simply a trick. But we have also said that a “technique” is a “trick” that is used more than once.
The way we found \(A(t)\) is the technique called “Variation of Parameters” and it works like this. The solution of equation (19.55) contains the parameter \(A\) which is a constant. To solve IVP (19.56) we “vary” the parameter \(A\) by pretending that it is a function rather than a constant. Then we proceed to find out what function \(A(t)\) has to be.
Having made our guess the only thing to do now is try it in IVP (19.56) and see if it works.
That is we suppose that \(\bar{y}(t) = A(t)\cdot e^{Kt}\) is a solution of IVP (19.56) and try to find out what \(A(t)\) must be. From the Product Rule we see that
\begin{equation*} \dfdx{\bar{y}}{t}=A(t)Ke^{Kt}+\dfdx{A}{t}e^{Kt}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Putting this into our differential equation in IVP (19.56) we have
\begin{align*} \underbrace{\textcolor{red}{AKe^{Kt}}+\dfdx{A}{t}e^{Kt}}_{=\dfdx{\bar{y}}{t}}-\underbrace{\textcolor{red}{KA(t)e^{Kt}}}_{=K\bar{y}}\amp{}=\cos(t)\\ \dfdx{A}{t}e^{Kt}\amp{}=\cos{t}. \end{align*}
So apparently all we have left to do is compute the integral
\begin{equation} A(t) = \int e^{-Kt}\cos (t)\dx{t}\tag{19.59} \end{equation}
to find \(A(t)\) and the solution of (19.56) will be
\begin{equation*} \bar{y}=A(t)e^{Kt}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Unfortunately this is well beyond our ability at the moment so we will back up a bit and look at some slightly simpler integrals first.
