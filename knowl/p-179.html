Saving a sine or cosine would lead to an integrand with a square root, which we are trying to avoid, if we can help it. In this case, there are a couple of trigonometric identities that can help. They the Half-Angle formlas:
\begin{equation*} \sin^2(\theta) = \frac12\left(1-\cos(2\theta)\right) \text{ and } \cos^2(\theta)=\frac12\left(1+\cos(2\theta)\right) \text{.} \end{equation*}
Using these identities in our integral we see that
\begin{align*} \int\sin^2(\theta)\cos^2(\theta)\dx{\theta}\amp=\frac14\int(1-\cos(2\theta))(1+\cos(2\theta))\dx{\theta}\\ \amp=\frac14\int (1-\cos^2(2\theta))\dx{\theta}\\ \amp=\frac{1}{4}\int\sin^2(2\theta)\dx{\theta} \end{align*}
