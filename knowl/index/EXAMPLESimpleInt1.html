Example 17.1.2.2. A Simple Integration.

For example, can you find a formula for this integral: \(\int2t\dx{t}\text{?}\) Before reading on give it a try.
Clearly \(f(t)=2t\) so from Definition 17.1.2.1 we see that \(F(t) = t^2\) is one antiderivative, and we can capture all of the antiderivatives by adding an arbitrary constant. Thus
\begin{equation*} \int 2t\dx{t} = t^2 + C\text{.} \end{equation*}
This shows that the expression \(\int f(t)\dx{t} \) represents a multifunction much like the “\(\text{arc}\)” functions we encountered in Chapter 5. However, none of the complicated domain and range difficulties that caused so many headaches in Section 5.4 will be in play here. Since the multifunctions arising from integration are relatively simple, we will dispense with the formal language and call
\begin{equation*} \int 2t\dx{t} = t^2+C \end{equation*}
the most general antiderivative of \(f(t) = 2t\text{.}\) There will be times when we will want to choose only one of the functions represented by \(\int f(t)\dx{t}\text{.}\) This will consist entirely of choosing the value of \(C\) that works for the problem in front of us.
