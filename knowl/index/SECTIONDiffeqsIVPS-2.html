Differential Calculus gives us an extraordinarily powerful set of tools for modeling real world phenomena. We saw some simple examples of this when we studied differentiation. For example, suppose we have a body with mass equal to one kilogram falling freely under the force of the earth’s gravitational pull. we know that the acceleration, \(a\) of the body is the derivative of its velocity, \(v\text{,}\) so: \(a=\dfdx{v}{t}\text{.}\) Moreover we saw in [cross-reference to target(s) "SECTIONGalileoDrops" missing or not unique] that Galileo found that a body falling freely near the surface of the earth would accelerate downward at a constant rate we’ve called \(g\text{.}\) This is fully described by the differential equation
\begin{equation} \dfdx{v}{t}=g,\tag{1.4} \end{equation}
which is a simple model of the motion of a falling body.
