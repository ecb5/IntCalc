Problem 2.133.

Consider the quarter circle given by \(y=\sqrt{1-x^2}\text{,}\) where \(-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \le x\le \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\text{.}\) Since the circumference of the unit circle is \(2\pi \text{,}\) the length of this quarter circle should be \(\frac{\pi }{2}\text{.}\)
(a)
Use an integral to verify 5  this result.
(b)
Suppose we tried to compute the length of a quarter of the circle in the following way
\begin{equation*} \int_{x=\left( -\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}},\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right) }^{\left( \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}},\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right) }\sqrt{\left(\dfdx{x}{y} \right)^2 +1}\dx{y}=\int_{x=\left( -\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}},\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right) }^{\left( \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}},\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right) }\sqrt{1+\left(\dfdx{y}{x} \right)^2 }\dx{x} \end{equation*}
We’d have gotten an arclength of zero. What goes wrong?
