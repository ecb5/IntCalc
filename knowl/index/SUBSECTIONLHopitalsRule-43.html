If we replace \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) in Problem 12.4.1.12 with their tangent lines at \(x=a\) we have
\begin{align} \limit{x}{a}{\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}} \amp = \limit{x}{a}{\frac{f(a)+f^\prime(a)(x-a)}{g(a)+g^\prime(a)(x-a)}}\notag\\ \amp = \limit{x}{a}{\frac{0+f^\prime(a)(x-a)}{0+g^\prime(a)(x-a)}}\notag\\ \limit{x}{a}{\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}}\amp = \frac{f^\prime(a)}{g^\prime(a)}\tag{12.5} \end{align}
(We need to assume that \(g^\prime(a)\neq0\text{.}\) Do you see why?) This is consistent with all of our examples above so it appears that the Principle of Local Linearity 5.2.0.5 has led us in the right direction. All of the evidence we’ve gathered so far suggests rather strongly that when we are faced with the indeterminate form \(\frac{(\rightarrow0)}{(\rightarrow0)}\) simply replacing the numerator and denominator with their tangent lines will work.
