Example 8.10.1.3.

Suppose we have \(6\) pounds of a very unstable isotope which is known to lose half of its mass to radioactive decay in \(2\) hours. If we let \(y(t)\) be the mass of our isotope sample at any time \(t\text{,}\) then the IVP we need to solve is
\begin{align*} \dfdx{y}{t}=ry, \amp{}\amp{} y(0)=6. \end{align*}

Aside: Comment.
In view of our discussion above about positive versus negative rates of change it is tempting to write the differential equation as \(\dfdx{y}{t}=-ry\text{.}\) While this is not necessarily wrong, it is not helpful. The variable \(r\) can be any number, positive or negative. If we do our analysis correctly the correct value will emerge whether we write \(r\) or \(-r\text{.}\)

As always the solution of a differential equation of this form is
\begin{equation*} y(t) =6e^{rt} \end{equation*}
where and \(r\) is an unknown constant. To find \(r\) we must use the additional information given. Half of our isotopeâ€™s mass is lost in \(2\) hours, so \(y(2)=3\text{.}\) Thus
\begin{align*} y(2)=3\amp = 6e^{r(2)}\\ \frac12\amp = e^{r(2)}\\ \ln\left(\frac12\right)\amp = \ln\left({e^{2r}}\right)\\ \amp = 2r \end{align*}
or
\begin{equation*} r =\frac12\ln\left(\frac12\right). \end{equation*}
Thus
\begin{equation*} y(t) = 6e^{\frac{t}{2}\ln\left(\frac12\right)}. \end{equation*}
This is correct but again it is cumbersome. Also the lack of an obvious negative in the exponent is a little troubling.
