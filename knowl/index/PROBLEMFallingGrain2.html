Problem 19.1.0.4. Terminal Velocity.

(a)

Let \(z=Rv-Mg\) and use this to show that
\begin{equation} \int \frac{1}{Rv-Mg}\dx{v}=\frac1R\int \frac1z\dx{z}.\tag{19.4} \end{equation}
Remember that \(Mg\) is a constant (why?).

(b)

Use the substitution \(u=\frac{1}{Rv-Mg}\) and the appropriate Integration Rule from Section 17.2 to show that
\begin{equation} \int \frac{1}{Rv-Mg}\dx{v}=\frac1R \ln \left( Rv-Mg\right) + C\text{.}\tag{19.5} \end{equation}

(c)

Combine equations (19.3)–(19.5), to show that
\begin{equation} v(t)=\frac{Mg}{R}+\left(v_0-\frac{Mg}{R}\right)e^{-\frac{R}{m}t}\text{.}\tag{19.6} \end{equation}
Hint.
This problem is both harder and simpler than it might appear to be.
It is harder because it is not just a matter of combining equations (19.3)–(19.5). After that is done, you will still have to solve for \(y(t)\text{.}\)
It is simpler because we’ve developed equation (19.6) in full generality. That is, we have not assigned numerical values to the parameters (constants). As a result it looks more complicated than it really is. Recall Example 1.2.0.1.

(d)

Compute \(\limit{t}{\infty}{v}\text{,}\) and explain why this value is called the terminal velocity of the sand grain settling in the water. Notice that the terminal velocity is independent of \(v_0\text{.}\) Does this make sense physically? Explain.
