Definition 21.1.2.4. Definite Integral.

Given a function \(f(x)\text{,}\) the formula
\begin{equation} \int_{x=a}^{b} f(x)\dx{x}\tag{21.7} \end{equation}
represents the sum of all of the differentials of the form \(f(x)\dx{x} \) beginning at \(x=a\) and ending at \(x=b\text{.}\) Such an expression is called a definite integral.
The integral can be evaluated by computing
\begin{equation} \eval{\int f(x)\dx{x}}{x}{a}^b = \eval{\int f(x)\dx{x}}{x}{a}-\eval{\int f(x)\dx{x}}{x}{b} \text{.}\tag{21.8} \end{equation}
Often we will have a formula, say \(F(x)\text{,}\) for one of the antiderivatives of \(f(x)\) in the multifunction \(\int f(x)\dx{x}\text{.}\) In that case equation (21.8) reduces to
\begin{equation*} \eval{\int f(x)\dx{x}}{x}{a}^b = F(b)-F(a) \end{equation*}
