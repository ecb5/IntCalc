In Figure 6.2.5.3 the horizontal axis is time so it shows the same bouncing spring at three different times. Assume that our spring is anchored in some way at the top. When the weight of the object and the restoring force of the spring balance exactly we say that they are in equilibrium as illustrated by the leftmost spring in Figure 6.2.5.3 . Robert Hooke 9  (1635–1703), who we encountered back in Section 5.10, was a leading elder scientist when Newton was a young, rising star in English science. Hooke was the first to show that the restoring force exerted by a spring (the force back toward equilibrium) is directly proportional to the length of its extension beyond equilibrium. This is known today as Hooke’s Law for Springs (or just Hooke’s Law) and it is true whether the spring is stretched or compressed.
