Second, notice that like a substitution, the integration by parts technique does not compute the integral. Rather, it replaces that integral with, hopefully, an easier integral. This means that as long as the integration by parts technique is applied correctly, it is not wrong; it just might not be helpful. For example, we could have let \(u=\cos(x){}\) and \(\dx{u}=x\dx{x}\text{.}\) This would give \(v=x^2/2\text{.}\) Substituting these into equation (1.6) we get
\begin{equation*} \int x\cos(x)\dx{x}=\frac{x^2}{2}\cos(x)+\frac{1}{2}\int x^2\sin(x)\dx{x}. \end{equation*}
Everything we’ve done is correct, but we have an integral that is worse than before. Practice is required to us this technique as well. Lots of practice.
