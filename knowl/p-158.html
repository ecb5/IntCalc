If we make the substitution \(x=\sin(\theta)\text{,}\) then \(\dx{x}=\cos(\theta)\) as before so,
\begin{equation*} \int\frac{\dx{x}}{(1-x^2)^\frac32} = \int\frac{\cos(\theta)}{\left(\sqrt{1-\sin^2(\theta)}\right)^3}\dx{\theta}=\int\frac{\cos(\theta)}{\cos^3(\theta)}\dx{\theta}=\int\sec^2(\theta)\dx{\theta}=\tan(\theta)+c\text{.} \end{equation*}
